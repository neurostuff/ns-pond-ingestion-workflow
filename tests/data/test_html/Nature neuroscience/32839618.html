Abstract

Time perception and prediction errors are essential for everyday life. We hypothesized that their putative shared circuitry in the striatum might enable these two functions to interact. We show that positive and negative prediction errors bias time perception by increasing and decreasing perceived time, respectively. Imaging and behavioral modeling identify this interaction to occur in the putamen. Depending on context, this interaction may have beneficial or adverse effects.

Main

Time perception in the sub-second range is essential for many animal behaviors1. Subjective perception is affected by motivational and emotional states, which usually increase perceived duration2,3. Learning is driven by efficient processing of reinforcement signals and mainly by prediction error (PE):4,5 outcomes can be better than expected, a positive prediction error (PE+), or worse than expected, a negative prediction error (PE). Although classically independent processes, recent studies suggest that time perception and PE might be related6,7. Time perception engages striatal regions and their dopaminergic inputs8,9, and neural correlates of PEs have been found in the same circuits. Moreover, PE+ and PE have been associated with increased and decreased activation of dopaminergic neurons, respectively4,10, and perceived duration could be increased or decreased by activation or deactivation of dopaminergic neurons, respectively11. In addition, time perception is compromised in Parkinsons disease and other basal ganglia and dopamine-related disorders12,13. We therefore hypothesized that signed PEs would differentially affect the perceived duration of a stimulus, and that such bias would be associated with differential striatal activity.

Participants determined which of two sequentially presented images was of longer duration in a two-alternative-forced-choice paradigm (2AFC; Fig. 1a). There were two types of trials: (1) short-long (SL), in which the duration of the first image was shorter, and (2) long-short (LS). The difference in time duration between the two images (t) varied across trials. Each image was overlaid by a number indicating a monetary gain or loss.

Assuming a reference-dependent model of value where participants learn to predict the relative outcome14,15, the value of the first image serves as a reference point for predicting the value of the second image. Therefore, a PE would be PE+ if the difference between the values of the images was larger than expected and PE if the difference was smaller than expected. A PE0 occurs when the difference was as expected. Importantly, a trials outcome was determined only by the numbers presented on the images and was completely independent of the time-discrimination performance (correct/incorrect). A first study established the behavioral bias, and a consecutive functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) study replicated the behavior and elucidated the neural correlates (n=18/35).

Because discrimination is easier for larger values of t, we indeed found a significant main effect of t (Fig. 1b; no significant interaction between PE type and t, or three-way interaction). In line with our hypothesis, we found a significant interaction between PE type and trial type (SL/LS; Fig. 1c). This result was robust for each group separately (behavior-only and fMRI) and also when considering the different PE magnitudes (Extended Data Figs. 13).

What drives this interaction between PE type and trial type? In our design, the PE occurred only when the second stimulus appeared. Therefore, in SL trials when the value induces a PE+/PE, if the image is perceived as longer/shorter, it would lead to a perceived larger/smaller t between the two stimuli, resulting in easier/harder discrimination and hence better/worse performance. For LS trials, PE+/PE would induce the opposite change in perceived t, resulting in an opposite effect on performance.

Accordingly, we observed better performance in SL trials for PE+ compared to PE and to PE0, and trials with PE showed worse performance compared to PE0. The opposite pattern occurred in LS trials; performance was worse in PE+ compared to PE trials and to PE0 trials, and PE showed better performance compared to PE0 (Fig. 1c). Together, these results demonstrate that PE+ and PE induce an increase and decrease in the perceived stimulus duration, respectively.

A second consecutive stimulus may bias the perceived duration of the first stimulus, termed time-order error (TOE). Here, this would induce more errors in SL trials because the first image would be perceived as longer (Fig. 1c; Extended Data Fig. 4a and 5). To account for this, we quantified the individual TOE and normalized performance accordingly (Fig. 1c,d and Extended data Fig. 5). As expected, the main effect of trial type was no longer significant, nor was the interaction between trial type and t, whereas all other results, and importantly, the significant interaction between PE type and trial type, remained significant (Fig. 1c,d).

We further accounted for individual differences by normalizing the objective t by each participants just-noticeable difference (JND). All findings were replicated using the individually normalized psychometric curves (Fig. 1e and Extended data Figs. 1e, 2e and 3e). There was no overall change in perceptual thresholds (Extended Data Fig. 6b), indicating that the bias was due to the instantaneous PE imposed in a trial.

We performed several control experiments to confirm that the bias was due to PE, rather than to valence or the outcome of the second image. First, we found that if the difference between the first and second images is predictable (even if it holds value), time perception is not altered (Extended Data Fig. 6b and Supplementary Note). Second, the results were replicated in a similar experiment but when all values were positive, that is, where PE+/PE included only gains. Finally, the results were replicated when the value in the second image was fixed, and PE+, PE0 or PE were respectively induced by different values on the first image (Extended Data Fig. 7). Therefore, time perception is biased by the processing of signed PEs, and not by the valence (gains/losses) or the magnitude.

To provide trial-by-trial and individual information, we adapted a reinforcement-learning approach that considered three main factors affecting the perceived duration: objective time difference (t), bias due to PE () and TOE (). According to the main findings, PE decreased the perceived duration of the second image, which decreased and increased the perceived difference in SL and LS trials, respectively; and an opposite bias occurred in PE+ trials (Fig. 1f). The expected value (EV) was the expected difference between the value in the second and the first images throughout the experiment and was updated on a trial-by-trial basis (Extended Data Fig. 4b). The probability of making a correct discrimination was then modeled by a logistic function and fitted individually for each participant. This model successfully captured individual behavior (Fig. 2a,b and Extended Data Figs. 2f and 3f), performance (Fig. 2c) and TOE (Fig. 2d). The bias magnitude () was similar across PE+ and PE (Fig. 2e). To further validate the model accuracy, which estimates a continuous PE value, we replicated the main result (PE-typetrial-type interaction) with several different thresholds (|PE0|<0.005, 0.01, 0.1, 0.2 and 0.5).

Using the model-derived trial-by-trial PEs as parametric modulators, we identified brain regions previously shown to be involved in PE encoding: the ventral striatum, midbrain and dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (ACC; Fig. 2f, Extended Data Fig. 8 and Supplementary Table 1).

In addition, we used the estimated probability for a discrimination error as reflecting fluctuations of uncertainty in the perceptual task and found a negative correlation (higher activation with low error probability) in Brodmann area 47 and between the nucleus accumbens and ventral ACC (Fig. 2g), previously implicated in perceptual confidence (Supplementary Table 2). These findings strengthen the model validity and show that it integrates PE and time estimations in our task to capture variability in perceptual judgments.

Because both time perception and PE involve striatal activity, we hypothesized that interaction in the striatum could contribute to the PEtime bias. We therefore conducted a whole-brain analysis using two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) with factors PE type and performance (incorrect/correct), designed to identify regions that underlie the effect of PE leading to differences in performance, namely the PE time perception bias.

We found a significant interaction between PE type and performance in the putamen (Fig. 3a). During PE+ trials, activation in the putamen was increased for incorrect compared to correct discriminations, whereas during PE trials, the putamen was deactivated for incorrect compared to correct judgments. This was the case both when including PE0 trials and when omitting them. Moreover, the individual difference in putamen activity between PE+ and PE was correlated across correct and incorrect trials (Fig. 3b). A significant interaction was also found in the dorsal ACC (Fig. 3c and Supplementary Tables 3 and 4), and when considered separately, an individual relationship was observed between PE+ and PE0 and PE0 and PE (Fig. 3d).

Finally, to further establish a link between regional activations and the behavioral PEtime bias, we quantified the interaction in putamen activity and correlated it with the behavioral bias at an individual level (Fig. 3e,f). These results suggest a direct link between interaction of activity in the putamen for PE and time duration and the behavioral bias that PE induces on time perception.

Our findings provide evidence that PEs bias time perception, and suggest that interaction between these two fundamental functions is driven by interaction in striatal activations. We found a bidirectional effect, in which a positive PE resulted in overestimation of duration, and negative PE resulted in underestimation of duration. These findings call for revisiting the notion that arousal alone, during either negative16 or positive stimuli17, dictates longer perceived duration, and that predictability may induce shorter perceived duration18. The bidirectional bias that accompanied signed PE cannot be accounted for by absolute (attention-like) signals. A more integrative mechanism that combines differential patterns of attention due to valence, saliency19, information20 and unpredictability18, might account for our findings.

Because both of these processes are essential for daily tasks, the effect of such biases on learning and memory formation that rely on computations of prediction errors on one hand and on estimating durations on the other could be of major importance. Therefore, the overlap in striatal activity that underlies the behavioral bias can either be an evolutionary benefit or an unfortunate by-product, for example, by influencing temporal-difference learning6,7. Abnormal interactions in striatal circuits underlying time duration and PE-driven learning can therefore underlie and contribute to psychopathologies.

Methods

Experimental design

Participants

Eighteen healthy individuals (5 males) participated in the behavior-only group and 35 healthy right-handed individuals (15 males) participated in the imaging group (fMRI). Additionally, 19 participants (8 males) participated in a gain-only control group. Participants ages varied between 2240 years. In the imaging group, the mean age was 26 years and median age was 25 years. No statistical methods were used to predetermine sample sizes, but our sample sizes are similar to those reported in previous publications. All studies were approved by the Helsinki committee of the Sourasky Medical Center, under protocol number 028709-TLV (Ministry of health protocol no. HT5271) and further approved by the Institutional Review Board (IRB) of the Weizmann Institute. All participants had normal or corrected-to-normal vision and reported no attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD). Informed consent was obtained from all participants before the experiment. Participants were compensated for their time and according to the accumulation of gains and losses in the experiment (but no less than the minimum payment as determined in the protocol). Compensation was independent of the performance in the time-discrimination task. All investigators were blinded to any group, participant or sequence allocation during data collection and analyses. One participant in the imaging group was unable to complete the fMRI scan due to unexpected physical influences inside the magnetic field, and another participant could not complete the scan due to extensive movements; both participants were excluded.

Visual stimuli

White images with black numbers displayed in the center of the image were presented on a gray background. The images were identical in size, presented in the center of a 21-inch screen with refresh rate of 60Hz (lag time shorter than 1ms) and spanning a visual angle of approximately 5.13.8, with a red fixation cross displayed before the image appeared. In all tests, stimulus presentation was implemented by MATLAB (R2014b, MathWorks) using the Psychophysics Toolbox21,22.

Time-duration discrimination paradigm

Two images were presented sequentially with a 0.5-s delay between them. After the presentation of the second image, participants had to determine which image had been presented for a longer duration (Fig. 1a). One image was always displayed for a duration of 500ms, the reference duration, whereas the other image was presented for 500ms and an additional duration (t). Because the JND in time-duration discrimination tasks have been reported to range between 1520% of the reference duration23,24, we set t in the present experiment to range from 0ms (equal presentation time for both images) to 133ms, corresponding to a t of 026.6% of the 500-ms reference duration.

To generate PEs, numbers representing monetary gains and/or losses were overlaid on each image. The first image was always presented with the number zero, while the number of the second image could be negative (PE), zero (generating no PE; PE0) or positive (PE+). To create a baseline expectation of the difference between values overlaid on the images (second imagefirst image), a large proportion of trials were PE0 trials (60% in the behavior-only group and 80% in the imaging group), and an equally smaller proportion were PE+ and PE trials. For the behavior-only group, PEs were generated by presenting numbers ranging from 5 to +5, in steps of 0.5. For the imaging group, to avoid loss-aversion effects25 in brain activity, we used twice the magnitude of PE+ compared to PE26, and PEs were generated by presenting either 2 (PE), 0 (PE0) or +4 (PE+). There was a uniform distribution of presentations across PE values (that is, all values were presented in a similar number of trials, with the exception of PE0 trials). Moreover, the number of trials for each t was equal across PE values, with a counterbalanced and fully random order of presentation. Randomization of trials was used for all participants. For the imaging group, we used OPTSEQ2 software to determine the sequence of trials and intertrial intervals before the experiment, and every participant was randomly assigned to one of the generated sequences. The accumulation of monetary gains and losses corresponded to an actual monetary compensation provided at the end of the experiment. Importantly, the monetary compensation was independent of the performance on the duration discrimination task.

Gain-only group

The paradigm for this group is largely similar to the main paradigm described above, with the following modifications. First, values on the images were positive only (+1,+2 and +4), meaning that participants could only gain money in each trial. In addition, while we fixed the value in the first image for the other groups, here we allowed different values also in the first image. Specifically, in SL trials the first image was always overlaid with the number +2, while the number of the second image could be either +1 (generating PE), +2 (generating PE0) or +4 (generating PE+). In LS trials, in contrast, the second image was always overlaid with the number +2, while the number of the first image could be either +1 (generating PE+), +2 (generating PE0) or +4 (generating PE). Finally, as in the main group, 80% of the trials were PE0 trials, with an equally smaller proportion being PE+ and PE trials, and the t was varied in the same range and distribution, with one additional value of 83ms, corresponding to 16.6% of the reference duration (500ms).

Just-noticeable-difference paradigm

JND estimates for each participant represent the minimal difference in time duration between two stimuli that can still be detected with high probability. Two psychophysical methods were used to estimate the JND in the present study. First, we used a one-up-two-down staircase procedure27. Specifically, in each trial, two images were presented sequentially (one for 500ms and the other for 500ms+t). If participants correctly discriminated which image was presented for the longer duration, the order of presentation was reversed, and if participants again made a correct discrimination, t was decreased by an adaptive amount of time. Whenever an incorrect discrimination was made, t was increased by the same amount (up to a maximum of 250ms). Initial t was determined on 125ms, with a 10% decrease of the step size after every trial (regardless of participants discrimination accuracy). This process repeats itself until a stopping criterion of correct discrimination after six cumulative errors have been reached (number of trials was not limited), at which point a threshold has been determined which yields an EV of 0.707 probability of making a correct discrimination. Of note, while numbers were presented on the images at all times, monetary gains and losses were associated with the numbers only at the test conducted at the end of the experiment (General procedure). The second procedure used the method-of-constant-stimuli (MCS), in which a fixed set of predetermined t values (identical to main paradigm) were used. Otherwise, the presentation of images was similar to before. The resulting data were then used to generate a psychometric curve for each participant, by fitting a generalized linear regression of the responses to a binomial distribution, and the threshold was defined as the t during which participants had a 0.707 probability of making a correct discrimination.

Constant-loss (no surprise) condition

Following the main task, all participants in the imaging group performed 40 additional trials using the same paradigm as in the main task, except the second image value was always 2, while the first image value was always 0. This condition was designed to generate a constant loss condition, where the trials are identical to PE trials except for the predicted nature of outcomes. We therefore hypothesized that performance in constant-loss trials would resemble performance during main-task PE0 trials but would be different than performance during main-task PE trials, even though the images and outcomes are identical.

General procedure

Behavior-only group

Following general instructions, a JND time-duration discrimination threshold was first estimated for each participant. A JND estimate using the staircase procedure was conducted with a zero number overlaid on both images, and two additional JNDs were estimated using two MCS procedures of 40 trials each. In one of the MCS procedures, both the first and the second image had the number zero, while in the other MCS procedure the first image was presented with the number zero, and a random number was presented on the second image. This allowed us to control for threshold, visual confounds and value (Extended Data Fig. 6a). Next, participants performed the time-duration discrimination paradigm.

Imaging group

The procedure of the imaging group is largely similar to that of the behavior-only group, with the following modification: JNDs were estimated outside the MRI scanner, before and after the main paradigm and for all images and pairs. In the second estimation of JND (but not the first), after the scan, participants gained and lost money based on the numbers presented on the image, thus allowing us to control for reward-value confounds. The time-duration discrimination task and the constant-loss task were performed while undergoing fMRI scanning. A minimum of three training trials were provided inside the scanner to allow participants to become accustomed to the scanner.

Gain-only group

The procedure for the gain-only group was similar to the procedures described above: following instructions, a JND threshold was estimated using a staircase method followed by a time-duration discrimination paradigm. At the end of the experiment, the JND was re-evaluated.

TOE measures from behavior

A well-established perceptual phenomenon is TOE, which predicts that the duration of the first stimulus in a sequence of stimuli with equal durations is perceived as longer compared with succeeding stimuli durations28,29.

TOE can be defined as the difference in probability of successful discrimination as a function of trial type (that is, SL or LS):

$$\mathrm {TOE} = \frac{{p\left( {R_{10}{\mathrm{|}}S_{10}} \right) - p\left( {R_{01}{\mathrm{|}}S_{01}} \right)}}{2}$$
(1)

Where R10 represents a response that the first stimulus is longer, and S10 represents an LS trial (that is, the first stimulus is longer). R01 and S10 represent the opposite response (that is, the second stimulus is longer) given an SL trial.

To compute the above we extracted individual performance during PE0 trials separately for every t, and used the resulting TOEs to correct the probability of discrimination error in all PE-type trials (Fig. 1c,d and Extended Data Figs. 1b,d, 2b,d and 3b,d):

$$\begin{array}{l}\mathrm {corrected}\_p\left( {dt} \right)_{SL} = \mathrm {observed}\_p\left( {dt} \right)_{SL} - \mathrm {TOE}\left( {dt} \right)\\ \mathrm {corrected}\_p\left( {dt} \right)_{LS} = \mathrm {observed}\_p\left( {dt} \right)_{LS} + \mathrm {TOE}\left( {dt} \right)\end{array}$$
(2)

Consequently, positive and negative TOE in time-duration discrimination occurs if the first stimulus is perceived as having a longer and shorter duration, respectively. In our experiment, we found positive TOE across all t values (Extended Data Fig. 4a), thus offering an explanation for why the probability of making a mistake when t=0 (that is, the duration of both images is identical) was different from chance level and opposite between LS and SL trials (Extended Data Figs. 1c, 2c and 3c). Specifically, when the duration of the first image was longer (LS trials), TOE causes an even longer perceived duration, generating easier trials (larger perceived t). By contrast, when the duration of the first image is shorter (SL trials), TOE causes a shorter perceived duration, leading to more difficult trials (smaller perceived t).

To compute unbiased behavioral TOE (and compare it to the model-derived estimate), we took the intersection between performance in PE0 trials and 0.5 as the proportion of discrimination errors at t=0.

Computational modeling

Performance (the probability of making an incorrect discrimination) was modeled as a logistic function:24,30,31

$$p\left( {\Delta t} \right) = \frac{1}{{1 + e^{ - f\left( {\Delta t} \right)}}}$$
(3)

We assumed that performance depends linearly on t, that is, larger t leads to better performance:

$$f\left( {{\mathrm{\Delta }}t} \right) = b_1\left( {{\mathrm{\Delta }}t} \right) + b_2$$
(4)

Next, we addressed other parameters that could influence the perceived t.

First, we included the TOE. We found positive TOE across all t values (Extended Data Fig. 4a), which predicts that the duration of the first stimulus is perceived as of longer duration. Here, TOE was modeled by the parameter .

Second, the bias in time duration discrimination due to PE, here denoted by the parameter , is caused by the mismatch between the difference in outcomes presented in first and the second image (secondfirst) and the expected outcome difference. Namely, the value of the prediction error on trial i was computed as the difference between the actual presented values and current estimation of EV:

$$\mathrm {PE}_i = (R_i - \mathrm {EV}_{i - 1})$$
(5)

where

$$R_i = R_{i, \mathrm {second}\,\mathrm {image} - }R_{i, \mathrm {first}\,\mathrm {image}}$$
(6)

EV was initialized to 0 and updated on every trial using the learning-rate parameter :

$$\mathrm {EV}_i = \mathrm {EV}_{i - 1} + \alpha \times \left( {R_i - \mathrm {EV}_{i - 1}} \right)$$
(7)

Our results indicate that PE+ and PE cause the duration of a stimulus to be perceived as longer and shorter, respectively. Accordingly, PE+ decreases performance in LS trials (causing the perceived duration of the second stimulus to be more similar to that of the first stimulus) and vice versa in SL trials. Incorporating and into the model gives the following expression:

$$f\left( {dt} \right) = b_1\left( {{\mathrm{\Delta }}t_i + k_1\left( {\varepsilon + \theta \times \mathrm {PE}_i} \right)} \right) + b_2$$
(8)

where

$$k_1 = \left\{ {\begin{array}{*{20}{c}} { - 1,} & {\mathrm{SL}}\;{\mathrm{trials}} \\ {1,} & {\mathrm{LS}}\;{\mathrm{trials}} \end{array}} \right.$$

Finally, one additional parameter, , was added to the model to account for a ceiling effect observed in LS trials (in these trials performance is initially much closer to the perceptual threshold (JND) due to TOE thus might be bounded). The final model looks as follows:

$$f\left( {dt} \right) = (b_1 - \gamma ) \times \left( {\Delta t_i + k_1\left( {\varepsilon + \theta \times \mathrm {PE}_i} \right)} \right) + b_2$$
(9)

where

$$\gamma = \left\{ {\begin{array}{*{20}{c}} {\gamma ,} & {\mathrm{SL}}\;{\mathrm{trials}} \\ {0,} & {\mathrm{LS}}\;{\mathrm{trials}} \end{array}} \right.$$

The model was then plugged into the logistic probability function and estimated separately for every participant.

Model parameters were estimated by computing the maximum a posteriori probability using the MATLAB function fmincon (MathWorks). We assumed uniform prior on the parameters and used bounds as follows:

$$b1 \in [ - 20,20]$$
$$b2 \in [ - 20,20]$$
$$\gamma \in [ - 10,10]$$
$$\theta \in [ - 2,2]$$
$$\varepsilon \in [ - 2,2]$$
$$\alpha \in [0,1]$$

To make sure that results were not biased due to outliers, we repeated the estimation process with a Gaussian distribution over each parameter with a mean 0 and variance X, where X is equal to the bound described above for every parameter (for example, was modeled as N(0,2)). Finally, we also computed unconstrained maximum likelihood of the parameters with no priors and no bounds (except from the learning rate that remained bounded) using the MATLAB function fminunc. All results are highly similar with respect to model fit and parameter values.

fMRI data acquisition

Images were acquired on a 3T Siemens MAGNETOM Tim-Trio scanner. Functional T2-weighted images were acquired using a gradient-echo EPI sequence (repetition time (TR)=2,000ms, echo time (TE)=30ms, flip angle=75; 32 slices with 10% gap scanned in a descending order with phase encoding direction anteroposterior at 30 toward coronal from anterior commissureposterior commissure plane32; slice thickness=3mm, voxel size=333mm3 and field of view (FOV)=216) in five separate scanning sessions (up to 2min between sessions). Anatomical T1-weighted images were acquired after the functional scans (TR=2,300ms, TE=2.98ms, flip angle=9, voxel size=111mm3 and FOV=256). The anatomical scan covered the whole brain, while the functional scan covered the whole brain excluding a small area in the dorsal part of the parietal lobe. To improve signal-to-noise ratio of the event-related design, the order of trials and intertrial interval in the scanner were determined using OPTSEQ2 (ref. 33).

All imaging data were preprocessed and analyzed using Brain Voyager QX 3.4 (Brain Innovation34) and MATLAB R2014a (MathWorks) with BVQX/Neuroelf toolbox v1.0 (Jochen Weber; https://neuroelf.net/). Preprocessing included slice scan-time correction, motion correction and high-pass filtering. Images were then co-registered and normalized into Talairach space35 and spatially smoothed with an isotopic 6-mm FWHM (full width at half maximum) Gaussian kernel.

Statistical analysis

Data analysis

Performance on the time-duration discrimination task was estimated as the proportion of incorrect discriminations for each combination of PE type (PE+, PE and PE0), trial type (SL/LS) and t. These results were then analyzed using repeated-measures ANOVAs with these factors and performance as the dependent variable. Post hoc tests were performed using the TukeyKramer test, effect sizes were estimated by Cohens d or 2, and null results were estimated by Bayes factor. Data distribution was assumed to be normal, but this was not formally tested. Trials in which no response was made were discarded from the analysis.

Normalization to JND

The objective t was normalized by each participants staircase JND measured at the beginning of the experiment. Normalization for every individual was completed as follows: t values (ms) were transformed to individual JND units, interpolated at a range of 01.5 JND and then fit to a logit function. Four participants with no valid JND measure were pre-excluded from this analysis.

fMRI data analysis

Analyses consisted of random-effects ANOVA based on general linear models, with event regressors defined from the onset of the first image until the offset of the second image, modeled as box-car functions and convolved with a canonical hemodynamic response function. All models included six regressors to account for head movements and a regressor to account for the motor response, modeled from the onset of the cue until the participants response.

Multiple-comparison correction on cluster size was performed using a non-parametric permutation test36,37. Null distribution of maximal cluster size was built separately using 1,000 iterations for every analysis (PEcorrectness interaction; all trials with probability weights as parametric modulators). On every iteration, all labels of all trials were randomly shuffled for every participant, and a general linear model for every voxel was computed using the same definitions as in main analysis. Finally, an ANOVA model was created and maximum cluster size was extracted (direct or diagonal proximity in one dimension was sufficient to include voxels in same cluster) using the MATLAB function bwlabeln. The cluster-defining threshold was set at P<0.005.

Analysis using trial-to-trial PE estimates as parametric modulations consisted of separate contrasts for PE types and was corrected for multiple comparisons using a false discovery rate of q<0.05. Three participants were pre-excluded from the trial-by-trial fMRI analysis because their modeled learning rate was zero, generating a regressor of zeros, which could not be included in this analysis.

Computational model selection

Our computational models were built to track the trial-to-trial probability of discrimination error, corresponding to the behavioral measure used in analyses.

We tested five variations of the model:

Model 1 the selected model (six parameters; b1, b1, , , and ).

Model 2 no free parameter b2 (five parameters).

Model 3 no (five parameters).

Model 4 no b2, no (four parameters).

Model 5 separate parameters for the bias due to PE+ (+) and PE () (seven parameters).

For each model, Akaikes information criterion (AIC) was estimated using the computed likelihood of the model for each participant regulated by the number of parameters. AICs were used as model evidence in a Bayesian model selection for group studies38, in which the exceedance probabilities of all models (that is, the probability that each model is more frequent in the population than other models) is estimated. Results indicated a 0.98 probability for model 1 to be the model best explaining the evidence and, therefore, it was the selected model.

Assigning PE type to trials according to the computational model

To generate different trial types according to the modeled PE, trials were categorized based on the sign and the magnitude of the estimated PE. Since almost no trials precisely estimated a PE=0, and to assign enough trials for all PE types to generate a valid statistic, we selected a threshold for which a trial with absolute value of PE smaller than that threshold would be assigned as a PE0 trial, and a PE larger or smaller would be assigned as a PE+ and PE trial, respectively:

$$\left| {{\mathrm{PE}}_i} \right| < {\mathrm{threshold}}:{\mathrm{trial}}\;{{i}}\;{\mathrm{is}}\;{\mathrm{PE}}0$$
$${\mathrm{PE}}_i \le - {\mathrm{threshold}}:{\mathrm{trial}}\;{{i}}\;{\mathrm{is}}\;{\mathrm{PE}} -$$
$${\mathrm{PE}}_i \ge {\mathrm{threshold}}:{\mathrm{trial}}\;{{i}}\;{\mathrm{is}}\;{\mathrm{PE}} +$$

A threshold of 0.1 was chosen since it produced a similar proportion of PE0 trials as in the actual design. To make sure that this selection did not bias the results, we also tested thresholds of 0.2, 0.5, 0.01 and 0.005. All tests resulted in the same main effects and interactions except the three-way interaction, which wasnt significant for low (below 0.05) thresholds. This indicates a robust PE-type statistic across different levels of classification.

Correlation between individual interaction activations and behavioral bias

We calculated an interaction score for each participants putamen activity and correlated it with the individual behavioral bias () derived from the model:

$$\textrm {Interaction\_activity\_score} = \left( {\beta \left( \textrm {{PE+ ,correct}} \right) - \beta \left( \textrm {{PE- ,correct}} \right)} \right) - \left( {\beta \left(\textrm { {PE+ ,incorr}} \right) - \beta \left( \textrm {{PE- ,incorr}} \right)} \right)$$
(10)

where (PE+, correct) is the average regional activity in trials of PE+ with correct response and so forth. The interaction_activity_score provides an approximate individual quantification of the interaction found in the imaging analysis (PE_TYPEperformance). To establish robustness of this measure we performed a bootstrap analysis by resampling a different set of participants every time and recalculating the correlation between the putamen interaction factor and the behavioral bias for each subset of participants. The distribution of the correlation coefficients and their respective P values were estimated using Fishers exact test.
