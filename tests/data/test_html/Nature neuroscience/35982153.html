Abstract

The organization of the basic tissue and functional properties of the cerebellum across development is unknown. Combining several large datasets, we demonstrate in the human cerebellum a static tissue gradient in adults that mirrors a similar growth-rate gradient across development. Quantitative tissue metrics corroborate unique densities of certain lipids and proteins among lobules, and cerebellar structural development closely follows cerebellar functional properties through childhood.

Main

While a fundamental objective of neuroscience is to understand the mechanisms of human brain function and its development, the lack of in vivo research on the cerebellum in humans leaves a striking gap in current working models of the brain and cognition. The human cerebellum, classically associated with error-correction signals in motor behavior, has been shown recently to play a large role in cognitive behaviors more broadly1,2. Given its potentially formative role in developmental deficits such as autism3,4,5, a working model of the development of typical microstructural and functional properties of the cerebellum is paramount for understanding such developmental disorders. The sheet of cerebral neocortex demonstrates hierarchical anatomical gradients of myelination6, thickness7 and prolonged growth of tissue microstructures across development8. Whether or not such hallmarks describe the cerebellum and its development, and whether they play a role in its function, has yet to be studied. Answering these open questions requires large, multimodal datasets across human development, which we combine here with quantitative magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and mass spectroscopy (MS) to demonstrate that the cerebellum shows striking structural-functional and developmental gradients, proving to be not as structurally invariant as once believed9.

In neocortex, cortical myelination forms a sensory-association gradient, with high myelin content in primary sensory regions and decreasing content into higher-level association cortex. Is this a universal principle of all neural tissue, also present in the cerebellum? Employing neuroanatomical images (n = 1,018, ages 22–37 years) from the adult Human Connectome Project10 (HCP), we algorithmically identified the lobular parcellation11,12 of each individual’s cerebellum (Fig. 1a) from T1-weighted (T1w) images to quantify how tissue microstructure may vary as a function of lobular position in the cerebellum. Traversing the cerebellum from anterior to posterior, there is believed to be a sensory-to-association functional gradient13. To test the hypothesis that tissue properties will vary across cerebellar lobules from anterior to posterior, we derived contrast maps in each individual thought to be sensitive to the local myelin content14 by dividing each individual’s T1w image by an aligned T2-weighted (T2w) image (Fig. 1b). Shown in Fig. 1c, the T1w/T2w ratio forms a striking S-shaped gradient with large ratios (that is, higher myelin content) in anterior cerebellar lobules that decrease to a nadir in Crus II, then rise as one proceeds counter-clockwise towards lobule IX, and drops again at lobule X (Fig. 1c). This anterior–posterior gradient is readily visible on a three-dimensional (3D) rendition of the cerebellum (Fig. 1d) and is largely consistent across different sagittal planes (Extended Data Fig. 1).

In cortex, it is a general theory that highly myelinated structures such as primary sensory regions emerge quickly and show the least structural development through childhood, while association cortex shows prolonged development15,16. Is this a universal phenomenon of neural tissue development, also visible in the cerebellum? Given the observations that the cerebellum may show a sensory-cognitive gradient in adults13, we hypothesized that the observed tissue gradient reflects this underlying functional map and would thus show differential development along its trajectory. Employing the developmental HCP dataset (HCP-D, n = 519, ages 9–19 years) in a similar fashion, median T1w/T2w ratio values were extracted from every individual’s lobules. A clear developmental gradient emerges (Fig. 1e), with lobules corresponding to sensory representations showing flat or negative development trends, while higher-level lobules show proliferation with age. Regression lines fit to each lobule’s development confirm this gradient (Fig. 1g). Interlobular myelin differences appear larger in childhood, with children showing a more pronounced tissue gradient. To measure this, we quantify interlobular heterogeneity through s.d., showing it decreases significantly from childhood to adulthood (Fig. 1f), and additionally show that heterogeneity declines linearly across childhood (Fig. 1h). These findings were replicated with a pediatric-specific cerebellar parcellation (Extended Data Fig. 2). Taking advantage of twin pairs in HCP data, we further found that the tissue gradient is influenced by genetic factors, and that the heritability index of each lobules’ tissue values forms another gradient (Fig. 1i), with posterior lobules showing more heritability than anterior lobules. These gradients are also observed in a functional parcellation of the cerebellum17 (Extended Data Fig. 3), map differentially onto previously observed resting-state gradients in adults18 (Extended Data Fig. 4), and explain the most variance in a data-driven analysis (Extended Data Fig. 5).

Given the relatively qualitative nature of structural images from standard MRI, we sought to replicate, and further validate, the existence of this cerebellar gradient using quantitative metrics and separate datasets. Advances in quantitative MRI (qMRI)19 have made possible the quantification of the volume of macromolecular tissue within a voxel (MTV), as well as its composition (through relaxivity, R1, sensitive to myelin; Fig. 2a) yielding images with units that, like one’s temperature, can be compared meaningfully across people. While T1w/T2w ratio is a contrast map thought to reflect myelin content, R1 and MTV more directly assess the presence of macromolecules like myelin with quantifiable units. In 16 young adults, we image lobules I–III through VI with quantitative MRI to quantify relaxivity; we only quantify these upper lobules as they reside outside a magnetic field inhomogeneity. We have the a priori hypothesis that we should observe decreasing amounts of hydrophobic materials like myelin. In Fig. 2b, we verify this hypothesis, with R1 decreasing significantly from lobules I–III to VI (lower R1, less hydrophobic material such as myelin). To verify the tissue growth suggested by the T1w/T2w ratio data from HCP-D, data from 35 children (ages 5–12 years) and 29 adults (ages 22–26 years) who underwent qMRI were analyzed to see whether posterior lobules show more pronounced tissue growth than anterior lobules. We replicate the observation of differential tissue growth across lobules: adults, relative to children, show significant growth of MTV in Crus I, but not lobules I–IV (Fig. 2c), demonstrating that neural tissue, including but not limited to myelin, grows. Thus, both standard and qMRI metrics corroborate unique tissue environments across lobules that proliferate at different rates. To get an even finer-scale picture of the identity of the molecules driving MRI-based tissue differences across the cerebellum, we performed a proteomic analysis of a human postmortem cerebellum tissue sample using MS. Tissue samples taken from lobules I–IV show significant enrichment of proteins (Supplementary Table 1) compared with samples taken from Crus II, including myelin basic protein, synapse structures, and neurite growth factors (Fig. 2d). Formal enrichment analysis of the proteomic data confirms lobule I–IV proteins involved in dendritic and synaptic structures, which, along with myelin, would be detectable at a bulk level in MRI. However, in Crus II, enriched proteins were involved primarily in DNA and RNA regulation—factors not as readily detectable at the level of MRI. Thus, spectroscopy provides proteomic insight into the MRI-based cerebellar gradient.

If these structural changes are meaningful, then we should also observe concomitant changes in cerebellar function across development. Moreover, we have the strong a priori hypothesis that functional changes should mirror the tissue gradient. Atypical myelination of axons results from atypical development20, and impaired axonal myelination drives abnormal functional properties21. One possibility is that the tissue growth we observed across development contributes to the temporal properties or stability of functional responses within the cerebellum. We thus hypothesized a developmental reduction in low-frequency oscillations within the neural signal, measured with resting-state functional MRI (rs-fMRI). We analyzed data from the same children and adults who had also completed rs-fMRI scans. Rs-fMRI is sensitive to functional correlations between brain regions and can be a stable probe of local functional processing22. The amplitude of low-frequency fluctuations from the power spectrum of the rs-fMRI time-series, when taken as a ratio over the power spectrum (fractional amplitude of low-frequency fluctuations (fALFF); Fig. 3a), is a reliable functional fingerprint robust to physiological noise23, and is thus a useful biomarker of local changes in temporal properties across development. To first verify that fALFF forms a topological gradient similar to T1w/T2w ratio, we derived fALFF in each voxel within each adult’s cerebellum. Extracting the median fALFF from each lobule, we again found an S-shaped gradient (Fig. 3b), strikingly similar in shape (Fig. 3c), but inverted in sign, to the aforementioned T1w/T2w ratio gradient. Developmentally, functional changes of the cerebellum were closely yoked to structural changes: posterior lobules showed more dramatic functional decreases in fALFF (Fig. 3d,e), and differential development rates across cerebellar lobules dampened the heterogeneity of the functional gradient from childhood to adulthood (Fig. 3f). Furthermore, lobular functional development is correlated with changes in T1w/T2w ratio (Fig. 3g) and, within an individual, the shape of the structural (T1w/T2w ratio) and functional (fALFF) gradients is coupled so tightly across development, that the mean correlation coefficient between the two remained stably above 0.5, despite both gradients undergoing changes in amplitude (Fig. 3h).

Overall, we provide multiple sources of evidence for the existence of a multifaceted gradient in the human cerebellum whose features show coordinated development. Consistent with neocortical theories, a static tissue gradient observed with adult T1w/T2w images mirrored a gradient of myelin development that we observed in a separate developmental dataset. In turn, these structural changes may drive a gradient of changes in functional dynamics within rs-fMRI in the same individuals, mirroring a recently observed cortical connectivity pattern in adult fMRI data18. qMRI data of the cerebellum confirmed differences in macromolecular structures between lobules, and combined with mass spectroscopy analyses of postmortem tissue, demonstrated that the cerebellum seems to be more structurally variegated across lobules than previously thought. Similar to neocortex8, the cerebellum shows surprisingly prolonged tissue growth, but unlike cortex, cerebellar lobules involved in cognitive processes were the most heritable in a twin-pairs dataset, perhaps related to the cerebellum’s uniquely accelerated evolution24 in humans and great apes. Although the current work examines development within each lobule, functional representations can span multiple lobules (for example the default network), so future work should examine how asynchronous lobular development might contribute to different aspects of a single behavior or function. These findings substantially advance our understanding of how the human cerebellum develops both structurally and functionally from childhood to adulthood. The existence of this multifaceted gradient provides a useful biomarker for future research to classify atypical development of the cerebellum.

Methods

HCP-YA dataset and preprocessing

Data from the S1200 release of HCP-YA dataset10 were analyzed in the current study. The HCP-YA project recruited 1,206 healthy young adults, in which 1113 participants underwent multimodal MRI protocols. Among all participants, some were twin pairs, including both monozygotic (MZ) and dizygotic (DZ) twins. Structural MRI (T1w and T2w images) and rs-fMRI were acquired using a 3-T Siemens Skyra scanner (Siemens). For the structural MRI session, two scans were acquired for both T1w and T2w images, and only scans with ‘good/excellent’ quality were included from the HCP dataset for further analysis. Functional MRI for each participant included in this study were rs-FMRI. The rs-fMRI data were collected in two sessions, each containing two 15-min runs, during which participants passively fixated on a central fixation cross and think of nothing in particular. Only participants with the complete acquisition of T1w images, T2w images and all rs-fMRI sessions were included for the analyses, leading to a sample of 1,018 young adults ranging in age from 22 to 37 (mean age 28.7 ± 3.7 years, 546 females).

Details of the MRI acquisition were provided by Van Essen et al.10. Importantly, for structural MRI imaging, the T1w and T2w images were all acquired at 0.7 mm isotropic resolution (T1w: repetition time (TR) = 2,400 ms, echo time (TE) = 2.14 ms, inversion time (TI) = 1,000 ms, flip angle (FA) = 8°; T2w: TR = 3,200 ms, TE = 565 ms). For rs-fMRI imaging, the two scans in each of the two rs-fMRI sessions were acquired in opposite phase encoding directions (left-to-right and right-to-left) with the following parameters: TR = 720 ms, TE = 33.1 ms and voxel size = 2 mm3.

The minimally preprocessed structural MRI and rs-fMRI data released by the HCP S1200 were used in the current study. Detailed information of the minimal preprocessing pipelines can be found in Glasser et al. 201325, as well as online: https://www.humanconnectome.org/study/hcp-young-adult/document/1200-subjects-data-release. In brief, volumetric minimal preprocessing for structural MRI data includes gradient distortion correction, readout distortion removal, repetition averaging and nonlinear registration to the MNI standard space. After completion of structural preprocessing, volumetric rs-fMRI data were minimally preprocessed, which comprised gradient distortion correction, head-motion correction, echo planar imaging distortion correction, denoising with ICA-FIX, native space and MNI standard space registration and, lastly, global intensity normalization.

HCP-D dataset and preprocessing

Data from the developmental HCP-D dataset were analyzed in this study26. The HCP-D project collected multimodal MRI brain imaging for each participant on a 3-T Siemens Prisma scanner (Siemens). Data content, acquisition and preprocessing of HCP-D follow similar protocols to the HCP-YA dataset. Data analyzed here are part of the recently released HCP-D v.2.0 release, representing a cross-sectional cohort of 652 neurologically typical children. Identical to the HCP-YA dataset, data of participants who completed all T1w, T2w and rs-fMRI scan acquisitions were retained for analysis. This resulted in a total of 519 participants aged 9–19 years included in developmental analyses; 8-year-old participants were excluded due to the small sample size. The sample size for each age bin, collapsing across both sexes, was no less than 30. Detailed sample information on age and gender is shown in Extended Data Fig. 6.

Detailed information on MRI acquisition and preprocessing was provided elsewhere25,27. The acquisition protocols and minimal preprocessing are substantially similar to those of HCP-YA except for necessary adaptations for hardware and youth populations. Briefly, one T1w and one T2w scan were acquired for each participant at 0.8 mm isotropic resolution (T1w: TR = 2,500 ms, TE = 1.81 ms, TI = 1,000 ms, FA = 8°; T2w: TR = 3,200 ms, TE = 564 ms). For rs-fMRI scans, two rs-fMRI sessions with a pair of scans in opposite phase encoding directions (anterior–posterior and posterior–anterior) were also acquired for each participant with the following parameters: TR = 800 ms, TE = 37 ms, voxel size = 2 mm3. To reduce the total scanning duration for children to maximize the retention rate and data quality, the rs-fMRI scans acquired in HCP-D were 6.5 min, resulting in a total of 26 min of rs-fMRI data.

Cerebellar parcellation

Before all analyses, the cerebellum was first isolated from other brain regions by segmenting its constituent lobules. For the analysis of HCP data, both adult and developmental, we employed a recent deep-learning approach called ACAPULCO12. This segmentation procedure uses a U-Net architecture with locally constrained optimization trained on parcellations hand-segmented from experts, allowing it to segment the cerebellum quickly while respecting an individual’s unique anatomy and overcoming image noise. Because this segmentation approach operates on individual anatomy, we feel it is ideal for answering our developmental questions. It also currently allows for reliably segmenting 11 of the cerebellum’s main lobules: I–III, IV, V, VI, Crus I, Crus II, VIIb, VIIIa, VIIIb, IX and X. See Fig. 1a for an example of each lobule used in the current study. The lobules are colored in Fig. 1a and elsewhere simply to visually differentiate them. Importantly, the ordering of the lobules as written was used in ordering of data presentation to test the hypothesis that a sensory-association gradient, which runs from anterior (lobules I–III as sensory), to posterior (Crus II as association) and back around to sensory in lobule X.

Notably, besides the ACAPULCO model pertaining to with adult data, an independent ACAPULCO model was pretrained with pediatric data in which lobules I–V were grouped into a single label. To verify that the segmentation network was not biased in anyway in one age group versus another, this pediatric ACAPULCO network was used to reanalyze all of the developmental HCP data. We replicated all main findings (Extended Data Fig. 2) with the pediatric network to show that the chosen segmentation procedure did not introduce any developmental confounds.

ACAPULCO cannot be used to generate parcellations from the developmental qMRI data because these images were not collected with a full field-of-view, omitting the lower half of the cerebellum. To define the lobules of the upper half, structural T1w images were submitted to the Spatially-Unbiased Infratentorial Template (SUIT) toolbox28, which employs a nonlinear registration algorithm to align individuals’ cerebella to standard MNI152 volumetric space, from which a parcellation of the cerebellum is derived based on a probabilistic atlas of the cerebellum. The presence of large anatomical landmarks in the upper half of the cerebellum was sufficient to align the probabilistic atlas of the cerebellum to each individual in order derive labels for each lobule. Lobule-wise results were obtained by averaging voxels from the same lobule in both the left and right hemispheres. All analyses in the current study were performed on volumetric data; cerebellar flatmap representations seen in figures were created with the SUIT toolbox29 and were performed only for visualization purposes.

T1w/T2w ratio

Because signal intensity increases with time after proton excitation in T1w images, and signal decays in T2w images, it is thought that taking the ratio of these two volumes would both remove spatial signal inhomogeneities and square the sensitivity of each voxel’s signal to myelin (which is thought to be the main source of voxel intensity in these structural MR images). Thus, following the proposed protocol from Glasser and Van Essen14, the ratio of T1w and T2w images was calculated (Fig. 1b) for each subject to get a measure sensitive to myelin content within each voxel of the cerebellum. Whereas the ACAPULCO labeling of cerebellar lobules is generally very accurate, given that the main fissures of the cerebellum are consistent across individuals, the large number of participants in this dataset precluded the hand-cleaning of any small imperfections in lobule masks. We thus extract the median value from a given lobule when extracting an individual’s T1w/T2w ratio. As a descriptive statistic, the median is robust to outliers, in this case, voxels showing partial volume effects. Data plotted in the violin plots of Fig. 1c were entered into a one-way repeated analysis of variance (ANOVA) (factors of lobule): F(10, 9,790) = 4,503.21, P < 0.001. To verify that a sensory-association myelination gradient can be observed with functionally defined regions of the cerebellum (as opposed to the current anatomically based approach), the analyses were repeated using the Buckner-7-network cerebellar atlas, replicating the finding that sensory regions of the cerebellum show the most myelination, and together show the smallest amount of development (Extended Data Fig. 3). We also used a data-driven, principal component analysis (Extended Data Fig. 5) to verify that our anatomically derived structural gradient explains the largest amount of variance across the developmental HCP cohort. Last, using medial to lateral anatomically defined ROIs within individuals, the gradient shape remains largely the same across these different sagittal planes (Extended Data Fig. 1).

To evaluate the differences in trajectories illustrated in Fig. 1e, data were entered into a two-way ANOVA with factors of age group and lobule. Age groups were binned evenly into two groups: 9–14 years old, and 14–19 years old. The interaction term between age group and lobule (indicative of different developmental slopes) was significant: F(20, 5,469) = 7.08, P < 0.001. The linear regression lines fit to data in Fig. 1e result in slopes which are plotted in Fig. 1g, and the significance (for example different from zero) of these individual regression fits are shown with asterisks in Fig. 1g, with a single asterisk denoting an α less than 0.05 after false discovery rate (FDR) correction.

To investigate whether the amplitude of the T1w/T2w ratio gradient changes with development, we quantified the s.d. of the median T1w/T2w values across an individual’s lobules, which we deem here interlobular heterogeneity. If a gradient is more pronounced in a given individual, they will have higher s.d. across lobules. For each individual, interlobular heterogeneity was quantified and scattered in Fig. 1h against their age in years. We can fit a regression to this data, which allows us to express change in heterogeneity as an annual-percent rate of change: −1.94%. The fit was statistically significant at P < 0.001, R = −0.25.

Heritability analysis

Some participants within the HCP-YA dataset are monozygotic and dizygotic twin pairs, allowing us to ask to what extent variation in the observed T1w/T2w ratio values across individuals might result from all genetic influences, rather than environmental or experiential factors. To that end, 123 pairs of monozygotic twins and 67 pairs of dizygotic twins were extracted from the dataset (using the correct and updated zygosity labels) all of whom completed the full protocol of T1w, T2w and rs-fMRI sessions. Falconer’s heritability index (h2) was calculated for each lobule 30,31 according to the following formula:

$$h^2 = 2\left( {r_{\mathrm{MZ}} - r_{\mathrm{DZ}}} \right)$$
(1)

where rMZ is the intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) of monozygotic twin-pairs and rDZ is the ICC of dizygotic twin-pairs. Most lobules showed moderately heritable tissue values (h2 > 0.3). While such h2 values are thought to reflect a definite genetic role of the phenotype, with posterior lobules Crus I, Crus II and VIIb exceeding values of 0.3, their significance did not survive FDR correction. Nonetheless, it is clear that these posterior lobules show qualitatively higher heritability than anterior lobules (like I–III and X).

qMRI data and processing

To verify that the tissue differences implied from the standard MRI analyses result from tissue structures that can be quantified more directly with qMRI, 16 young adults were scanned at Princeton University using a 3-T Siemens Skyra with a similar qMRI protocol with a larger field-of-view (FOV) that encapsulated the entirety of the cerebellum. The Siemens equivalent of the spoiled gradient echo scan (fast low-angle shot 3D scan, FLASH) was used with a TR of 19 ms, TE of 3.34 ms and flip angles of 4, 10, 20 and 30°. Echo planar inversion recovery scans were also similar to the Stanford protocol (see below) with necessary adjustments for differences in scanner hardware: TR = 2,920 ms, and inversion times of: 2,400, 1,200, 400 and 200 ms. The qMRI scans are designed to yield whole-brain maps with voxel values sensitive to the composition (Relaxivity, R1) and MTV. R1 is the precise rate at which, in Hertz, excited protons relax back to field alignment, and although it is certainly influenced by the neighboring tissue environment, it is thought to be largely sensitive to the presence of myelin. More myelin leads to higher R1 values. Here, we quantify R1 to verify the standard MRI findings that suggest decreasing myelin content from anterior to posterior cerebellar lobules. A similar cerebellar parcellation procedure similar to the HCP data analysis was followed for each participant using the ACAPULCO toolbox. While the lower/posterior lobules of the cerebellum cannot be currently analyzed due to strong inhomogeneity of the magnetic field at the base of the brainstem, the upper/anterior lobules (I–VI), are outside of this bias zone, and these lobules neighbor the ventral temporal cortex where qMRI has been used to reliably quantify tissue development in previous studies (for example, Gomez et al.8). Using the segmentation and extraction pipeline similar to the HCP data above, we sought to test the hypothesis that anterior (lobules I and II) lobules show more tissue content (higher R1 rates) compared with posterior lobules. In Fig. 2b, box plots demonstrate a clear gradient, with decreasing R1 rates from anterior to posterior lobules as hypothesized. A repeated-measures ANOVA was performed, showing a significant effect of lobule: F(3,45) = 29.18, P < 0.001.

To use qMRI to verify that tissue is indeed growing from childhood to adulthood, we can take advantage of an existing qMRI dataset. As part of a recent study on brain development at Stanford University8, 35 children between the ages of 5 and 12 years old, and 29 adults between the ages of 22–26 years old, completed a qMRI protocol. The study was originally designed with the goal of minimizing scan time in a pediatric setting for the occipital and temporal lobes, and thus did not contain a full FOV. Nonetheless, in every participant, the FOV included the entire cerebral cortex as well as the upper half of the cerebellum. While it is thus not optimized for quantifying interlobule differences, it is sufficient to quantify whether tissue is growing from childhood to adulthood. To that end, qMRI estimates proton density in each voxel, which allows for the derivation of the precise fractional volume of each voxel that belongs to macromolecules (for example, not water), defined above as MTV. To verify tissue growth, here we quantify changes in MTV from childhood to adulthood. Data were acquired on a 3-T GE Discovery scanner with a phase-array 32-channel head coil. Scan protocols were similar to those established in Mezer et al.19. R1 rates were measured using four spoiled gradient echo images with flip angles of 4, 10, 20 and 30 degrees (TR = 14 ms, TE = 2.4 ms), with a scan resolution of 1 mm isotropic, with 130 axial slices, prescribed to follow in parallel the tilt of the temporal lobe. Four spin-echo inversion recovery scans with an echo planar imaging readout were collected: TR = 3,000 ms, inversion times = 2,400, 1,200, 400, 50 ms, TE was set to minimum full, with 2× acceleration. The qMRI data were analyzed using the mrQ v.2 software package (https://github.com/mezera/mrQ), which results in brain-wide maps of MTV. Each qMRI subject was aligned to the standard MNI volumetric space and processed through the SUIT pipeline. Because the pipeline uses a volumetric approach, it can maximize its fit to the portion of the cerebellum and cortex within the FOV, and we were thus able to derive the lobule masks for the cerebellum in each person. Lobules beyond Crus I were outside the qMRI FOV in some participants. As described below, we use a thresholding approach so that even if there is partial coverage of a given lobule within an individual’s data, voxels near and outside the imaging view will be excluded. To further ensure that only voxels being included in the analysis belonged to cerebellar tissue and were as free from partial volume effects as possible (for example, CSF, bone, and so on), a thresholding approach was used to maximize the inclusion of voxels with biologically sensible values. For MTV, voxels with values greater than 0.4 and less than 0.05 were excluded. After this thresholding, surviving voxels were averaged within a given lobule in each participant. If there is differential tissue growth in anterior versus posterior lobules, then the anterior-most lobule (I–IV) should show little-to-no tissue growth whereas the posterior-most lobule from which we can reliably measure (Crus I) should show more pronounced development. Figure 2c shows violin plots quantifying MTV with data normalized to children in lobules I–IV and lobule Crus I to visualize the quantity of tissue growth from childhood to adulthood. Crus I shows significant growth in MTV, while lobules I–IV show little-to-no change.

Mass spectroscopy proteomics analysis of the cerebellum

To validate that the tissue environment between anterior and posterior cerebellar lobules are indeed distinct at the protein level, as suggested by standard and qMRI metrics, we performed an analysis on postmortem tissue samples from a human cerebellum (3.5-year-old female, right hemisphere). While the tissue sample was from a young donor, it remains an ideal sample to test our hypothesis. That is, from standard MRI-based metrics, we find higher tissue density in lobules I–IV compared with Crus II (Fig. 1c), and this difference is even more striking in children compared with adults. So, in this postmortem sample, there should be large dynamic range between anterior and posterior lobules to detect statistical differences. N = 5 tissue samples were taken from an anterior region spanning lobules I–IV and N = 5 tissue samples were taken from a posterior region spanning Crus II. Crus II was chosen as it was one of the most distinct regions from lobules I–IV (Fig. 1c) in terms of T1w/T2w ratio and easily accessed during tissue extraction given its posterior position. Cerebellar fissures were used to localize tissue extraction locations. The primary fissure that separates lobules V and VI was identified. From this tissue, samples were selected anterior to lobule V spanning a section roughly centered on lobule III extending slightly anterior towards lobule I and II and slightly posterior towards lobule IV. For the other tissue samples, the horizontal fissure was identified, which separates Crus I and Crus II. Samples were taken inferior to the horizontal fissure to sample Crus II. For both the anterior and posterior extractions, tissue samples were extracted via scalpel, cut roughly perpendicular to folia striations visible on the cerebellar surface, and cut a few millimeters deep down to the white matter so that each sample included gray and white matter. Samples were about 20 mg each, and sampled in roughly the same sagittal plane of the cerebellum.

All ten tissue samples were processed through an identical tissue processing pipeline designed for shotgun proteomics of formalin-fixed tissue, described below. Tissue processing was completed by the Proteomics and Mass Spectrometry Core of the Department of Molecular Biology at Princeton University. Formalin-fixed cerebellum samples were dissolved in 5% SDS and 50 mM TEAB buffer. Trypsin digestion was performed using S-Trap microspin column digestion protocol. Trypsin-digested samples were dried completely in a SpeedVac and resuspended with 20 μl of 0.1% formic acid pH 3 in water; 2 μl (~360 ng) was injected per run using an Easy-nLC 1200 UPLC system. Samples were loaded directly onto a 45 cm long 75 μm inner diameter nanocapillary column packed with 1.9 μm C18-AQ resin (Dr. Maisch) mated to a metal emitter in-line with an Orbitrap Fusion Lumos (Thermo Scientific). Column temperature was set at 45 °C and 2-h gradient method with 300 nl min–1 flow was used. The mass spectrometer was operated in data-dependent mode with a 120,000 resolution MS1 scan (positive mode, profile data type, AGC gain of 4 × 105, maximum injection time of 54 s and mass range of 375–1,500m/z) in the Orbitrap followed by HCD fragmentation in the ion trap with 35% collision energy. A dynamic exclusion list was invoked to exclude previously sequenced peptides for 60 s and maximum cycle time of 3 s was used. Peptides were isolated for fragmentation using quadrupole (1.2m/z isolation window). The ion trap was operated in rapid mode.

Raw files were searched using Sequest HT algorithms32 within the Proteome Discoverer v.2.5.0 suite (Thermo Scientific); 10 ppm MS1 and 0.4 Da MS2 mass tolerances were specified. Carbamidomethylation of cysteine was used as fixed modification, oxidation of methionine, deamidation of asparagine and glutamine were specified as dynamic modifications. Pyroglutamate conversion from glutamic acid and glutamine are set as dynamic modifications at peptide N-terminus. Acetylation was specified as dynamic modification at protein N-terminus. Trypsin digestion with maximum of two missed cleavages were allowed. Files were searched against UP000005640 Human database downloaded from Uniprot.org.

Scaffold (v.5.0.1, Proteome Software Inc.) was used to validate tandem mass spectrometry (MS/MS)-based peptide and protein identifications. Peptide identifications were accepted if they could be established at greater than 95.0% probability by the Scaffold Local FDR algorithm. Protein identifications were accepted if they could be established at greater than 99.9% probability and contained at least two identified peptides. Protein probabilities were assigned by the Protein Prophet algorithm33. Scaffold data represent normalized protein quantities, relative to the amount of quanta observed within a given tissue sample.

Lastly, Scaffold data of the identified proteins were exported (for example, quantification values, and significance of expression difference between the two tissue sample sources) for plotting using MATLAB (Mathworks). For each protein, the log2 of the fold change between the two tissue sample sources (Crus II/lobules I–IV) was scattered against the –log10 of the P value of this difference restyling from a t-test, as shown in the volcano plot in Fig. 2d. Of all identified proteins from the spectroscopy analysis, 289 were enriched in lobules I–IV compared with Crus II (P values < 0.05); 91 proteins were enriched in Crus II compared with lobules I–IV. While we identify a few individual example proteins in Fig. 2d, for quantification purposes, we use protein enrichment analyses below to compare the identified proteins between tissue samples. A list of all enriched proteins can be seen in Supplementary Table 1. The enriched proteins from each respective lobule were entered into an enrichment analysis using ToppGene (https://toppgene.cchmc.org). The selected P value method was probability density function, and the searched features were molecular function, biological process and cellular component. For lobules I–IV, significantly enriched cellular components included synapses, dendritic tree, neuronal projection and myelin sheath (all with Bonferroni corrected P values < 10−17). Some of these proteins are highlighted with arrows and text in Fig. 2d. For Crus II, the top identified cellular component clusters are cell-internal in nature, including spliceosome and nuclear protein complexes (Bonferroni corrected P values < 10−4).

Fractional amplitude of low-frequency fluctuations

Using the rs-fMRI data of both HCP datasets (see preprocessing methods above), the fALFF was calculated as the ratio of the low-frequency (0.01–0.08 Hz) power to the full-range power in the frequency domain23. Specifically, the fALFF for each voxel was calculated using the rs-fMRI time-series by first removing a linear drift trend, then transforming the time-series to frequency domain and finally computing the power ratio of the low-frequency range (0.01–0.08 Hz) to the full range (HCP-Adult: 0–0.69 Hz; HCP-D: 0–0.625 Hz). Frequency ranges vary slightly between datasets as the highest frequency measured is limited by the sampling rate (TR), which is slightly different between datasets, although both are subsecond. As with structural data, the median fALFF from each lobule was extracted in each individual. Data shown in Fig. 3b were entered into a repeated-measures one-way ANOVA (factor of lobule): F(10, 9,790) = 6,163, P < 0.001.

To evaluate the differences in trajectories illustrated in Fig. 3d, data were entered into a two-way ANOVA with factors of age group and lobule. Age groups were binned evenly into two age groups as for structural data (<14 and >14 years old). The interaction term between age group and lobule (indicative significantly different developmental slopes) was significant: F(20, 5516) = 10.17, P < 0.001. The significance of the regression fits in each lobule are shown with asterisks in Fig. 3e, a single asterisk denoting an α less than 0.05 after FDR correction.

The interlobular heterogeneity is quantified as a function of age for fALFF in Fig. 3f. The development of heterogeneity was fit using a linear regression as done for Fig. 1h. The annual-percent rate of change (the slope term) was −2.52. The fit was statistically significant at P < 0.001, R = −0.40.

For Fig. 3g, we compared the development of T1w/T2w ratio and fALFF directly across all participants. That is, for each lobule, the median structural (T1w/T2w ratio) and functional (fALFF) value was extracted for each participant and a Pearson correlation was performed between the measures across all participants. The resulting Pearson coefficient for each lobule is plotted in Fig. 3g. The significance of the correlations is shown with asterisks: a single asterisk denoting an α less than 0.05 after FDR correction. Another way to quantify the relationship between structural and functional development is to ensure they are coupled within an individual across development. To that end, within each participant, mean T1w/T2w ratio and fALFF values were derived in each lobule, and the two gradients were correlated with each other to result in a single Pearson coefficient for each individual. Should structure and function develop independently of each other, then the magnitude of the coefficient should decrease with age as the two gradients become increasingly distinct. Plotted in Fig. 3h, the coefficient in each participant is plotted, demonstrating that the correlation remains stable with a positive trajectory, with an average R2 greater than 0.5 across all ages.

To compare the fALFF gradient observed here, and the T1w/T2w gradient described earlier, to recently identified cerebellar gradients from adult cerebellar resting-state connectivity with neocortex, we formally related our fALFF and T1w/T2w maps within each individual to the two gradients identified in Guell et al.18 using a regression approach to derive variance explained and β values for each Guell gradient in predicting either fALFF or T1w/T2w ratio. In Extended Data Fig. 4, we find that fALFF maps best onto Gradient 1 and T1w/T2w maps most heavily onto Gradient 2.

Statistical analyses

Where relevant (two-sample t-tests, linear models, and so on) resulting P values are always two-tailed. The grouping factors in all conducted ANOVAs are reported in the methods above where described. The degrees of freedom, as well as the number of individuals within a given statistical analysis, are reported above in parentheses following the statistical value. Data were assumed to be normal, and no gross violations of data normalcy were observed: the distribution of all data going into analyses can be observed in the relevant violin plots within all main figures. Given that the groups being compared were defined by age (child versus adult, and so on), randomization was not employed for group selection. Correction for multiple comparisons was performed with an FDR approach in each relevant analysis. No statistical methods were used to predetermine sample sizes but our sample sizes are similar to those reported in previous publications using HCP data, as well as Gomez et al.8. Data collection and analysis were not performed blind to the conditions of the experiments; all participants underwent identical data collection procedures.

Extended Data Fig. 5 Data-driven analysis replicates the T1w/T2w ratio lobular gradient across the cerebellum.

The inner box of the violin plot indicates interquartile range and the center dot indicates median value.
