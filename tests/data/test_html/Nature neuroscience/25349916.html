Abstract

In the last decade, major advances have been made in the availability of shared neuroimaging data, such that there are more than 8,000 shared MRI (magnetic resonance imaging) data sets available online. Here we outline the state of data sharing for task-based functional MRI (fMRI) data, with a focus on various forms of data and their relative utility for subsequent analyses. We also discuss challenges to the future success of data sharing and highlight the ethical argument that data sharing may be necessary to maximize the contribution of human subjects.

Main

Neuroimaging, particularly MRI, has become the most powerful tool available to human neuroscience. The flexibility of MRI allows the measurement of macrostructural features (such as regional thickness of the cortex or volume of specific structures), microstructural features (such as diffusion anisotropy within white matter tracts) and a host of functional characteristics (from task-driven activation to resting functional connectivity). The scale of these data ranges from relatively small (for a single structural MRI data set) to very large (for high-resolution multimodal data sets and dense connectivity matrices) (Fig. 1). As an example, the Human Connectome Project (HCP)1 has, to date, released the entire imaging data sets for the first 511 participants in their study. To obtain the entire data set for this release (which exceeds 18 terabytes), one must order the data on multiple hard drives (the so-called Connectome in a Box) to be delivered by mail. Although the size of neuroimaging data pales in comparison to some other forms of data (such as whole-genome sequencing data), we take the need for 'sneakernet' delivery as a compelling criterion for considering these as 'big data'.

Very few individual studies involve data on the scale of the HCP, but a major factor in the expansion of neuroimaging data resources into big-data territory is the development of shared resources that span multiple studies and laboratories. Until recently, most of the neuroimaging data collected as part of research studies resided solely on hard drives within the laboratories of individual researchers. However, in the past 5 years the field of neuroimaging has seen a revolution in the development of open-access data sharing resources, and several large projects are currently under way that will increase the amount of available data. Although they represent only a minute fraction of all data collected during this period, these shared resources nonetheless push the limits of conventional approaches to data analysis and storage.

In this Review, we will outline the current state of data sharing within the field of neuroimaging, highlighting the opportunities and challenges that are arising as these big-data resources become increasingly accessible. We will focus particularly on sharing task-based fMRI data, which poses the greatest challenges because of the complex metadata needed for analysis; for overviews of data sharing in neuroimaging, see recent papers2,3,4. At the same time, many of the issues raised here are also relevant to sharing other types of data from cognitive neuroscience, including electroencephalography (EEG), magnetoencephalography (MEG) and behavioral data.

What are the goals of data sharing?

Maximizing the contribution of research subjects. The Belmont Report, which provides the framework for human subjects research in the United States, makes it clear that researchers have a duty to both reduce potential harm to subjects and maximize the possible benefits of the research. Because sharing data will necessarily increase the potential benefit to society of the subject's participation by providing greater opportunities for scientific discovery, Brakewood and Poldrack5 argued that researchers may have an ethical duty to share their data unless doing so would increase risk to the subjects. Although sharing neuroimaging data clearly presents potential risks to privacy, these can be effectively mitigated (discussed below), making the refusal to share data ethically indefensible in most cases.

Enabling new questions. There are many kinds of questions that simply cannot be asked using single data sets that are enabled by the availability of large shared data sets. One such class of questions concerns the characterization of common latent neural networks that underlie many different cognitive tasks. In standard cognitive neuroscience studies, one generally uses a manipulation within a single task to identify the neural systems that are putatively involved in the manipulated mental process6. This strategy has led to the association of some brain regions with a wide range of different mental processes; for example, the anterior cingulate cortex has been associated with mental processes as varied as pain, speech, cognitive control and tongue movement. An alternative approach, which has the potential to provide more selective structure-function associations7, is to examine the underlying neural components that span across multiple task manipulations. A number of studies have taken advantage of shared data sets to examine this kind of question8,9,10,11, providing new insights into the large-scale organization of fundamental cognitive operations in the brain.

Enhancing reproducibility. There is increasing concern about the reliability and reproducibility of scientific research12,13. One major source of concern within neuroscience is the low statistical power of many published studies14, given that this increases the proportion of published findings that are false15 and can create unwanted biases16. These worries have increased pressure to amass larger samples, but in many cases this is not feasible owing to recruitment constraints (for example, when the number of subjects with a particular disorder in any geographical area is limited) or financial limitations. Data sharing allows the generation of sample sizes that would not be feasible within a single laboratory, which enables the testing of hypotheses with much greater power and thus enhances the reproducibility of the results. This has been clearly seen in genetic association studies, for which very large sample sizes are necessary and publications by multi-site consortia are now the norm17.

Reproducibility has classically been considered in terms of the ability to replicate the same result across independent data sets. However, analysis of fMRI data involves complex, multistep analysis streams, and thus another important aspect of reproducibility concerns the ability to reproduce results on the same data using different analysis pipelines. Carp18 used a shared data set to examine the effects of a large number of variations in data analysis parameters (testing over 16,000 different pipelines) and found that in some cases the analysis choices had a substantial impact on the results. The widespread sharing of data would allow researchers to more thoroughly assess the reliability of published results across processing streams; for example, Ioannidis and colleagues19 have presented a measure called the vibration ratio that is defined as the ratio of the highest and lowest effect sizes across all possible data-processing decisions. Although many researchers will not have the computational resources to perform such analyses across a large set of analysis pipelines, the open availability of data would allow researchers with those resources to better assess the degree to which results are reproducible across analysis choices.

Improving research practices. In concert with the concerns about reproducibility have come worries about the degree to which some relatively standard practices may result in greatly inflated false-positive rates. Such concerns were raised several years ago in the neuroimaging community regarding circularity (also known as 'double dipping') in region-of-interest analyses20,21,22. More recently, the exercise of analytic flexibility (present in many fields including psychology and neuroimaging) has been shown to increase false-positive rates to the degree that even physically impossible results can be found to be statistically significant23. The knowledge that data will ultimately be shared provides an incentive for researchers to ensure that they engage in research practices that will stand up to further examination, and this positive effect is supported by recent evidence. Wicherts et al.24 requested data for a large number of psychological studies and then compared papers for which data either were or were not voluntarily shared upon request. They found that papers for which data was shared had greater effect sizes and lower incidence of apparent statistical errors than those for which data was not willingly shared.

Test bed for new analysis methods. New analytic techniques are continually being developed for neuroimaging data, and shared data provide a common test bed for those techniques as well as benchmarks against which new methods can be evaluated. The 1,000 Functional Connectomes project25 serves as an excellent example; data from this project have been used in a number of papers to demonstrate the application of new methods26,27,28,29. Similarly, the data from the OpenfMRI project have been used to examine the effects of different processing pipelines on analysis outcomes18 and test new methods to characterize the brain systems that span across multiple cognitive tasks10. Although the HCP is still collecting data, the early releases have already resulted in papers modeling functional segregation in the human cortex30, dynamic analyses of functional connectivity31 and demonstrations of substantial vascular contributions to Granger causality measures32.

Reducing the cost of doing science. Making data available can greatly reduce the cost of scientific research when those data are relevant to new questions. Table 1 provides a description of a number of currently available shared neuroimaging data sets (including but not limited to data sets involving task-based fMRI). Together, these resources include data from more than 8,000 participants. Under the assumption that each data set required an average of 1 h of MRI scanner time to acquire (at a cost of $500 per hour of data, which is a common rate in the United States), these data represent an estimated value of more than $4 million for acquisition alone, not to mention the cost of recruitment and data processing. Not all questions of interest can be answered using existing data sets, but to the degree that they can, the use of these shared data instead of collecting redundant new data could be a significant source of savings for researchers, allowing more money to be spent on personnel involved in data analysis.

Protecting valuable scientific resources. One often-overlooked benefit of data sharing is that it provides a redundant backup for important data sets. In discussing retrospective data sharing with neuroimaging researchers, it has become clear that a number of landmark data sets are now inaccessible as a result of hardware failure or lack of the necessary media readers to retrieve data from backup. Inclusion in a major database serves as a hedge against such hardware problems and ensures that data will be available and accessible long into the future.

Varieties of data sharing

There are a number of different types of data that can and have been shared from task-based neuroimaging studies, which vary in the amount of detail and degree of processing (Fig. 1). Table 1 lists the currently available resources at each level of data sharing.

Coordinate-based data. The most common form of data sharing involves the data that are actually reported in a publication. Many neuroimaging papers report the locations of significant effects in a relatively standard format that involves specification of three-dimensional coordinates in a standard coordinate space (such as the MNI305 and Talairach spaces). These coordinates can be used to reconstruct, in effect, the activation map associated with a particular statistical test, but the highly sparse nature of the reported data (often 1020 points out of the >200,000 voxels in a standard analysis) means that there is a substantial degree of information loss. Despite this, coordinate-based meta-analysis can be surprisingly effective at capturing the same patterns present in the original image-based data, though it is weaker than the full statistical maps33. Early approaches such as the BrainMap project34 used manual annotation of activation tables, which provides relatively precise annotation but requires significant human effort (and unfortunately the full BrainMap database is not openly accessible). More recently, the Neurosynth project35 has developed methods to automatically extract activation coordinates from publications, providing a much larger open database at the expense of precision in the annotation.

Statistical maps. An intermediate approach is to share images that reflect the processed results of a study, such as the statistical map obtained for a particular comparison of interest. This has the advantage of requiring much less effort and metadata because the analysis has already been completed, yet it still allows others to closely examine the results and use them for meta-analysis. Previous work33 has shown that meta-analyses based on image data are substantially more powerful than meta-analyses using coordinate-based data. The sharing of statistical maps is currently supported by the NeuroVault project, which allows easy uploading of statistical maps and visualization of those maps using a web browser.

Even though we focus here on statistical maps, in a more general sense one could talk about sharing all model parameters estimated from data. Those models will evolve, and in the future we will see meta-analyses being performed on parameters of models other than the massively univariate general linear models that are the basis for nearly all shared data today.

Raw data. The term 'raw' is deceptively complex in the context of MRI data. Because the data are acquired in k-space and then reconstructed to create images, the rawest form of MRI data would be k-space data prior to reconstruction. However, very few researchers actually save the unreconstructed k-space data; instead, the primitive data from the scanner are the reconstructed images. Here we use the term raw to refer to reconstructed images that have not been subjected to any post-processing operations.

The fMRI Data Center36 pioneered the sharing of raw data sets, and the OpenfMRI project37 supports such sharing for task-based fMRI data; similar resources are provided by the International Data-sharing Initiative (INDI) and 1,000 Functional Connectomes projects for resting-state fMRI4, and a number of projects for structural MRI (Table 1), as well as the Human Connectome Project for all of these data types. Raw data provide the greatest degree of utility and flexibility for reanalysis, but they also pose the most challenges owing to data set size and, in the context of task-based fMRI, the need for extensive metadata to accomplish the analysis.

Sharing task-based fMRI data

In comparison to sharing structural MRI and resting-state fMRI data, sharing task-based fMRI data has proven remarkably difficult. The earliest efforts toward this began almost 15 years ago, with the fMRI Data Center (fMRIDC) at Dartmouth College38. Over the 7 years of its existence, the fMRIDC collected 122 data sets and managed to provide access to them until the end of 2012, when it was closed because of a lack of continued funding. More recently, the OpenfMRI project37 has begun to share task-based fMRI data, with a current total of 24 data sets and 529 subjects. In contrast, more recent sharing efforts for resting-state fMRI data have already made available several thousand data sets. A primary source of this difference is the fact that task-based data sets are generally more unique than structural or resting-state fMRI data sets because each study typically employs a different task manipulation to examine a specific psychological process. This means that substantially more metadata is required to successfully analyze each task data set; one needs to know not only the MRI acquisition parameters but also the specific order and timing of stimulus presentation during the task. Even though there is ongoing work to establish standards for representation of this information (Box 1), most current projects have resorted to the use of arbitrary standards derived from specific software platforms. For example, OpenfMRI has developed a metadata organization framework based on the FSL software package, which can result in significant need for metadata curation if the data were originally processed using a different software package. We hope that with the development of metadata frameworks and their adoption by the major software packages, there will be less need for curation, which will make sharing task-based data more straightforward. At this point, however, it still remains challenging.

Box 1: Metadata standards in neuroimaging

To be useful, shared data require an appropriate description of how they were acquired and how they have been transformed (i.e., their provenance), which is generally referred to as metadata. To maximize metadata usefulness, it needs to be expressed in a common (and preferably formal) language that is easy to understand for researchers wanting to reuse the data, which in general requires a set of accepted standards. Such standards are often challenging in the scientific context because methods are rapidly evolving, but the neuroimaging community has reached a level of maturity at which it has become possible to formalize many of the descriptors necessary for a neuroimaging data set. One such standard is the XML (extensible markup language)-based Clinical and Experimental Data Exchange (XCEDE)56, which is a hierarchical XML schema that provides a framework to describe outputs of a wide range of experimental designs (not limited to neuroimaging). However, XCEDE falls short when it comes to describing complex provenance features specific to neuroimaging. To fill this gap, the Neuroimaging Data Model (NIDM)3 has been developed by the Data Sharing Task Force of the International Neuroinformatics Coordinating Facility. NIDM builds upon a well-established World Wide Web Consortium standard of describing provenancePROV-DM (http://www.w3.org/TR/prov-dm/)and extends it with terms found in XCEDE and DICOM (Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine) headers. A highly relevant subset of NIDM is NIDM-Results (http://nidm.nidash.org/specs/nidm-results.html), which is an attempt to formalize the description of neuroimaging analysis results produced by popular software packages such as SPM, FSL and AFNI. The format includes information about the statistical design matrix and type of inference as well as the statistical maps themselves. It is still a work in progress, but when finished and widely adopted it will substantially improve reproducibility and meta-analysis accuracy.

Beyond the data provenance and statistical design, it is also important to describe the psychological task used in a cognitive neuroimaging study because very slight changes in the task can result in major differences in activation patterns. To date this description has largely relied upon free text (i.e., the methods section from the relevant publication), but recently the Cognitive Paradigm Ontology project has been developed to formally describe cognitive tasks57. Although it is still under development, this project should ultimately provide the basis for rich semantic annotations of cognitive neuroimaging experiments.

At the highest level, cognitive experiments also require annotation of the psychological processes that are meant to be manipulated by the task. The Cognitive Atlas is a project that aims to formally describe these psychological processes and their relation to cognitive tasks so that the processes can be uniquely specified in the context of meta-analysis58. The Cognitive Atlas currently serves as the basis for annotation of data sets in the OpenfMRI project, providing a framework for linked-data analyses that can span from raw data sets to high-level semantic annotations.

Quantification and calibration

It is important that shared data are quantified in a way that allows them to be effectively combined and compared. For example, the 2-bit discrete code of nucleotides in the DNA sequence makes sharing genetic sequences relatively straightforward and effective. In comparison, most neuroimaging measures are not quantitative, in the sense of quantifying a specific physical parameter, but instead are relative measurements. It is essential to note that the lack of quantification does not necessarily impede the useful sharing of data. For example, the T1-weighted structural MRI images shared by the Alzheimer's Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI) have proven remarkably useful even though they are not quantitative and may vary between scanners. However, the use of quantitative measures can greatly increase the biological interpretability of the results obtained from shared data.

Recent advancements in quantitative MRI have provided the ability to generate quantitative maps of T1 and T2 relaxation constants39, providing greater power for multi-site and longitudinal structural studies. Similar attempts have been made to generate quantitative measurements of functional imaging signals, though this is fundamentally more challenging because the signal measured by standard fMRI techniques reflects a combination of vascular and metabolic contributions. Even though most analyses use relative measures (comparing the response of the signal to task manipulations or the correlation of signals across brain regions), interactions between the measured variables and vascular effects can influence the results. 'Calibrated fMRI' is an umbrella term describing various methods developed to disentangle vascular and metabolic contributions to blood oxygenation leveldependent contrast40. Most of those techniques are dependent on a combination of respiratory manipulations and arterial spin labeling techniques (see, for example, ref. 41), which makes them more difficult to administer and limits their temporal/spatial resolution and brain coverage.

Although calibrated fMRI may provide better interpretability and comparability in the future, especially for data sets that span large age ranges, the use of standard, noncalibrated blood oxygenation leveldependent contrast fMRI techniques does not preclude the effective reuse of data. Most commonly, the functional response is reduced to a statistical measure, and it is this measure that is subsequently combined across studies. Although the raw data themselves may not be on a common scale, the parametric statistical maps (for example, z-statistic maps) can be compared across different data sets in an effective manner33. For example, previous work has shown that cognitive processes can be effectively decoded across individuals using fMRI data collected on different MRI scanners using very different parameters by using statistical maps as the decoding basis37.

Credit for data sharing

Many researchers feel that sharing has relatively little upside but significant potential downside, and a fundamental challenge for advocates of data sharing is how to incentivize the process. A commonly noted concern is that others could take the data and publish results without crediting the researchers who generated the data. One approach to this issue is to release the data under a restrictive data use agreement that requires inclusion of the original researchers as coauthors on any resulting papers. This approach has been taken by the ADNI consortium42 and has been successful in leading to a large number of publications, but it has been criticized for being incompatible with current authorship standards43. In addition, any limitations on data use will necessarily reduce the utility of the data for the broader community. It is for this reason that some projects, such as OpenfMRI and 1,000 Functional Connectomes Project/INDI, have released their data under relatively unrestrictive licenses (such as the Creative Commons) or public domain dedication so that the data can be used by the broadest possible set of researchers. These projects make the assumption that researchers will behave according to community norms and acknowledge the source of the data in their publications, though the creators of the data do not necessarily receive direct credit (for example, though citation).

Another solution to the credit problem is the publication of 'data papers', which are meant solely to describe a shared data set44. Two new journals (Nature Scientific Data and GigaScience) are dedicated to such papers, and both have recently published work describing data sets shared through OpenfMRI45,46. This solution fulfills two goals: (i) a highly cited publication gives appropriate credit to the researchers for their data sharing efforts and (ii) the availability of data to peer reviewers provides flexible quality control, ensuring maximal reusability of the shared data. In contrast to the approach taken by the ADNI consortium, researchers providing the data are rewarded through citations to their data paper instead of coauthored papers.

Challenges for the future

As data sharing grows, we foresee a number of continuing challenges to its adoption and ultimate success.

Quality control. Using someone else's data requires trust that the data were collected in a way that ensures quality and integrity47. Because one cannot be certain of the circumstances under which data were acquired, there will necessarily be some degree of uncertainty regarding how consistently the actual acquisition procedures matched those described in the publication. It is clear that some researchers feel uneasy about this (for example, see comments on ref. 47), but the experience of previous and ongoing data sharing efforts suggests that users of shared data can generally perform independent quality control measures that are sufficient to ensure the integrity of the data. In addition, some data sharing projects (such as OpenfMRI) perform quality control on the data before sharing to ensure that they meet minimal quality standards.

Sharing code as well as data. As data analysis workflows become more complex, it becomes increasingly impossible to reproduce an analysis without access to the code used to perform that analysis. For this reason, we would argue that sharing all analysis and processing code is as important as sharing raw data. Resources such as GitHub allow easy and open code sharing as well as the potential for collaborative development. Although code sharing is still relatively uncommon, we believe it should become more routine. It should be noted that some journals in other fields (for example, the journals of the American Economic Association) require submission of any computer code used in data analysis; we believe that this would greatly enhance research practices in neuroimaging as well.

Controlling error rates. It is interesting that the term 'data mining', now embraced by much of the technological and scientific world, was until recently a pejorative term (also known as 'data dredging') referring to the act of searching through data until one finds a result that confirms a preconceived hypothesis. A serious concern is that as large data sets become more widely available and many researchers perform analyses, there will be an increasing number of spurious findings that simply reflect false positives arising from the large number of analyses being performed. This concern becomes increasingly worse as data sets expand because with very large data sets one can almost always find statistically significant effects, even if their magnitude is very small48.

There are a number of potential ways to address this concern. First, researchers embarking on analysis of a shared data set can register their intended analyses publicly, as is done in other areas of research for which shared data sets are analyzed49. This provides the researcher with the ability to clearly label results as either hypothesis driven or exploratory so that the former will obtain greater confidence. Second, researchers can adopt a strategy in which they always begin their analyses on a subset of the intended data set and only apply the analysis of choice to the remaining data once it has been fully parameterized and optimized. This approach will prevent overfitting to the specific data set at hand. This strategy was enforced in the attention deficit hyperactivity disorder 200 (ADHD-200) competition50, in which a portion of the data were released for model development and then unlabeled data were provided for model validation. The increased use of such competitions, which have been highly effective in the field of machine learning (for example, ref. 51), would be a welcome development in the field of neuroimaging.

Data factories versus the data bazaar. One approach to the need for large data sets is to sanction a small number of 'data factories' that will generate large data sets and make them available to the field, the HCP being a canonical example. Although there is certainly an argument to be made for targeted collection of large data sets, we also believe that all data collected by investigators (regardless of the size of the study) should be shared, thus generating a 'data bazaar' that offers data from many individual boutiques rather than a single large factory. These two approaches will enable the exploration of different types of questions because the factory will provide a limited set of measurements on a large number of individuals (to enable, for example, well-powered genetic association analyses), whereas the bazaar will generate many small data sets with different types of measurements (to enable, for example, analyses of relations between different kinds of tasks).

Ethical concerns. As noted, sharing data has often raised privacy concerns. For most neuroimaging studies, the primary worry is that individuals could be identified through the reconstruction of facial features from anatomical images. This risk can be mitigated through the use of 'de-facing' techniques that remove facial features from the anatomical images. Certain studies may also raise more generic concerns about subject identification; for example, the HCP sample is collected from families in a geographically constrained area, which makes it more likely that subjects could be identified using information such as age and family structure. For this reason, the HCP has adopted a multi-tier sharing approach, in which a subset of data from unrelated subjects is available more openly but access to the full data set is provided in a more restrictive manner.

It is important to ensure that subjects are aware that their data may be shared and used by other investigators. Many researchers now include language in their consent forms regarding the possibility of data sharing, but if the data are to be shared openly it becomes impossible to tell subjects exactly how their information will be used. This issue is particularly relevant in the context of the ongoing process within the United States government to reform the regulations that direct human subjects research, which could have significant impacts on how shared data are treated5. To the degree that the new regulations require detailed consent regarding future uses of data, the ability to share data collected in the United States could be seriously affected.

The role of journals and funding agencies. The only way to achieve data sharing across the entire field of neuroimaging would be for journals and funding agencies to demand it. Data sharing in molecular biology was largely driven by the adoption of a set of standards published in 1996, known as the Bermuda Principles52. These principles mandated that all genetic sequencing data be made freely available to the public in a timely fashion. Thus, many journals now require that genomic sequence data be submitted to a database such as GenBank before publication of an article based on those data. Together this has led to an explosion in the amount of shared genomic data available (with more than 174 million nucleotide sequences and 189 million whole-genome sequences in the GenBank database as of August 2014 (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/genbank/statistics/).

Unfortunately, the field of neuroimaging has a rocky history of journal-mandated data sharing. In 2000, the Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience required that all data for published neuroimaging papers be deposited in fMRIDC, which was met by a strong negative reaction from some in the neuroimaging community38. This stricture continued until the fMRIDC stopped taking new submissions in June 2006 owing to a lack of further funds (http://web.archive.org/web/20061019054001/http://www.fmridc.org/f/fmridc/aboutus/news.html/#news139). Although the reaction against mandated data sharing continues to reverberate in the community, it is likely that many researchers are more attuned to the value of data sharing today than in 2000.

The US National Institutes of Health currently mandates data sharing for grants greater than $500,000/year in direct costs, which means that most research grants fall outside this requirement. In addition, the timing of data sharing in these large projects is not specified, which means that it often is scheduled for the period after the grant has ended, when there is no funding left to support data sharing activities and no leverage for the granting agency to ensure that data sharing occurs. A development that could help improve this situation is for the National Institutes of Health to track data sharing in the same way that it currently tracks the submission of grant-related papers to PubMed Central; just as researchers who fail to deposit their papers in PubMed Central may jeopardize future funding, researchers could similarly be held accountable for the implementation of their promised data sharing activities. A potential technical solution to this is the use of LinkOuts within PubMed, which provide a direct link from the PubMed record of a publication to related electronic resources (such as online publications or data sets). For example, if data are indexed within the Neuroscience Information Framework53 and linked to a PubMed identifier, then a LinkOut record is automatically generated for the data set via the Neuroscience Information Framework LinkOut broker54; this method is being used to provide LinkOuts to all publications with data sets stored in the OpenfMRI database.

Scaling data sharing to the entire field. Although we and others have long advocated that all researchers share their raw data, it is clear that if our colleagues throughout the field suddenly decided to heed this call, there would not be sufficient resources. This is not a problem with data size: for example, if authors of 1,000 of the more than 2,000 fMRI papers published in 2013 decided to share their raw data, this would comprise a data set of approximately 2.7 terabytes (assuming that they are on average equal in size to the raw data sets in the OpenfMRI database), which is relatively manageable. The real challenges come in curating the data sets to make them usefully accessible to researchers across the field. Because there are no common standards for the organization of MRI data, most data sets require some degree of reorganization to match the standard structure of a specific database. As noted earlier, these challenges are relatively minimal for structural MRI and resting-state fMRI but are particularly acute for task-based fMRI, which requires substantially more metadata for effective analysis37. Within the OpenfMRI project, we estimate that it takes at least 10 h of curatorial effort per study to reorganize the data to match our standard structure and to translate the task metadata into the appropriate standard format for analysis; in some cases (for example, when the metadata are organized in ways that do not map transparently onto the OpenfMRI organizational scheme) the degree of curatorial effort can be much greater. The development of formal metadata frameworks (Box 1) should help address this problem.

Another solution to the scaling challenge is to change the desired endpoint from full sharing of raw data to sharing of intermediate results such as statistical maps, which may be a good middle ground between raw data and coordinate-based data. This still requires submission of metadata, but in this case the metadata addresses the nature of the statistical analysis used to generate the image and the task contrast being investigated rather than the specific order and timing of stimuli. Some of this metadata can be automatically extracted from the logs of the commonly used software packages such as SPM (Statistical Parametric Mapping), FSL, AFNI (Analysis of Functional Neuroimages), etc. It has long been argued that this may be the sweet spot for data sharing because it could be relatively easily achieved using a 'share this map' button with standard analysis packages. With the development of the NeuroVault project, which allows submission of statistical maps and metadata by a web page and an application programming interface for programmatic access, there is hope that this level of data sharing may become much more common.

The cost of sharing. It is an unavoidable fact that data sharing comes with a cost. Maintaining databases, curating data sets and metadata, and providing storage space and bandwidth for large downloads all cost money (Box 2), and there are currently few mechanisms to cover these costs. One useful development would be for researchers to directly budget for the costs of data sharing in their grant proposals, through a data manager who would oversee the organization and submission of the data to the appropriate repository. Because of the high costs of maintaining a large database, the field should also consider the use of peer-to-peer distribution systems such as Academic Torrents or BioTorrent55. This would allow the cost of data transfer to be spread across institutions and interested parties.

Box 2: Computational challenges for big neuroimaging data

Whereas analysis of a single fMRI data set can often be accomplished on a personal computer, analyzing and uploading large shared data sets to a database poses serious computational challenges, which have inspired a number of developments in recent years.

High-performance computing

Analyzing large MRI data sets requires access to high-performance computing systems. For example, performing motion correction on each fMRI time series in the HCP data set takes roughly 5 min, such that running even this simple operation on all fMRI data sets across all of the 511 subjects in the latest release would take roughly 42 h if performed in serial; for jobs such as cortical reconstruction that can take up to 24 h per subject, this would translate to well over a year of computing time to serially process the entire data set. Most groups processing such large data sets use compute clusters via a grid-computing interface. For example, using the Stampede cluster at the Texas Advanced Computing Center (the seventh fastest supercomputer on Earth as of November 2013), it is possible to obtain access to 4,000 cores for up to 48 h, which is equivalent to almost 22 years of computing time on a single machine. Given the embarrassingly parallel nature of nearly all the processing operations performed on these data, the use of parallel programming frameworks such as the Message Passing Interface is generally unnecessary, and substantial speedups can be achieved using standard grid engines without the need to modify existing software tools.

Databases

The sharing of large neuroimaging data sets requires powerful data management systems to both organize and provide these data to researchers. A number of such systems have been developed, including XNAT (Extensible Neuroimaging Archive Toolkit)59, LONI IDA (Laboratory of Neuro Imaging Image Data Archive)60, LORIS (Longitudinal Online Research and Imaging System)61 and COINS (Combined Online Information System)62. XNAT has gained particular interest given its open-source availability and that it serves as the basis for the HCP's ConnectomeDB63. XNAT's RESTful application programming interface makes it particularly useful for data sharing because the entire database's contents can be accessed programmatically via the web.

Bandwidth

Providing very large data sets can require large amounts of bandwidth. The HCP has adopted a commercial solution (Aspera Connect) that accelerates file transfers using a browser plugin; however, even this tool is not sufficient for sharing the full HCP data set, which is distributed by mail on a set of physical hard drives through the Connectome in a Box option. Another potential solution is the use of peer-to-peer sharing systems such as BitTorrent, as demonstrated in the BioTorrents project55; however, such solutions are only viable for data that are shared in a completely open manner.

Cloud computing

As an alternative to transferring the data to a local computing resource, it is increasingly possible to use data stored on cloud storage systems (such as Amazon's Simple Storage System) and perform the analyses using cloud computing resources that have direct access to that storage system. For example, the entire OpenfMRI database is available on Amazon's storage system, and an Amazon Machine Image is available from the Neuroimaging Informatics Tools and Resources Clearinghouse that allows one to allocate a system on the Amazon Elastic Compute Cloud with many of the major neuroimaging analysis packages already installed. The primary downside of cloud solutions is the potential for significant costs, though this can often be avoided through a solid understanding of cloud pricing markets.

Pipelines

To process large neuroimaging data sets efficiently, a number of pipeline tools have been developed. These tools provide the means to specify entire data processing workflows and manage the execution of those workflows on high-performance computing resources. Multiple pipelining solutions exist for neuroimaging analysis. The best known is the LONI Pipeline64,65, which provides a complete solution including a graphical user interface and grid computing interface. Nipype is a popular open-source, Python-based pipelining framework built with flexibility in mind66. Both LONI Pipeline and Nipype not only manage efficient execution of pipelines but also provide provenance tracking67. Other tools include the pipeline system for Octave and Matlab (PSOM)68.

Conclusion

The amount of openly available, shared neuroimaging data has increased greatly over the last decade, but sharing task-based fMRI remains challenging for technical, social and financial reasons. We believe that data sharing is a responsibility of researchers unless it is precluded by compelling ethical concerns because failing to share data minimizes the contribution of the individuals who volunteer to participate in research. However, widespread sharing of neuroimaging data would require resources that do not currently exist. Researchers, journals and funding agencies must work together to identify and implement technical solutions that allow the most effective data sharing without greatly increasing the burden on researchers.
