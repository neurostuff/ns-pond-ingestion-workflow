Abstract

Humans, and animals, often encounter ambiguous situations that require a decision on whether to take an offense or a defense strategy. Behavioral studies suggest that a strategy decision is frequently made before concrete options are evaluated. It remains enigmatic, however, how a strategy is determined without exploration of options. Here we investigated neural correlates of quick offense-versus-defense strategy decision in a board game, shogi. We found that the rostral anterior cingulate cortex and the posterior cingulate cortex complementally encoded the defense and attack strategy values, respectively. The dorsolateral prefrontal cortex compared the two strategy values. Several brain regions were activated during decision of concrete moves under an instructed strategy, whereas none of them showed correlation with defense or attack strategy values in their activities during strategy decision. These findings suggest that values of alternative strategies represented in different parts of the cingulate cortex have essential roles in intuitive strategy decision-making.

Main

When pressed to make a response under an uncertain circumstance, people and organizations first select a strategy and then determine a concrete response under the guidance of the selected strategy1,2,3. In normative theories, the optimal strategy is derived from exhaustive exploration of all options4,5. In practice, however, a strategy is often determined in advance of a specific option decision1,2. For instance, behavioral observations have suggested that a strategy decision is made on the basis of situation analyses, in which the current situation is classified as either an opportunity or a threat2,3,6. It has been proposed that the strategy decision is largely a process of perception rather than deliberate reasoning. To date, neural bases of decision-making in choosing the optimal strategy remain largely unexplored, although those of decision-making to select the best option have been extensively explored7,8.

Appropriate offense and defense behaviors are critical for an organism's survival in the competitive world. It has been shown in cats and rats that the innate motor behaviors of offensive attack in response to imminent opportunities of resources and those of defensive protection in response to imminent threats are both mediated by subcortical structures including midbrain and amygdala9. Humans, and animals as well, also often encounter ambiguous situations in which potential opportunities and threats coexist. Thus, it is critical to decide whether an offense or defense strategy should be taken before selecting a concrete attack or defense response. As there are both potential opportunities and threats, the process of strategy decision has to include a comparison of opportunities and threats. In addition, the opportunities and threats in such a situation are usually distal; thus, the process of strategy decision is cognitive rather than emotional or reflexive. It has been hypothesized that prefrontal cortical areas are involved in the cognitive process to prepare an appropriate defense when a distal threat is detected, whereas the midbrain evokes reflexive defense responses to a proximal threat10,11. The medial prefrontal cortex has been found active when a distal threat is detected12,13. However, little is known about neural correlates underlying the cognitive process for choosing a strategy of attack or defense.

Board games, such as chess, provide a good opportunity to study mechanisms of strategy decision. As the games are zero-sum with two players and information-complete, theoretically the optimal strategy can be determined by minimizing the maximum loss for either player (the minimax algorithm)14. However, this is practically intractable. Instead, proficient players, as well as computer programs, often use heuristic strategies to guide the search for the best next move15,16. In this study, we used functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), together with artificial intelligence evaluation of game moves, to explore neural bases of offense-versus-defense strategy decision in shogi17. Shogi, Japanese chess, is particularly suited for examining mechanisms of offense-versus-defense strategy decision, as the dichotomy between attack moves and defense moves is clearer in shogi compared with chess. We found that the rostral anterior cingulate cortex (rACC) and the posterior cingulate cortex (PCC) were activated in correlation with defense and attack values, respectively, in the given board position, and that the two values were compared in the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex. The network including the cingulate cortex and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) is essential in intuitive strategy decision-making.

Results

The experimental tasks and subjects' performance

Seventeen high-ranked amateur shogi players were engaged in a strategy decision task (Fig. 1a), in which the subjects decided whether the optimal strategy for the next move was attack or defense in a given game position. As there is a general consensus in shogi about classification of moves as attack and defense (Online Methods), we did not elaborate the definitions of attack and defense to the subjects in the experiments. As a control task, the subjects decided the best option for the next move under an instructed strategy (either attack or defense). Following an instruction of the task type (strategy decision or option decision under the attack or defense strategy), we presented the subjects with a game position for 4 s, during which time we instructed the subjects to decide the optimal strategy or the best move. Then we presented two (for strategy decision) or four (for option decision, three concrete moves, including the best one, and the fourth for a move other than the three (another)) choices, and the subjects chose one of them within 2 s. Board positions were trial-unique, and 60 trials of the strategy decision were randomly mixed with 60 trials of the option decision. The optimal strategy and the best option for each board position had been determined by a professional shogi player and by a computer shogi program (Online Methods). As the decisions made by the professional player completely matched those made by the computer program, we decided to regard these decisions as objectively optimal.

In the strategy decision task, the percentages of correct responses (in which the subject selected the optimal strategy) in both attack problems (with attack as the optimal strategy; 76%) and defense problems (with defense as the optimal strategy; 58%) were significantly higher than the chance level (50%) (one-tailed paired t test with 17 subjects, P = 0.0000082 and P = 0.032, respectively; Fig. 1b), while the percentage of correct responses for attack problems was significantly higher than that for defense problems (two-tailed paired t test with 17 subjects, P = 0.0038). This asymmetric behavioral performance indicates that the subjects preferred the attack strategy to the defense strategy (see below for more details on this bias). The percentages of correct responses (in which the subject selected the best move) in the option decision task were much lower under both attack instruction (19%) and defense instruction (16%). The subjects chose the another option in about 40% of trials (43% in attack problems and 39% in defense problems). The percentage of correct responses was not significantly higher than expected if the subjects randomly selected one of the three concrete options in the remaining trials. However, when we compared the responses to each problem among the subjects, we found that all 17 subjects selected the same concrete option in eight of the 60 problems given in the strategy decision task. The odds were significantly larger than expected, if the subjects were selecting one of the three concrete options randomly in 60% of trials (P < 0.00001, -squared test). Thus, it appears that the subjects were doing their best to select the best move.

Conversely, response times in the strategy decision task were significantly shorter than those in the option decision task (two-tailed paired t test with 17 subjects, P = 0.0063; Fig. 1c). The number of options for selection was different between the two tasks (two versus four), raising a concern of whether this comparison was fair. However, in additional psychophysical experiments conducted with two choices for both the strategy and option decision tasks, the response times in the strategy decision task were also significantly shorter than those in the option decision task (two-tailed paired t test with 5 subjects, P = 0.0098, Cohen's d = 3.2; Supplementary Fig. 1a), and the response accuracy in the strategy decision task was significantly higher than that in the option decision task (two-tailed paired t test with 5 subjects, P = 0.00054, Cohen's d = 5.9; Supplementary Fig. 1b). These results are consistent with the idea that the strategy decision was made intuitively without an exhaustive search of concrete moves.

Brain activities during strategy decision

To uncover brain regions specifically associated with strategy decision, we compared brain activities during the decision-making period between the two tasks. Several areas had significantly higher activities in the strategy decision task than in the option decision task (Fig. 2a and Table 1), including the right DLPFC, the rACC, the PCC, the bilateral lateral temporal cortex (LTC) and the left hippocampus (HC). Of these areas, rACC was less deactivated during strategy decision-making than option decision-making. We observed differential activities in both rACC and PCC between trials in which the subjects selected attack and those in w45hich they selected defense, but difference directions (higher in attack selection or higher in defense selection) were opposite between rACC and PCC (Fig. 2b,c). The activities in rACC were higher in trials in which the subjects selected defense, whereas those in PCC were higher when they selected attack (two-way repeated ANOVA with subjects' selection (attack or defense), and correct or error as within-subject factors; for rACC, F1,64 = 5.8, P = 0.019 for selection main effects, F1,64 = 1.8, P = 0.12 for correct/error main effects, F1,64 = 1.6, P = 0.14 for interaction; for PCC, F1,64 = 4.7, P = 0.028 for selection main effects, F1,64 = 1.7, P = 0.13 for correct/error main effects, F1,64 = 1.2, P = 0.20 for interaction). Unlike the activities in the rACC and the PCC, the DLPFC was comparably activated regardless of the subjects' selection (F1,64 = 1.4, P = 0.17 for selection main effects, F1,64 = 1.5, P = 0.15 for correct/error main effects, F1,64 = 1.7, P = 0.13 for interaction; Fig. 2d). Thus, strategy selections were differentially represented in rACC and PCC, but not in DLPFC. None of the activities in rACC, PCC or DLPFC depended on the correctness of subjects' selection. In contrast, LTC and HC were significantly activated only during error trials in the strategy decision task (F1,64 = 1.7, P = 0.13 for selection main effects, F1,64 = 4.1, P = 0.05 for correct/error main effects, F1,64 = 1.6, P = 0.14 for interaction; Fig. 2e). We performed the statistical tests described above without correction for repeating the tests in the four brain areas. However, even when we applied Tukey's post-hoc test with correction for the number of tested areas, selection (attack/defense) main effects remained significant in the rACC (q = 3.2, > qcritical, = 0.05 = 2.8) and the PCC (q = 2.9), although the correct/error main effects in LTC/HC became insignificant (q = 2.6).

Neural encoding of strategy values

What information was represented in these areas specifically activated in strategy decision, and how were strategies selected through networks in these areas? Inspired by the perceptual perspective in the managerial decision-making studies2,3,6, we here propose a simple value-based model that rACC and PCC represent the defense strategy value (DSV) and the attack strategy value (ASV) of the game position, respectively, whereas DLPFC compares the two values to make a strategy decision. This is consonant with theories in general perceptual decision-making, in which prefrontal areas compare two opposing signals from posterior perceptual areas18,19. To clarify the information respectively encoded in rACC, PCC and DLPFC, we quantified ASV and DSV for each game position by using a computer program of shogi play (Online Methods), as computer shogi programs have become powerful and competitive with professional players.

Using these estimated values as independent variables of logistic regression, we found that the mean value of the best three attack moves and that of the best three defense moves gave the best fit of the subjects' strategy choices (Supplementary Fig. 2ac). Therefore, we defined ASV and DSV at each position by these mean values. We also found that separate ASVs and DSVs, rather than differences between ASVs and DSVs, could better explain intersubject variances in the selection behavior (Supplementary Fig. 2d). We therefore conducted another general linear model (GLM) analysis of fMRI data, using subjective strategy values (SASV and SDSV; Online Methods) as separate variables, to search for brain regions that had activities correlated with these values. As it has been shown in previous studies of value-based decision-making that the difference between values of chosen and unchosen options (Schosen Sunchosen) is represented in the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (VMPFC)20,21,22,23, we also included the difference between subjective values of chosen and unchosen strategies (Schosen Sunchosen; Online Methods) as the third independent variable in the analysis. Activities in brain regions in which the subjective attack value is compared with the subjective defense value to make a strategy selection will be correlated with this variable. These three parameters were largely independent (correlation coefficient was 0.090.21 between SASV and SDSV, 0.030.07 between SASV and Schosen Sunchosen and 0.020.06 between SDSV and Schosen Sunchosen). We found activities significantly correlated with these subjective strategy values in the same cortical regions that were specifically activated during strategy decision-making (paired t test with 17 subjects, family-wise error correction, P < 0.005, cluster size = 39; Fig. 3 and Table 1). In the regions of interest (ROIs) defined by the first GLM analysis (Fig. 2a), rACC activities were positively correlated with SDSV (paired t test with 17 subjects, P = 0.0076, Fig. 3a), whereas PCC activities were positively correlated with SASV (P = 0.00084, Fig. 3b). In addition, DLPFC and LTC/HC activities were correlated with Schosen Sunchosen; the correlation was positive for DLPFC (P = 0.0092, Fig. 3c) and negative for LTC/HC (P = 0.00089, Fig. 3d). When Schosen was comparable with Sunchosen, the response time tended to be longer (Supplementary Fig. 3). In these cases, as the decision-making was difficult, it might call for the retrieval of similar situations from long-term memory. Note that Schosen Sunchosen was identical to |SASV SDSV|, or both values were small, around zero (Online Methods). As expected from this relation, DLPFC and LTC/HC activities were also correlated with |SASV SDSV|. However, DLPFC or LTC/HC activities were not correlated with SASV SDSV in an analysis including only SASV SDSV as a regressor (P > 0.05).

Thus, rACC and PCC activities were correlated with both strategy selections and strategy values. One of the two correlations could just reflect the other, as strategy selections were highly associated with strategy values (Goodman-Kruskal = 0.69, P < 0.00001 between the selection and SASV; = 0.72, P < 0.00001 between the selection and SDSV). To determine whether rACC and PCC activities were more closely associated with strategy selections or strategy values, we conducted partial correlation analyses. We selected trials with similar middle values of the relevant strategy (DSVs for rACC and ASVs for PCC; Fig. 4a), divided them into two groups according to strategy selections made by individual subjects, and compared brain activities in rACC and PCC between the two trial groups. We used ASV and DSV, but not SASV and SDSV in these analyses. Because the division of trials and comparison of activities were all conducted within each subject, the use of objective or subjective strategy values would not change the results at all. Objective strategy values were used only for plotting concrete values along the axes in Figure 4a. We reasoned that if the activities had represented strategy values, their magnitudes should have been similar between the two trial groups. On the other hand, if the activities had represented strategy selection, there should have had a clear difference in the magnitude between the two groups. We found that the former was the case for both rACC (two-tailed paired t test, P = 0.37 comparing the two data points in the yellow zone in Fig. 4b) and PCC (P = 0.41 comparing the two data points in the red zone in Fig. 4c). In contrast, when we compared the activities between the trials with the same strategy selection but considerably different strategy values, we found significant differences for both rACC (P = 0.00089 for the attack selection and P = 0.036 for the defense selection; Fig. 4b) and PCC (P = 0.00074 for the defense selection and P = 0.0093 for the attack selection; Fig. 4c). Thus, strategy values but not strategy selections were encoded in the activities of rACC and PCC.

Several brain regions, including the posterior DLPFC (pDLPFC), dorsal premotor cortex (PMd) and pre-supplementary motor area (pre-SMA), were activated during the option decision, as compared with the intertrial interval (ITI) during which the subjects were conducting a simple detection task (paired t test with 17 subjects, P < 0.05, false discovery rate (FDR)-corrected; Table 2). None of these regions, even at the ROI level, showed significant correlation with SASV or SDSV in their activities during strategy decision (P > 0.05, uncorrected). This general absence of correlation with ASVs or DSVs in the brain regions associated with option decision supports the idea that the representations of strategy values in rACC and PCC were independent from the evaluation of individual moves. Meanwhile, during the option decision task, no brain areas showed activities significantly correlated with either ASVs or DSVs (P > 0.05, uncorrected). Even in the rACC and PCC areas defined by the first GLM analysis, activities were not significantly correlated with either ASVs or DSVs (paired t test, uncorrected; P = 0.22 for SASV and P = 0.16 for SDSV in rACC, P = 0.20 for SASV and P = 0.17 for SDSV in PCC).

Response bias to attack

A prominent feature in our data was the widely varied response bias among the subjects, even though the subjects showed similar response sensitivity (d; Fig. 5a). Overall, there was a significant shift in the distribution of the response bias toward the attack side (mean = 0.38, s.e.m. = 0.10, P = 0.00066 using two-tailed paired t test; Online Methods). In the logistic regression model (see equations (1) and (2) in Online Methods) describing subjects' selection behaviors, the response bias was expressed by the ratio of a1 to a2 (a1/a2) and a3 (Fig. 5d). The values of a1/a2 and a3 also varied among the subjects but were mutually uncorrelated (r = 0.11, P = 0.34). Both of them were highly correlated with the response bias determined by the signal detection theory (r = 0.65, P = 0.0024 for a1/a2 and r = 0.56, P = 0.0097 for a3; Fig. 5b,c). Both a1/a2 and a3 were correlated with activities in rACC and PCC. The value of a1/a2 was correlated with coefficients by which activities were regressed to strategy values (DSV and ASV), while a3 was correlated with 0, which represents the signal component that constantly occurred in strategy decision (equation (6) in Online Methods). The polarity of the correlation was opposite between rACC and PCC. The values of a1/a2 and a3 were negatively correlated with DSV and 0, respectively, in rACC (r = 0.50, P = 0.020 between a1/a2 and DSV, Fig. 5e; r = 0.60, P = 0.0054 between a3 and 0, Fig. 5f), whereas a1/a2 and a3 were positively correlated with ASV and 0, respectively, in PCC (r = 0.43, P = 0.042 between a1/a2 and ASV, Fig. 5g; r = 0.62, P = 0.0040 between a3 and 0, Fig. 5h). There was no significant correlation between a3 and DSV in rACC (r = 0.18, P = 0.24), between a3 and ASV in PCC (r = 0.03, P = 0.45), or between a1/a2 and 0 in either region (r = 0.12, P = 0.32 in rACC and r = 0.15, P = 0.28 in PCC).

Thus, the attack bias observed in the majority of subjects had neural correlates, i.e., the deflated encoding of DSVs in rACC and inflated encoding of ASVs in PCC. A default selection of attack was unlikely the cause for the attack bias because attack selections were not faster than defense selections (two-tailed paired t test, with 17 subjects, P = 0.37; Supplementary Fig. 4a), and the response time difference between attack and defense selections was not correlated with the response bias across subjects (r = 0.076, P = 0.39; Supplementary Fig. 4b). Moreover, even in the three subjects who had response bias toward defense, rACC activities were higher during defense selection, and PCC activities were higher during attack selection (Supplementary Fig. 5). Finally, if rACC activities were associated with switches from the default strategy (attack) to the alternative strategy (defense), they would have been larger when the subjects selected defense than when they remained in attack. However, the analyses shown in Figure 4 demonstrate that rACC activities were correlated with defense values but not defense selections. As far as defense values of the problems were similar, there was no difference in rACC activity between attack and defense selections (Fig. 4b). A similar discussion is applicable to PCC activities. The bias toward attack in these amateur players resembles the phenomenon of overconfidence observed in many strategy decision situations, described as the optimism bias in planning24. The overconfidence or optimism bias has been attributed to either underestimation of difficulty in future work25 or positive emotion26. We argue that our subjects may have overestimated potential opportunities and underestimated potential threats. In notable contrast, we did not observe such a response bias in professional shogi players who performed the same strategy decision task (two-tailed paired t test with 6 subjects, P = 0.42, compared with the neutral point, and two-tailed t test, P = 0.043, Cohen's d = 1.9 between the professional and the amateur groups; Fig. 5a).

Functional interaction among the brain regions

To infer functional interactions among the regions in strategy decision, we analyzed correlation between trial-by-trial variances in activities of the DLPFC, rACC, PCC and LTC/HC (Supplementary Fig. 6). We removed stimulus-driven correlations by subtracting DSV-dependent components from activities in rACC, ASV-dependent components from activities in PCC, and components dependent on Schosen Sunchosen from activities in DLPFC and LTC/HC before calculating the correlation (Online Methods). Activities in rACC were negatively correlated with those in PCC either in attack selection or in defense selection during the strategy decision task (z test, P = 0.027 and 0.038, respectively; Fig. 6a). There was a significant positive correlation between DLPFC and PCC when the subject selected attack (z test, P = 0.026), whereas the correlation was significantly smaller (z test, P = 0.0066) and insignificant (z test, P = 0.16) when the subject selected defense (Fig. 6b). On the contrary, there was a significant positive correlation between DLPFC and rACC when the subject selected defense (z test, P = 0.041), while the correlation was significantly smaller (z test, P = 0.0089) and insignificant (z test, P = 0.19) when the subject selected attack (Fig. 6c). Negative correlation between DLPFC and LTC/HC became insignificant after removing the stimulus-driven correlation (z test, P = 0.26 for attack selections and P = 0.41 for defense selections, respectively, Fig. 6d). Therefore, DLPFC adaptively interacted with rACC or PCC depending on the ongoing strategy selection, whereas rACC and PCC always interacted with each other, directly or indirectly (Fig. 6a).

Discussion

We found that values of two competing strategies, defense and attack, were represented by activities in rACC and PCC, respectively, and that differences between the competing strategy values were represented by activities in DLPFC. The degree of attack bias of each subject correlated with the brain activities in rACC and PCC of the subject, negatively with the DSV representation in rACC and positively with the ASV representation in PCC. These results suggest that the experienced players determined the optimal strategy based on the simultaneous representations of DSV in rACC and of ASV in PCC. DLPFC may have been involved in the decision by representing differences between the subjective values of chosen and unchosen strategies. Although there are several brain regions activated during option decision as compared with the ITI period, none of those regions showed correlation in their activities during strategy decision with DSVs, ASVs or their differences. Therefore, the respective representations of DSV in rACC and ASV in PCC may be directly generated from analyses of features present in the given board pattern rather than through evaluation of individual concrete options. This idea was also supported by the shorter response times and higher response accuracy in strategy selections than those in option selections.

In post-experiment interviews, high-level amateur shogi players specified that there are key features of board positions for making optimal strategy decisions in the middle of a game, which are associated with the strength of the castle and the strength of the attack formation of both sides. Strong castle and attack formation of one's own side and weak castle and attack formation of the opponent favor an attack selection, and the opposite favors a defense selection. However, the key features vary considerably across board positions and cannot be simply described as a whole. Shogi experts may have developed the ability to intuitively determine the strength from perceived features through past experience of extensive game play.

Our fMRI results cannot be interpreted by other psychological accounts. First, one may argue that the PCC involvement in the strategy decision task might be associated with memory27. This seems unlikely as the areas thought to be associated with memory (LTC and HC) exhibited a different activation pattern from that in the PCC. The portion of the PCC activated in the strategy decision task was separated from the ventroposterior PCC generally activated during the recognition of board positions17 (Supplementary Fig. 7). Second, the activation in rACC might reflect affective responses associated with threats12,13,28 rather than with cognitive processes. However, we found that subjects' autonomic response signals (e.g., heart rates) during the defense decision were not different from those during the attack decision (Supplementary Fig. 8a). In addition, the ventral striatum and amygdala, which are parts of affective circuits, were not activated during strategy decision (Supplementary Fig. 8b). Furthermore, activities in rACC did not necessarily correspond to threat. The presence of the possibility that the opponent makes an imminent attack, posing a strong threat, should make ASV very negative because the situation would be much worse if an attack move, rather than an effective defense move, was made to counter the threat. However, activities in rACC were not correlated with ASV, either positively or negatively (Fig. 3a). In short, ASV and DSV did not simply correspond to opportunity and threat, respectively, in our data. The relationships were somewhat complicated. Third, these activations in rACC and PCC, one may argue, might merely reflect the strategy switching. It has been recently proposed that dACC encodes the value for switching to an alternative strategy29. However, we detected no activation in dACC during strategy decision in this study (Supplementary Fig. 9a). The significant activation after an option selection (Supplementary Fig. 9b) indicates that dACC might be recruited for the strategy adjustment after option decision. The strategy selection before an option selection may be fundamentally different from the strategy adjustment after an option selection. Finally, as discussed above, we believe that it is unlikely that activities in rACC and PCC represented a default strategy and switching away from it.

Our findings that rACC and PCC represented values of defense and attack, respectively, in the current situation are consistent with previous observations that these two brain areas are involved in both mediating strategies12,13,30,31 and encoding subjective values32,33,34,35,36. Specifically, neurons in the ventral bank of the most rostral part of monkeys' cingulate sulcus are negatively correlated with the utility of the target (a combination of reward and aversion) in an approach-avoidance task, and microstimulation of this area shifts the animals' selection to avoid the aversion30. Avoidance in the approach-avoidance model is a passive response to protect self from a negative consequence, whereas a defense move in our shogi eperiment is an active response to avoid the potential negative consequence. Thus, rACC may have a critical role in a general defense system responsible for both passive and active protection behaviors10. Neurons in the PCC display higher activities when monkeys select actions associated with more variable rewards32 or nonstandard actions35. These properties of PCC neurons may render a faculty of PCC to represent strategy values for attack. An attack selection tends to be more risky than a defense selection, as an attack provides not only an opportunity to defeat the opponent but also the risk to lose one's own power. It remains to be studied whether the monkey PCC also represents strategy values for attack or this cognitive expertise function has developed specifically in the human PCC. In humans, rACC and PCC have often been reported to be involved in mind reading37, moral judgment38 and other social cognition tasks39. Here we propose that rACC and PCC are jointly recruited in these social tasks because social cognitive functions often involve evaluations of social situations, where individuals are required to choose among competing strategies.

The process of value-based stimulus or action selection has been extensively studied in tasks of selecting stimuli or actions associated with different outcome values7,8,36. The process of strategy selection in experienced subjects, revealed by the present study, is similar to that of the value-based stimulus or action selection in that both processes depend on the comparison of values between alternatives. However, there are also important differences. In value-based stimulus or action selection, values of alternatives are compared and those of chosen options minus those of unchosen options are represented in VMPFC20,21,40, and values of alternatives themselves are also represented in VMPFC23. We found that value comparison in strategy selection was implemented in DLPFC, but not in VMPFC (Supplementary Fig. 10). Moreover, values of alternative strategies were represented in regions (PCC and rACC) separate from the region in which the comparison was made (DLPFC). Neuronal activities differentially representing the values of two alternative targets have been found in DLPFC of macaque monkeys41,42. Finally, we found that values of different strategies were represented in different regions (attack values in PCC and defense values in rACC). Although we found that rACC and PCC functionally interacted with each other, as previously suggested by mutual anatomical connections between the two regions43, these areas represented only values of attack or values of defense. It remains to be studied whether these differences between the value-based strategy selection and the value-based stimulus or action selection are applicable to general situations, or are specific to the current tasks related to game play.

In conclusion, our results provide neural substrates for the intuitive perspective on strategy decision. The values of different strategies were separately represented on the basis of analyses of the current situation independent of the evaluation of individual concrete options. This fast and effortless approach to strategy selection by value comparison may be regarded as a type of heuristic44. As the capability to intuitively generate the idea of the best next move17,45, the capacity of this heuristic offense-versus-defense strategy decision in experienced players may have developed through extensive training and constitute domain-specific expertise in board games.

Methods

Subjects.

Seventeen high-rank amateur shogi players (34 dan, 33.2 2.7 years old, male) participated in the fMRI experiment. Six professional players (46 dan, 2046 years old, male) and eight other amateur players (34 dan, 2955 years old, male) participated in additional psychological experiments. Note that the dan scale for professional players is different from that for amateur players. Any of the professional players was much stronger than any of the amateur players participating the experiments. Each subject gave informed consent in accordance with our protocols approved by the RIKEN Research Ethics Committee.

Tasks in fMRI experiments.

Subjects were presented with a board position of shogi and chose the best strategy (attack or defense) for the next move in trials of the main task, whereas they chose the best option of concrete move under a given strategy in the control task (Fig. 1a). The main and control tasks are referred to as the strategy decision task and the option decision task, respectively. The board positions were taken from middle phases of real games played by professional shogi players. The strategy instructed in the option decision task was the optimal one for the board position presented in the trial. Each trial started with an eye fixation on the central cross for 1 s. After a 1-s presentation of an instruction indicating the task type (strategy or option, and attack or defense for option decision), a game position was presented for 4 s, and then it was replaced with options for selection. The subject selected one from two alternative strategies (attack and defense) in the strategy decision task, or from four options in the option decision task, by pressing one of two or four buttons within 2 s. The four options presented in the option decision task included three concrete moves and another option, which meant another possible move. One of the three concrete moves was the best move and the other two were selected from the moves that were given the second to fifth highest values (the values of individual moves were determined by a computer shogi program, see below). Each trial lasted for 15 s, and in the remaining time of the trial the subject was engaged in a simple detection task, detecting a gold piece from serially presented shogi pieces (each piece was presented for 150 ms followed by a mask for 100 ms). The subject reported the appearance of the gold piece by pressing a button. The experiment constituted 60 trials of the strategy decision task (attack was the optimal strategy in half of trials and defense was optimal in the other half) and 60 trials of the option decision task (the instructed strategy was attack or defense in half of trials, respectively). Trials of the two tasks were randomly intermingled in each of four continuous sessions. The problems were trial-unique and the same set of 120 problems was used commonly for all subjects. The set had been randomly divided into 60 problems used for strategy decision task and 60 problems used for option decision task: the division was also the same for all subjects. No feedback was provided to the subjects after their selections.

The subject viewed images on a rear-projection screen through a mirror (resolution, 1,024 768 pixels; refresh rate, 60 Hz). Normal or corrected-to-normal vision was achieved for each subject. All images were restricted to a square area 6 degrees (in visual angle) in size surrounding the fixation cross. The position of the left eye was monitored during the experiment.

Additional psychological experiments.

To examine the professional players' performance in the strategy decision task, we conducted psychological experiments on six professional shogi players. They performed the strategy decision task outside the MRI scanner. The task was identical to the strategy decision task used for amateur shogi players in the fMRI experiments. The same 60 shogi positions as those used in the fMRI experiments were used.

To confirm that experienced players make strategy decisions intuitively without exhaustive search of concrete options of the next move, we conducted additional psychological experiments on eight experienced amateur players (34 dan), who were different from the 17 subjects participating the fMRI experiments. The strategy and option decision tasks used in these psychological experiments were similar to those used in the fMRI experiments. However, the number of choices was two for both strategy and option decision tasks; the choices were shown together with the board position from the beginning; the subjects were encouraged to press a button whenever they got a 80% confidence, even during the presentation of board position; the time up occurred 4 s after the end of the 4-s board position presentation. One of the two concrete moves presented as selection options in the option decision task was the best one and the other was selected from the second to fifth best moves. The same 120 shogi positions as those used in the fMRI experiments were used. The assignment of the positions to the strategy and option decision tasks was random, and reversed between two halves of the subjects.

MRI parameters.

All MRI experiments were conducted using a 4T MRI system equipped with a head gradient coil (Agilent Inc.). Functional and structural images were acquired using a parallel imaging technique with a bird-cage radio-frequency transmit coil and a 4 channel phased-array receive coil. For functional image acquisition, a TSENSE technique46 was used with a two-segment, centric-ordered gradient echo T2* echo-planar imaging (EPI) sequence. The volume repetition time (TR) was 2 s, the echo time (TE) 15 ms, the flip angle 40, the slice thickness 4 mm, the in-plane resolution 3.0 3.0 mm2 and the field of view 19.2 cm 19.2 cm. Thirty interleaved slices were collected in an oblique orientation, tilted 15 from the anterior commissure-posterior commissure (AC-PC) line (going downward anteriorly). Two-dimensional anatomical images, with a four-segment T1-weighted magnetization-prepared rapid gradient echo (MPRAGE) sequence, and low-resolution (1.72 mm 1.72 mm 1.72 mm) 3D T1-weighted anatomical images, were also obtained in each experiment in the same slice orientation as in the EPI acquisition. High-resolution (1 mm 1 mm 1 mm) 3D T1-weighted anatomical images were acquired for each subject in a separate session.

Definition of attack and defense.

The moves to check the opponent's king or break the castle surrounding it were regarded as attack. To catch an important piece of the opponent away from one's own castle for the preparation of future attack (note that the captured pieces can return to the board, due to the unique drop rule in shogi) was also regarded as attack. The moves to protect the player's own pieces, mostly the king and the castle surrounding it, from the opponent's attack were regarded as defense. A professional player with whom we consulted through the course of designing the project confirmed that these definitions of attack and defense match the common sense among professional and high-level amateur shogi players.

In shogi, players place their own king near one corner of their side and surround it by a few pieces in a rather stereotyped manner (castle, kakoi in Japanese). There are at least several representative patterns of castles, each of which has a name (Mino-gakoi, Yagura-gakoi, Anaguma-gakoi, etc.) and several variations. Players try to keep the castle until the end of the game. The king almost never joins attack in shogi.

As compared to chess, the distinction of the game's middle phase from opening and end phases is clearer and the dichotomy of categorizing moves in the middle phase into attack and defense moves is also clearer in shogi. This is primarily due to the presence of the drop rule and the relatively weak powers of many types of pieces. The number of pieces on the board does not decrease toward the end of the game due to the drop rule, and a certain number of pieces can be used solely for protecting one's own king as a part of castle surrounding the king. In addition, many types of pieces can only move to immediately neighboring positions, and attacking the opponent's king or the castle surrounding the king requires preparation of good attack formation involving many pieces. Players use most of their moves in the opening phase of a game to make a castle around their king and prepare an attack formation. The middle phase of the game does not start until the completion of castle and attack formation. Therefore, most moves in the middle phase of a game in shogi are clearly recognized as a part of attack or defense.

Even in shogi, there are some moves each of which has both attack and defense functions. We have checked individual candidate board positions and have removed the positions, where the best move had dual functions.

Strategy value estimation.

The strategy values of attack (ASV) and defense (DSV) for each board position were determined by using a computational shogi-playing program (the Analysis mode in Gekisashi 11, http://www.logos.t.u-tokyo.ac.jp/~gekisashi/). This program calculates the value for each move by exploring sequences of moves from the current position onward to the depth of the 18th move (including moves of both sides) using an iteratively deepening minimax algorithm, coupled with a static state evaluation function that contains half a million parameters fitted by actual positions and their consequences in fifty thousand games that have been played by professional players. A similar algorithm for chess was described in Tesauro16. A positive value means that the situation would be better after the move as compared with the current board position, and a negative value mean that the situation would be worse. The value of zero means no gain or loss from the current board position. Because we found that the mean value of the best three moves best fit the strategy selections by the subjects (Supplementary Fig. 2 and see below), we decided to use the mean value of the best three moves to represent ASV and DSV of the board position. The strategy selected by a larger computer-calculated strategy value completely matched the strategy that a professional shogi player selected as the optimal strategy. Therefore, these strategies were regarded as objectively optimal strategies. Also, as the option having the maximum move value also completely matched the selection of the optimal option made by the professional player, these options were regarded as objectively optimal options.

fMRI data analyses.

Image processing and analyses were performed using BrainVoyager (Brain Innovation). To correct for the rigid head motion, all EPI images were realigned to the first volume of the first scan. Data sets in which translation motions were larger than 1.0 mm or rotation motions were larger than 1.0 degree were discarded. Functional EPI images were then registered, first to the 2D anatomical images, then to the low-resolution 3D anatomical images and finally to the high-resolution 3D anatomical images. They were spatially transformed into Talairach space48 by resampling the data with a resolution of 2 mm 2 mm 2 mm. A spatial smoothing with a 4-mm Gaussian kernel (full width at half-maximum) and a high-pass temporal filtering with a cutoff of 0.005 Hz were applied to all functional images.

Statistical parametric maps were created using general linear models (GLM). Trials were modeled with a group of regressors for the period of game position presentation and subsequent response time and one common regressor for the period of gold piece detection. The sum of the game position presentation period and subsequent response time was regarded as the time to make the decision. To obtain the results shown in Figure 2ae and Tables 1 (significantly different) and 2, the four combinations of actual selection (attack and defense) and objective evaluation of the selection (correct and error) covered the decision period of strategy decision trials and two instructed strategies (attack and defense) covered the decision period of option decision trials. The analyses to obtain the results shown in Figure 4b,c were similar to these, but a single regressor covered the decision period of a part of strategy decision trials selected by combinations of ASV or DSV and the subject's selection. To obtain the results shown in Figure 3 and Table 1 (significantly correlated), the estimated SASV, SDSV and Schosen Sunchosen covered the decision period of strategy decision trials as modulated regressors. SASV and SDSV used in these analyses were estimated by a1 ASV and a2 DSV, respectively, by assuming that SASV change depends only on ASV change and SDSV change depends only on DSV change. The constant components of SASV and SDSV could not be estimated, but it did not matter because we only determined the signal components that varied with SASV or SDSV, not those that occurred constantly in the strategy detection task regardless of SASV and SDSV. The regressors were convolved with a canonical, two-gamma hemodynamic response function (HRF). The regression beta coefficients were first computed for each subject, and then subjected to a group random-effect analysis of variance. The results shown in the left column of Figure 3 and Table 1 (significantly correlated) were obtained by voxel-based analyses, whereas those shown in the right column of Figure 3 and in Figure 5 were obtained with the regions of interest (ROIs) determined in the first GLM analyses with constant regressors. For multiple-comparison correction in the voxel-based analyses, the FDR was used in the first GLM analyses with constant regressors (P < 0.05, Table 1, significantly different) and the family-wise error correction in the second GLM analyses with modulated regressors (P < 0.005, cluster size = 39, Table 1).

Note that Schosen Sunchosen was positive in most cases, because the parameters of SASV SDSV (a1, a2 and a3) were determined in individual subjects to optimally fit their selections. Then, the subject tended to select attack when SASV SDSV > 0 and select defense when SASV SDSV < 0, which means that Schosen Sunchosen was positive. Schosen Sunchosen took a negative value only when the subject happened to select the strategy other than the one expected from the fitting. This occurred mostly when SASV SDSV was close to 0. Therefore, Schosen Sunchosen was identical to |SASV SDSV| in most cases, and when different their absolute values were small.

To determine whether PCC and rACC activities depended more essentially on strategy values or actual strategy selections, we compared the activities with close strategy values but different strategy selections. For PCC, we selected problems with ASVs in a small zone near the mean (3 s.e.m., red zone in Fig. 4a), and divided the trials where these problems were used into two groups according to actual strategy selections of individual subjects, and compared the activities between the two trial groups. A similar procedure was applied to rACC (yellow zone in Fig. 4a) but for DSVs. Similar results were obtained regardless of the exact band size for problem selections. The ROIs defined by the second GLM analysis with three modulated regressors for the strategy-decision period were used in these analyses, and similar results were obtained with the ROIs defined by the first GLM analysis with six constant regressors for the strategy decison and option decision period.

The dorsal activation was located in the precuneus. (b) vPCC was comparably activated in all trial types (F[1,102] = 2.1, P = 0.15, one-way ANOVA). Error bars indicate S.E.M. across subjects.

Supplementary Figure 8 The strategy decision was not associated with affective responses.

(a) The heart rates in attack and defense selections in the strategy selection task were not different from each other (P = 0.26, two-tailed paired t test). The heart rate was normalized by the values during the inter-trial intervals of all trials in each subject. (b) The regions (ventral striatum and amygdala) thought to be associated with affective responses were not activated during the strategy task (P = 0.32 in ventral striatum and P = 0.24 in amygdala, two-tailed paired t test, the minimum P value among the six conditions). Error bars indicate S.E.M. across subjects.

Supplementary Figure 9 The dorsal ACC (dACC) was not activated during decision-making in either the strategy decision task or the option decision task.

The ROI for the analyses here was defined by the peak coordinate for encoding of search value (peak Tal: [4, 32, 17], radius: 10 voxels) in Kolling et al. (2012). (a) dACC was neither activated during strategy decision nor during option decision (P = 0.35, two-tailed paired t test, the minimum P value among the six conditions). (b) fMRI activity time courses in dACC during trials of the strategy and option tasks. There was a significant activation in trials of the option task, but it occurred after the selection was completed. The grey bar indicates the problem presentation time.

Supplementary Figure 10 The ventromedial PFC (VMPFC) was neither correlated with subjective strategy values in the strategy decision task, nor with the chosen value in the option decision task.

The regressor encoding the value of chosen option during the option decision task was analyzed only in the trials where the subjects chose one of the three concrete options other than the other options (about 60% trials). The VMPFC ROI (blue) for the analyses here was defined by the peak coordinates for encoding of subjective values (peak Tal: right, [3, 32, 3]; left, [8, 35, 2], radius: 10 voxels) in Levy & Glimcher (2012). (a) The location of VMPFC ROI, which is ventral to the rACC region (red). (b) The estimated parameters of fMRI signals in VMPFC associated with SASV, SDSV and Schosen Sunchosen in the strategy decision task, and associated with the chosen values in the option decision task. None of them were significantly different from zero (P = 0.21, two-tailed paired t test, the minimum P value among the four conditions). Error bars indicate S.E.M. across subjects.
