Language fMRI abnormalities associated with FOXP2 gene mutation

Abstract

Half the members of the KE family suffer from a speech and language disorder caused by a mutation in the FOXP2 gene. We examined functional brain abnormalities associated with this mutation using two fMRI language experiments, one involving covert (silent) verb generation and the other overt (spoken) verb generation and word repetition. The unaffected family members showed a typical left-dominant distribution of activation involving Broca's area in the generation tasks and a more bilateral distribution in the repetition task, whereas the affected members showed a more posterior and more extensively bilateral pattern of activation in all tasks. Consistent with previously reported bilateral morphological abnormalities, the affected members showed significant underactivation relative to the unaffected members in Broca's area and its right homolog, as well as in other cortical language-related regions and in the putamen. Our findings suggest that the FOXP2 gene is critically involved in the development of the neural systems that mediate speech and language.

Main

The recent identification of the FOXP2 gene through the study of the KE family and case CS (refs. 1,2) has provided the opportunity to track the effects of mutation in this gene on brain structure and function and, ultimately, on the uniquely human ability of speech and language. The present study examines how the functional neuroanatomy of language is affected by a mutation in the FOXP2 gene, which to date is the only gene identified to be involved in speech and language acquisition.

Approximately half of the three-generational KE family have a point mutation in the FOXP2 gene, and these individuals have a pronounced verbal and orofacial dyspraxia3,4,5 that is transmitted as an autosomal-dominant monogenic trait6. As the speech disorder is neurodevelopmental in origin and has not resolved despite speech therapy, it has been proposed that the phenotype arises from bilateral disruption of the neural network that subserves speech and language functions7. The proposal that the disruption is bilateral is based on the observation that although left-hemisphere damage is sufficient to produce severe and chronic deficits of speech and language in adults, this is not the case in children8, presumably because the plasticity of the immature brain permits a homologous region in the undamaged hemisphere to take over new functions. Despite the fact that the brains of the affected members appear radiologically normal, bilateral morphological abnormalities9 have recently been identified7,10. Abnormally low levels of gray matter density have been found in the inferior frontal gyrus, the head of the caudate nucleus, the precentral gyrus, the temporal pole and the cerebellum, whereas abnormally high levels of gray matter density have been found in the posterior superior temporal gyrus (Wernicke's area), the angular gyrus and the putamen. How these structural abnormalities affect brain function during language processing remains unclear. So far, the only evidence comes from a positron emission tomography (PET) study in two affected family members, which suggests that regions with diminished gray matter density are overactive (that is, have higher cerebral blood flow) during word repetition compared to the same regions in controls11.

The aim of the present study was to determine the pattern of brain activation associated with the FOXP2 mutation in the KE family using functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI). We predicted that the regions that are morphologically abnormal bilaterally in the affected members would also be functionally abnormal, as evidenced by performance on language tasks. In the first experiment, a covert verb generation task (responses are thought but not spoken) was used to examine potential functional abnormalities during word retrieval, while avoiding the confounding factor of articulation. The second experiment comprised two overt (responses are spoken) language tasks—verb generation and word repetition—aimed at investigating speech articulation with and without the additional processes of semantic search and retrieval. Importantly, the overt verb generation task also allowed us to ascertain that any functional abnormalities identified in the covert task were not due to performance differences.

We found that, during both covert and overt language tasks, the affected members of the KE family show significant underactivation mainly in two regions that were previously found to be morphologically abnormal bilaterally: Broca's area and the putamen.

Results

Experiment 1: covert language task

Ten members of the KE family (5 affected, 5 unaffected) and two groups of control participants, individually matched on age, sex and handedness to each member of the family, performed an fMRI task that required the covert generation of verbs in response to hearing nouns (see Methods). Outside the scanner, all participants successfully performed the task overtly using a different set of nouns, although articulation was impaired in the affected members.

The distribution of activated loci in the unaffected group (Fig. 1 and Table 1) and in the two control groups (Table 1) was similar to that found in other studies of covert verb generation12,13,14. The main region activated was an extensive area within the left inferior frontal gyrus encompassing the pars opercularis and pars triangularis (the classically defined Broca's area). In contrast, activation in the affected group was atypical, with greater and more diffuse involvement of (i) the right hemisphere compared to the left and (ii) posterior compared with anterior regions bilaterally (Fig. 1 and Table 1). Importantly, no activation was detected either in the left inferior frontal cortex (Broca's area) or in its homolog on the right.

To identify the brain regions that were significantly underactive or overactive in the affected versus unaffected subjects, while controlling for potential confounding factors such as age, sex and hand preference, we used an analysis that incorporated results from all four groups ('Interaction analysis' in Methods). Any significant differences between groups shown by this comparison would thus be attributable to the effects of the FOXP2 mutation. For this analysis, we hypothesized that those speech and language regions that are known to be morphologically abnormal would show underactivation or overactivation.

As predicted, many of the regions that were morphologically abnormal were also found to be functionally abnormal (Table 2 and Fig. 2). Among these regions, the most significant finding was in the pars opercularis of the left inferior frontal gyrus15, corresponding to Brodmann's area 44, which showed fMRI underactivation (less fMRI activation in the affected than in the unaffected group; see Methods). Underactivation was also detected in the right putamen/globus pallidus, the left supramarginal gyrus, the right inferior frontal gyrus (Brodmann's area 45) and the left precentral gyrus (upper part).

The interaction analysis also showed that the affected members had significant bilateral overactivation (Table 2) in some of the morphologically abnormal regions, such as the posterior superior temporal gyrus (part of Wernicke's area) bilaterally, the upper part of the left precentral gyrus and the right temporal pole, as well as in a number of parietal regions that had not been predicted to be functionally abnormal.

The time course of signal changes (Fig. 3) indicates that underactivation in the left pars opercularis of the affected group was not due to a temporal shift in the response, but to a genuine absence of activation. Likewise, overactivation in the precentral gyrus of the affected group was due to a significant hemodynamic response during the generation period in this group only.

Direct comparison between the two family groups (Table 2) produced similar results to those obtained in the interaction analysis with respect to fMRI underactivation and overactivation, indicating that the latter results were not due to differences between control groups.

Experiment 2: overt language tasks

The covert verb generation experiment revealed that the affected members of the family show abnormal functioning of a number of regions within the language system during semantic retrieval and articulatory planning, neither of which requires execution of speech. Whether these functional abnormalities are specific to the requirements of the task (that is, semantic retrieval and covert production) or whether they reflect poor performance by the affected members, however, could not be determined with covert tasks alone. For that reason, the five affected and five unaffected members of the KE family who had participated in the covert task participated in a second fMRI experiment that involved overt verb generation and overt word repetition. Importantly, except for their articulation problems, the affected group did not differ from the unaffected group in performance on these tasks. For the analysis of these experiments, we hypothesized that the regions that showed abnormal activation in the covert verb generation experiment would again show abnormal activation.

Overt verb generation

Participants were instructed to generate one verb to each of 40 nouns, and there were a few omissions (trials in which the subject made no response). The affected and unaffected groups did not differ significantly in the percentage of generated words out of a total of 40 (affected group: mean 93.4%, range 90–98%; unaffected group: mean 85.8%, range 73–98%; Mann-Whitney, P > 0.50), or percentage of generated verbs out of the total number of words generated (affected group: mean 73.2%, range 48–97; unaffected group: mean 68.0%, range 43–95; Mann-Whitney, P > 0.50). The latter score was calculated because some members of both the affected and unaffected groups had generated nouns instead of verbs in a number of trials.

The fMRI results for experiment 2 are shown in Figure 4a and Table 3. In the unaffected group, the pattern of activation was similar to that observed in the covert verb generation task: it was mainly left-lateralized and involved an extensive region in the inferior frontal and perisylvian cortices. In contrast, activation in the affected members was mainly bilateral, but unlike the first experiment, there was detectable activation in the left inferior frontal gyrus. Nevertheless, the level of activation in that region was significantly less than in the unaffected group, as was the level of activation in the putamen bilaterally (Table 4 and Fig. 4b). No significantly overactive regions were detected.

Overt word repetition

The affected and unaffected groups did not differ significantly in the percentage of repeated words (affected group: mean 97.6%, range 95–100%; unaffected group: mean 96.6%, range 85–100%; Mann-Whitney, P > 0.50). All errors were omissions.

The fMRI results are shown in Fig. 4a and Table 3. In the unaffected group, the pattern of activation was more bilateral than during the generation task, especially over the inferior frontal regions (see ref. 16 for similar findings). In the affected group, the pattern was similar to that observed in the covert generation task, with extensive bilateral and posterior activation and no detectable activation over the left or right inferior frontal gyrus. As a result, the affected members showed significant underactivation relative to the unaffected members in the left inferior frontal gyrus, pars triangularis (Table 4 and Fig. 4b). Significant overactivation was detected only in the left anterior insular cortex (Table 4).

Discussion

The underactivation of the left inferior frontal gyrus seen in affected members of the KE family invites comparison with adult aphasic patients with frank lesions in this region. The language deficits of the two types of cases are indeed very similar5, particularly in tasks that require repeating nonwords and understanding complex syntax, morphology and past tense. It is interesting to note that although the affected members of the KE family show a significant deficit relative to unaffected members in generating words from different categories (i.e., phonemic, semantic), their impairment is much less severe than that of adult aphasic patients5. Furthermore, unlike aphasic patients, they do not differ from unaffected members in their latencies to produce single-word responses, for example in naming tasks5, and do not have any noticeable word finding problems during conversational speech. This overall profile suggests that the affected members are not impaired in fluency or in semantic retrieval per se, but rather in rapidly selecting several appropriate items from semantic memory, a function that has been actually attributed to the posterior part of the inferior frontal gyrus17.

As mentioned previously, congenital or early acquired lesions in the inferior frontal gyrus rarely result in chronic language deficits, as speech can develop in the homologous region of the right hemisphere18. The fact that evidence for such reorganization in the affected members has not been observed in our fMRI tasks, and that underactivation was also detected in the right inferior frontal gyrus in the first experiment, is consistent with our hypothesis that the mutation of the FOXP2 gene leads to bilateral abnormality in the functioning of brain regions involved in speech and language.

The posterior part of Broca's area has been described as essential for the retrieval and selection of factual knowledge19 through access to the appropriate semantic field20. It is noteworthy that this region is also implicated in phonological processing and retrieval21,22,23,24, inasmuch as a core deficit of the affected members, namely verbal dyspraxia, is most evident during repetition of complex nonwords5, a task that requires rapid selection and accurate sequencing of articulatory units. Another region involved in phonological processing, the left supramarginal gyrus14, was also found to be underactive in the affected members of the family.

Structural MRI studies of the KE family have shown that both the caudate nucleus and putamen are sites of morphological abnormality in the affected members7. Here the putamen was found to be functionally abnormal in both overt and covert generation tasks. Functional abnormality in the putamen suggests that neostriatal dysfunction may contribute to the behavioral syndrome. Indeed, acquired damage to the neostriatum in adulthood25 can result in language deficits similar to those observed in the KE family. No abnormal functioning of the head of the caudate nucleus was detected, possibly because the tasks used here did not reliably activate this region in either the control or unaffected group.

In the affected members, fMRI activation was observed in regions that are not usually involved in language tasks, particularly over the posterior parietal, occipital and postcentral regions. Overactivation could reflect the recruitment of a compensatory circuit to counter dysfunction within the normal circuit, the use of an alternative behavioral strategy, or simply extra cognitive effort or attention. For instance, overactivation of the posterior middle temporal gyrus during the covert verb generation task may reflect increased reliance on a semantic memory circuit, consistent with the idea that this region is involved in the representation of actions26. As for overactivation of the left anterior insular cortex during the repetition task, it should be noted that this region is thought to be crucial for speech articulation27.

In summary, the present findings demonstrate that the affected members of the KE family display highly atypical fMRI brain activation when performing both covert and overt verb generation tasks, as well as when repeating words. Abnormally low levels of activation were found in Broca's area as well as in speech-related cortical and subcortical brain regions. The FOXP2 gene may therefore have an important role in the development of a putative frontostriatal network28 involved in the learning and/or planning and execution of speech motor sequences, similar to that involved in other types of motor skills29,30.

Interestingly, it has also been hypothesized that Broca's area is the human homolog of area F5 in the monkey, which contains mirror neurons that discharge both during internally generated grasping movements of the hand and during observation of another primate performing these same movements31. On the proposition that this matching of the neural code in the two conditions underlies imitation, and that imitation of manual gesture is a primitive form of communication, it has been suggested that area F5 may have been crucially involved in the evolution of speech and language32,33. Studies of the expression of FOXP2 in area F5 in nonhuman primates would be of great interest in view of recent findings indicating that, in late primate evolution, the FOXP2 protein acquired changes in its amino-acid sequence that may have contributed to the emergence of speech34. Tracking the effects of these amino-acid changes phylogenetically could help determine whether they are in fact associated with morphological modifications in the putative premotor precursors of Broca's area in humans.

By revealing brain functional abnormalities within the language system, the present findings help to characterize the link between the genetic abnormality, the brain morphological abnormalities and the speech and language disorder in the KE family. Together, the results of this cross-disciplinary approach provide a further step toward understanding the role of FOXP2 in the uniquely human ability of speech and language.

Methods

Experiment 1: covert language task.

The participants were five affected and five unaffected members of the KE family from the second and third generation (Supplementary Table 1) who were not selected according to any particular criteria other than their availability. Ten control participants were individually matched to the KE family members for age (same age or up to 5 years older), sex and hand preference. Informed written consent was obtained from all participants, as per the guidelines set by the Great Ormond Street Hospital for Children and the Institute of Child Health Research Ethics Committee. To ensure that the covert verb generation task could be performed correctly, we asked all participants before they entered the scanner to generate one verb aloud in response to each of ten nouns that were not subsequently presented during the fMRI task. These nouns were presented aurally at 3-s intervals. All participants performed this task successfully, although articulation was impaired in the affected family members. It should be noted that despite their impaired articulation, the affected members of the KE family show neither longer production latencies (for example, in naming tasks5) nor single-word finding problems (as revealed by performance on the overt verb generation task) compared to the unaffected members.

fMRI data acquisition.

Scanning was done with a 1.5-T Siemens Vision system. Anatomical images were obtained from multi-slice T1-weighted FLASH (fast low angle shot) images [TR (repetition time) = 31 ms, TE (echo time) = 11 ms, flip angle = 40°, matrix size 256 × 256 × 64, voxel size 0.75 × 0.75 × 3 mm]. Functional MRI data were acquired using a whole-brain three-dimensional (3D) EPI (echo-planar imaging) sequence [TR = 87 ms, TE = 40 ms, flip angle = 30°, matrix 64 × 64 × 64, 3 mm isotropic voxels]. For each participant, two consecutive runs of 120 3D data sets were collected. Each run consisted of ten task/rest cycles with six data sets in each state. During the 'task' period, participants were asked to covertly generate single verbs to nouns presented via earphones every 2.8 s (∼12 nouns per cycle, or 120 nouns per run). During the 'rest' period, bursts of amplitude-modulated noise were presented every 2.8 s.

fMRI data analysis.

The images were analysed using SPM99 software (http://www.fil.ion.ucl.ac.uk/spm). Images were realigned, coregistered, normalized onto the default T1 template in SPM99 (Montreal Neurological Institute T1-weighted template, obtained from 152 healthy individuals) and smoothed to 3× the original voxel size. The statistical analysis for each of the four groups of participants involved the comparison of task vs. rest states in a block design. These group analyses resulted in four average task-related activation maps, namely those of the affected members of the family (KEa), their matched controls (Ca), the unaffected members of the family (KEu), and their matched controls (Cu).

Interaction analysis.

Because the affected and unaffected family members differed in age, sex and handedness, the direct comparison of activation among groups was not considered the most appropriate for this study. Therefore, we compared the activation of each KE family group with that of its own matched control group, and then we computed the difference between these comparisons. This difference reflects the effect of the FOXP2 mutation on brain activation in this task. To detect the brain regions that were underactive, we used the following comparison: (Ca − KEa) − (Cu − KEu). To detect the brain regions that were overactive, the comparison was: (KEa − Ca) − (KEu − Cu). Within SPM99, both contrasts were entered as 1 − 1 − 1 1 (fixed effect).

Affected vs. unaffected group: direct comparison.

This comparison was performed only to ensure that the results obtained from the above interaction analysis were not due to differences in activation between the two control groups.

Small volume correction.

Since it was hypothesized that the regions previously found to be morphologically abnormal in the affected members would also be functionally abnormal, small volume corrections were used to identify regions of overactivation and underactivation. A 20-mm radius spherical volume centered on [±40, 20, 10] was used to include the morphologically abnormal inferior frontal gyri10 and encompass Broca's area, and a volume centered on [±60, −34, 20] was used to include the morphologically abnormal superior temporal gyri10 and encompass Wernicke's area. For the other regions, the volumes were centered on the coordinates of maximal morphological abnormalities10: [±56, −4, 26] for the precentral gyrus, [± 26, 15, −40] for the temporal poles and [± 48, −62, 27] for the angular gyrus. To investigate differences within the basal ganglia, which are smaller structures, 15 mm radius spherical volumes were used, centered on [±32, 2, 0] for the putamen, on [±22, 4, −2] for the putamen/globus pallidus and on [±15, 16, 10] for the caudate nuclei (coordinates from ref.10).

Time course of fMRI signal changes in a region of interest.

The mean signal intensity changes within regions of interest (ROI) were plotted using a tool box (developed by K. Christoff; www- psych.stanford.edu/~kalina/SPM99/Tools/roi.html) implemented within SPM99. Two ROIs were chosen on the basis of the results obtained from the interaction analysis. The first ROI, in the left pars opercularis, was the cluster centered on [−60, 12, 12] (n = 36 voxels), as defined by the results from the underactivation contrast. The second ROI, the upper part of the left precentral gyrus, was the cluster centered on [−57, −15, 45] (n = 46 voxels), as defined by the results from the overactivation contrast. Signal intensities within each ROI were averaged across all voxels within that region, and across all generation/rest cycles for each subject. The group data were obtained by averaging these across subjects within each group, and the error bars indicate ± standard error of the mean (s.e.m.). Percent signal change for each scan was calculated relative to mean signal intensity across the five last scans of the rest period.

Experiment 2: overt language tasks.

Participants were the five affected and five unaffected members of the KE family who had participated in the covert language study. The overt language tasks took place approximately a year later. No control participants were recruited for the latter, as the covert task revealed little difference between results obtained from the interaction and direct comparison analyses. Moreover, performance during the overt verb generation task in the affected group matched that of the unaffected group (see Results). This match was thought to be difficult to obtain from healthy individuals who may perform at ceiling level.

fMRI data acquisition.

Scanning parameters were identical to those used in the covert language task. For each participant, one run of 120 3D data sets was collected. The run consisted of blocks of repetition (R) and generation (G), alternating with rest periods (passive listening to noise, N). The blocks were presented in the order GNRRNG repeated five times, with four data sets in each block. Each block started with the presentation of one stimulus via earphones, followed by a 4-s silent interval to allow for verbal response, immediately followed by data acquisition lasting 5.8 s (interstimulus interval, 9.8 s). This delayed data acquisition enabled motion artifacts generated during speech to be avoided, while still detecting the resultant hemodynamic response. During the G and R blocks, stimuli were single nouns, and a short instruction, “Repeat” or “Generate”, was presented at the start of each data block. During the rest periods, bursts of amplitude-modulated noise (as in the first experiment) were presented instead of nouns, and participants were instructed to rest. In total, 40 words to repeat and 40 words from which to generate verbs were presented. The total scanning time was approximately 45 min (anatomical and functional acquisition).

fMRI data analysis.

Images were realigned, coregistered, normalized and smoothed as for the covert task. The statistical analysis for each of the two groups of participants involved the comparison of Repeat vs. Rest and Generate vs. Rest states in a block design. As data acquisition was delayed by 4 s, convolution with the hemodynamic response function was not used. These group analyses resulted in two average task-related activation maps per task (Generation and Repetition), namely that of the affected (KEa) and unaffected (KEu) members of the KE family.

Affected vs. unaffected group: direct comparison.

Underactivation (KEu − KEa) and overactivation (KEa − KEu) in the affected group relative to the unaffected group was entered within SPM99 as in the first experiment, for both Generation vs. Rest and Repetition vs. Rest comparisons.

Small volume correction.

As in the first experiment, small volume corrections were used (10 mm radius spherical volume). They centered on the coordinates derived from the first experiment: [±60, 12, 12] for the pars opercularis of the inferior frontal gyrus, [±48, 39, 12] for the pars triangularis, [±63, −33, 24] for the supramarginal gyrus, [±51, 9, 33] for the upper part of the precentral gyrus and [±18, −3, −3] for the putamen/globus pallidus. Coordinates of overactive regions were also used (Table 2). For those regions that were morphologically abnormal but not functionally abnormal in the first experiment (i.e., the caudate nuclei, angular gyri and temporal poles), the same coordinates and spherical volumes as those described in the first experiment were used.

Behavioral performance.

Responses given by participants while performing the tasks within the scanner were monitored via the intercom system and identified by two experimenters.
