Mapping cortical change across the human life span

  • Elizabeth R. Sowell1,
  • Bradley S. Peterson2,
  • Paul M. Thompson1,
  • Suzanne E. Welcome1,
  • Amy L. Henkenius1 &
  • Arthur W. Toga1 

Nature Neuroscience volume 6, pages 309315 (2003)Cite this article

  • 14k Accesses

  • 1747 Citations

  • 90 Altmetric

  • Metrics details

Abstract

We used magnetic resonance imaging and cortical matching algorithms to map gray matter density (GMD) in 176 normal individuals ranging in age from 7 to 87 years. We found a significant, nonlinear decline in GMD with age, which was most rapid between 7 and about 60 years, over dorsal frontal and parietal association cortices on both the lateral and interhemispheric surfaces. Age effects were inverted in the left posterior temporal region, where GMD gain continued up to age 30 and then rapidly declined. The trajectory of maturational and aging effects varied considerably over the cortex. Visual, auditory and limbic cortices, which are known to myelinate early, showed a more linear pattern of aging than the frontal and parietal neocortices, which continue myelination into adulthood. Our findings also indicate that the posterior temporal cortices, primarily in the left hemisphere, which typically support language functions, have a more protracted course of maturation than any other cortical region.

Main

At what age during the human life span do different tissues within the brain stop 'maturing' and start 'aging'? Do different regions of the brain mature and age at different rates? Are the last regions in the brain to mature among the first, or the last, to show signs of aging? These questions are crucial for understanding how different brain systems support cognitive and behavioral maturation and the inevitable functional decline that accompanies aging. Despite the vital importance of understanding these processes, there has not yet been an in vivo study to map the effects of aging on brain morphology across the human lifespan from childhood through senescence.

Recent advances in magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) technologies now permit the detailed, noninvasive and safe assessment of human brain structure in vivo, even in normally developing children. Structural brain imaging studies in normal children and adolescents1,2,3,4,5,6 have been helpful in relating the dramatic maturation of cognitive, emotional and social functions with the brain structures that ultimately underlie them. Adolescent maturational changes in GMD, for example, occur in spatially and temporally specific patterns, with parietal cortices maturing earlier than frontal cortices3,7. White matter density also increases with age during adolescence2. Longitudinal studies show that gray matter volume increases in early childhood and then declines after puberty1, and white matter volume progressively increases1,8. Recent work has also assessed how normal aging affects brain structure9,10,11, providing insights into the subtle cognitive decline that occurs as part of this process. A decline in gray matter volume is prominent between adulthood and old age9,11, whereas white matter volume increases between 19 and 40 years, after which it steadily declines9. Voxel-based morphometry studies in normal adults show that gray matter loss with aging is most prominent in the insula and superior parietal gyri10.

Another important but unanswered question regarding the maturation and degeneration of brain tissues is how cellular changes through childhood and adolescence, observed as a loss of gray matter on MRI scans, are distinct from those that also appear as gray matter loss in MRI studies of aging. Continued myelination during childhood and adolescence12,13,14 could account for age-related increases in white matter, and perhaps for decreases in gray matter. Post mortem studies have shown that myelination continues into a person's 30s and perhaps beyond12,13,14, which could explain the increase in cerebral white matter observed into adulthood in the volumetric imaging studies. Myelination, synaptic pruning15 and cell shrinkage16,17 are all candidate cellular events that could result in the observed in vivo findings across differing brain tissues and ages.

Here we attempted to answer some of these important questions about brain development and aging in a large group of normal subjects from a community sample representing a wide age range (7 to 87 years). We studied nonlinear age effects on GMD on lateral and interhemispheric brain surfaces using high-resolution structural MRI. Data from each individual were analyzed with a series of manual and automated procedures that required careful matching of cortical anatomy across subjects, and allowed assessment of GMD, representative of gray matter thickness, at each of 65,536 anatomically matched points on the surface of each cerebral hemisphere. We found highly significant, nonlinear patterns of GMD loss, most rapid during the first six decades of life, over dorsal frontal and parietal lobes on both interhemispheric and lateral brain surfaces. The pattern of age-related change in GMD was inverted in posterior temporal cortices in the left hemisphere, where GMD gain continued until about age 30. The regionally and temporally variable pattern of age-related changes in GMD observed here may parallel cellular changes observed across the lifespan in post mortem studies.

Results

We constructed statistical maps of nonlinear (quadratic) age effects on GMD for all 176 subjects. Multiple regression was used to determine the variance in GMD (at each brain surface point matched across individuals) predicted by the age-squared variable once the linear effects of age had been removed. Because statistical tests were conducted at each of 65,536 surface points in each hemisphere, and because we had sufficient statistical power with 176 subjects, strict Bonferroni correction was used to avoid type-1 error at P = 0.05. Thus, statistical results for nonlinear age effects were considered significant only if they reached a probability threshold of P < 8 107.

Multiple regression analyses revealed significant, nonlinear age effects over large areas of the most dorsal aspects of the frontal and parietal regions on both the lateral and interhemispheric surfaces and in the orbitofrontal cortex (Fig. 1). Scatterplots of these effects show a dramatic decline in GMD between the ages of 7 and 60 years with little or no decline thereafter. A sample scatterplot of the quadratic effect of age on GMD at one brain surface point on the superior frontal sulcus is also shown in Fig. 1, and is similar to others in the dorsal frontal and parietal regions (Fig. 2). In the superior frontal sulcus, there was a loss of GMD of approximately 32% between the ages of 7 and 60 years, declining to only a 5% loss between the ages of 40 and 87 years. Notably, the most lateral aspects of the brain in the posterior temporal and inferior parietal lobes bilaterally show a different pattern, one in which the nonlinear age effects are inverted relative to the age effects seen in more dorsal cortices. There was a subtle increase in GMD until age 30, which remained stable until a precipitous decline in later decades (Fig. 1). In contrast to the percentage changes reported above for the superior frontal sulcus, only a 12% decline in GMD was observed in the superior temporal sulcus between the ages of 7 and 60 years, increasing to a 24% decline between the ages of 40 and 87 years. Intriguingly, the rapid decline in GMD in these lateral regions occurs when the age effects in the dorsal surfaces level off. Inter-individual variability in GMD was accentuated in these regions, contributing to less significant age effects here than on the more dorsal surface of the brain. Regions shown in white in Fig. 1 were thresholded at a probability of 0.01 and did not reach significance with the relatively conservative Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons. Nevertheless, these age effects on GMD are distinct in the posterior temporal and inferior parietal lobes bilaterally, most prominently in the left hemisphere.

Maps of the interhemispheric brain surface show pronounced GMD reduction in the dorsal frontal and parietal cortices bilaterally (Fig. 3). Notably, the cingulate gyrus and occipital cortex surrounding the calcarine sulcus do not show the rapid loss of gray matter. The pattern of GMD loss in these regions is more linear, and less pronounced, than that seen on the lateral brain surface (Fig. 4). The age at base gray matter loss (lowest point in the quadratic curve) or peak gray matter gain (in the posterior temporal lobes) was estimated for each brain surface point and mapped onto the average brain surface rendering of the lateral and interhemispheric surfaces (Fig. 5). Base GMD levels in the most dorsal aspects of the parietal lobes seem to hit their nadir earlier (at ages 4050 years) than do the frontal lobes (at ages 5060 years). The peak age maps show an intriguing pattern of age effects where the association cortices of the frontal and parietal lobes show the most robust GMD loss early in life, and primary auditory (lateral surface), visual and anterior cingulate (interhemispheric surface) cortices show a much shallower decline over the life span (black regions in Fig. 5).

Total brain white matter, gray matter and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) volumes were also assessed for nonlinear age effects using multiple regression. The quadratic effect of age on white matter volume in this sample was highly significant (regression coefficient = 1.94, P < 0.000001). The increase in white matter volume peaked at age 43 and declined thereafter (Fig. 6). On average, the oldest subjects had white matter volumes similar to those in the youngest subjects. Quadratic effects of age on gray matter volume were also significant (Fig. 4; = 0.60, P = 0.01). The quadratic effects of age on CSF volume were not statistically significant (Fig. 4; = 0.33, P = 0.06), though a strong linear increase in volume was observed with increasing age ( = 0.49, P < 0.01).

Discussion

Never before have cortical changes been mapped with anatomical cortical matching techniques in such a large sample of healthy, normal individuals between the first and ninth decades of life. This is also the first report, to our knowledge, where normative cortical changes have been carefully mapped on the interhemispheric surface of the brain. We have mapped the trajectory of maturation and aging on the brain surface using nonlinear statistical tests to determine the age at peak gray matter loss/gain and to create scatterplots across the lateral and interhemispheric brain surfaces. The regionally and temporally variable patterns of aging on GMD observed here probably reflect differences in the underlying cellular architecture in those regions, and likely contribute to the well-documented variability in cognitive functions associated with aging. Although we cannot directly measure myelin deposition, somal size or synaptic density using MRI, post mortem studies indicate that these cellular changes occur simultaneously throughout maturation and aging12,13,14,15,17. Results from this study may provide a new window into the timing of biological changes that shape the human brain over the life span.

The marked nonlinear effects of age on GMD over the dorsal frontal and parietal lobes, on both the lateral and interhemispheric surfaces, show that the most rapid loss in GMD occurs between 7 and about 60 years. What accounts for the loss in gray matter observed (with MRI) so early in development when cognitive and behavioral functions continue to improve? In a previous study, we found a loss of GMD concomitant with continued brain growth up to the age of 30 years4. We attributed this decline in GMD to increased myelination into the peripheral neuropil because if regressive changes alone caused the apparent cortical thinning, we would not see continued brain growth. While measuring GMD in this and in our previous report4, it is possible that we have also been indirectly measuring changes in myelination. The total volume of white matter was noted in this study to increase with age until a peak in the middle of the fifth decade, consistent with previous reports9. The increase in white matter volume with age suggests that the decline in GMD until age 40 is related to an increase in cerebral myelination. Post-mortem studies have also shown that brain weight remains relatively stable until about age 40, only after which there is a gradual decline18. Notably, however, decline in GMD seems to continue past the age at which white matter volume peaks and total brain weight begins to decline, whereas the volume of CSF increases over the entire age span studied here. Thus, the loss in GMD after age 40 probably results from degenerative changes, possibly in combination with continued late myelination. Perhaps the decline in GMD levels off only after myelination has ceased in any given brain region. Results from this study suggest that the net decline in cortical gray matter tissue resulting from degenerative processes in senescence, such as cell shrinkage16,17, are less perceptible using MRI than are the more plastic developmental changes that occur earlier in life. It is possible that subjects who would lose substantial gray matter into their 80s would not be 'normal' according to the inclusion criteria used here to recruit subjects.

We found a distinct pattern of age-related decline in GMD on the lateral surface of the brain, particularly in the posterior temporal and inferior parietal lobes. The nonlinear regression curve is inverted in these regions where there were subtle increases in GMD before a more precipitous decline. The nonlinear age effects in the posterior temporal region did not reach statistical significance when using Bonferroni correction. However, the results are supported by findings from a previous report with an independent sample in which we observed subtle, linear increases in GMD with age in this same posterior temporal region between the ages of 7 and 30 years19. Thus, a relaxation of the rather strict correction for multiple comparisons seems warranted, and the findings of increased GMD in the left posterior temporal region can be interpreted with confidence. Other investigators have reported that maturation in the temporal lobes of children and adolescents continues later than does maturation in other cortical regions1. Only with the extended age range and the cortical matching techniques used here could we determine definitively that the trajectory of maturation and aging in the posterior temporal lobes is distinct from any other region on the lateral or interhemispheric surface of the brain.

We have not previously mapped gray matter changes on the interhemispheric surface of the brain in any of our normative studies. Thus, for the first time we are showing distinctions between the limbic and primary visual cortices and the dorsal association cortices of the frontal and parietal lobes. As on the lateral surface of the brain, nonlinear patterns of GMD loss are most prominent in the dorsal-most aspects of the frontal and parietal lobes. A distinct pattern was observed in anterior (but not posterior) cingulate cortices where GMD loss was more linear, with a gray matter difference of only 12% between the youngest and oldest subjects. A similar pattern was noted in the occipital cortex surrounding the calcarine sulcus. Notably, both the cingulate and primary visual cortices (or their optic radiations) of the occipital lobe are known to complete myelination early, long before the age of the youngest individuals studied here12,13. In contrast, the association cortices of the frontal and parietal lobes lose considerable gray matter (or gain myelination), and these are the regions of the brain that continue myelination into adulthood12.

The precipitous drop in GMD in the posterior temporal lobes after age 30 could be a productive structural change resulting from continued, 'very late' myelination, rather than an early degenerative change. In support of this hypothesis, myelination has been observed post mortem exponentially through years of maturity to senium12 in the intracortical neuropil of frontal, parietal and temporal lobes. Nonlinear effects of age on GMD in the posterior temporal cortex were more prominent in the left than the right hemisphere, and were confined to posterior language cortices (Brodmann's areas 21, 22 and 40; for review, see ref. 20). Certain language skills, such as vocabulary and overall linguistic proficiency can continue to develop into later adulthood (for review, see ref. 21). This protracted development of skills is consistent with the late maturation of language regions relative to other cortical areas. Furthermore, certain language functions, such as language production and word retrieval, deteriorate as a function of normal aging (for review, see ref. 22) and have been localized to more anterior language cortices (Broca's area), which are regions that we found to mature and then decline earlier than the more posterior language cortices. Language comprehension skills, in contrast, are usually associated with posterior temporal lobe structures and are apparently less vulnerable to normal aging22. White matter volumes peak later in the temporal lobes as well9, providing further support for the idea that temporal lobe structures mature and degenerate later than other cortical regions. If, however, the decline in temporal lobe GMD late in life is caused by early degenerative changes and not by very late myelination, then the temporal lobes must have undergone peripheral cortical myelination much earlier in development than did other brain regions (earlier than the ages of the youngest children studied here). Further studies of cognitive function and brain structure are needed to assess relationships among brain maturation, aging and cognitive changes across the life span.

This study has several limitations. First, the normative sample was a cross-sectional one. Longitudinal studies will be needed to rule out possible cohort effects over the nine decades studied. A longitudinal study of this kind, however, will not be possible for many decades because MRI technology has only been available since the 1980s. Including subjects from infancy through earlier childhood would also provide more insight into early maturation and late degeneration of the CNS, but young children have difficulty remaining still in the scanner to provide sufficient motion-free images. Cognitive measures such as IQ were not available for all subjects to determine how well this sample represents the wider population beyond what the inclusion criteria would ensure. The older subjects who chose to participate in the study could have been 'super-normals' (high functioning retirees in the local community) and thereby contributed to the reduced rate of decline in GMD observed after the age of 70. With these caveats in mind, our results provide new insights into the spatial and temporal patterns of age-related change in cortical brain tissue over the human lifespan.

Methods

Subjects.

Brain imaging data were collected from 176 normal subjects (787 years old). We studied 86 women (mean age 33.9, s.d. = 22.3; 83 were right handed) and 90 men (mean age 31.0, s.d. = 21.3; 79 were right handed). All subjects were recruited from community households randomly selected from a telemarketing database23 and were studied as controls for individuals with tic disorders24. Subjects were excluded if they had a history of concussion, substance abuse or seizure disorder. Subjects were thoroughly screened for neurological impairments, psychiatric illness, history of learning disability or developmental delay using a structured diagnostic interview either administered or reviewed by a board-certified child and adult psychiatrist (B.S.P.). Experimental procedures complied with the standards of the Internal Review Board of Yale University (where the subjects underwent MRI) and informed consent was obtained from all subjects, and for the children, from their parents as well.

Imaging.

Subjects were scanned (by B.S.P.) with a single 1.5-tesla superconducting magnet (Signa; General Electric, Milwaukee, Wisconsin) located at Yale University. The MRI protocol was a whole-brain gradient-echo (SPGR) T1-weighted series acquired sagittally with section thickness of 1.2 mm, no gaps (TR = 24 ms, TE = 5 ms, NEX = 2, flip angle = 45, FOV = 30 cm, 124 slices).

Image processing.

The MR images from each individual were analyzed with a series of manual and automated procedures that included: (i) transforming brain volumes into a standardized three-dimensional (3D) coordinate space25 using a 12-parameter, linear, automated image registration algorithm26, (ii) classifying images into gray matter, white matter and CSF, (iii) removing non-brain tissue (scalp, orbits) and excluding the left hemisphere from the right, (iv) automatically extracting the cortical surface27, (v) tracing 35 sulcal and gyral landmarks on the lateral and interhemispheric surfaces of each hemisphere, (vi) transforming image volumes back into their own native image acquisition space by mathematically inverting the transformation which took them into standard space (step (i) above), (vii) spatially registering all segmented images and brain surfaces for each individual by defining 80 standardized, manually defined anatomical landmarks (40 in each hemisphere, the first and last points on each of 20 of the 35 sulcal lines drawn in each hemisphere)28 and (viii) estimating GMD over the entire cortical surface of each individual's brain in native space.

Semi-automated tissue segmentation was conducted for each volume data set to classify voxels based on signal value that was most representative of gray matter, white matter or CSF. A simple seeded-sample, minimum distance tissue classification algorithm was used and a detailed discussion of the reliability and validity of the protocol has been published elsewhere7.

Each individual's cortical surface was extracted using automated software27 that creates a spherical mesh surface that is continuously deformed to fit a cortical surface tissue threshold intensity value (signal value which best differentiates cortical CSF on the outer surface of the brain from the underlying cortical gray matter) from the left and right hemispheric brain volumes aligned in standard ICBM-305 space25. The resulting cortical surfaces are represented as a high-resolution mesh of 131,072 triangulated elements spanning 65,536 surface points in each rendered hemisphere.

Image analysts (S.E.W. and A.L.H.), who were blind to subject gender or age, traced each of 17 sulci on the lateral brain surface rendering in each hemisphere: central, pre-central, post-central and superior temporal sulcus (STS) main body, STS ascending branch, STS posterior branch, Sylvian fissure, primary intermediate sulcus, secondary intermediate sulcus, inferior temporal, superior frontal, inferior frontal, intraparietal, transverse occipital, olfactory, occipito-temporal and collateral sulci. An additional set of 12 sulci were outlined on each interhemispheric surface (callosal sulcus, inferior callosal outline, superior rostral sulcus, inferior rostral sulcus, paracentral sulcus, anterior and posterior segments of the cingulate sulcus, outer segment double parallel cingulate sulcus when present, parieto-occipital sulcus, anterior and posterior segments of the calcarine sulcus and the subparietal sulcus). In addition to contouring the major sulci, a set of six midline landmark curves bordering the longitudinal fissure were outlined in each hemisphere to establish hemispheric gyral limits. We have developed detailed criteria for delineating the cortical lines and for starting and stopping points for each sulcus using brain surface atlases as references29,30. The criteria for lateral surface sulcal delineations have been described previously along with reliability measures19. Similar criteria and reliability measures have been obtained for the interhemispheric sulcal landmarks31,32; complete details of the written anatomical protocol can be obtained from the authors.

Points on the cortical surfaces surrounding and between the sulcal contours drawn on each individual's brain surface were calculated using the averaged sulcal contours as anchors to drive 3D cortical surface mesh models from each subject into correspondence33. This allows the creation of average surface models and maps of age effects on GMD. Given that the deformation maps (acquired during cortical surface matching) associate the same cortical anatomy in each subject, a local measurement of GMD could be made for each subject and averaged across corresponding regions of cortex4,34. Briefly, a sphere with a radius of 15 mm centered at each cortical surface point was made and referenced to the same spatial location in the gray matter maps (each hemisphere measured separately) for each subject. We chose the 15-mm kernel size to help sensitize the analysis to the cortical structures of interest and increase signal-to-noise35. The proportion of segmented gray matter voxels relative to the total number of voxels in this sphere was computed (at each point) and stored as a map of gray matter proportion (with values 0.0 to 1.0) for each subject. The proportion of gray matter in each sphere in each individual reflects local cortical thickness.
