Abstract

Cultural differences and biological diversity play important roles in shaping human brain structure and function. To date, most large-scale multimodal neuroimaging datasets have been obtained primarily from people living in Western countries, omitting the crucial contrast with populations living in other regions. The Chinese Human Connectome Project (CHCP) aims to address these resource and knowledge gaps by acquiring imaging, genetic and behavioral data from a large sample of participants living in an Eastern culture. The CHCP collected multimodal neuroimaging data from healthy Chinese adults using a protocol comparable to that of the Human Connectome Project. Comparisons between the CHCP and Human Connectome Project revealed both commonalities and distinctions in brain structure, function and connectivity. The corresponding large-scale brain parcellations were highly reproducible across the two datasets, with the language processing task showing the largest differences. The CHCP dataset is publicly available in an effort to facilitate transcultural and cross-ethnic brain–mind studies.

Introduction

Brain structure and function are highly influenced by the continuing interaction of biological heritage and environmental factors1,2,3. For humans, cultural experience that occurs in social environment has a substantial impact on cognitive and social functions such as processes of perception, attention, emotion, mental attribution and language4. Noninvasive neuroimaging methods uncovered the neural basis of the human mind and behavior in the past few decades, and research on the brain measures affected by cultural or ethnic differences has gained interest in recent years5. However, most studies in this field have been performed on small samples through unimodal neuroimaging experiments on specific cognitive functions. It remains unclear how macroscopic influences, such as culture and ethnicity, shape the structure, function and especially connectivity of the human brain at both the global and regional levels. Brain imaging datasets based on large populations with significant cultural and ethnic diversity are crucial for addressing this question.

Recent advances in multimodal brain imaging enable the exploration of the large-scale structural and functional connectomic architectures of the human brain and their relationship with behavior. For example, two projects with major impact, namely, the 1000 Functional Connectomes Project6 and the Human Connectome Project (HCP)7, have catalyzed the exploration of brain–behavior associations8. The HCP consortium developed advanced techniques for imaging data acquisition, analyses and sharing9. In 2016, the HCP released high-quality multimodal neuroimaging, behavioral and genotype data. These data have already led to numerous scientific discoveries, including a more sophisticated parcellation of the human cerebral cortex10 and the neural correlates of individual differences in behavior11,12,13,14. However, the participants in these large-scale datasets were mainly Caucasians who lived in Western cultures; thus, it may not be possible to extrapolate these findings to people from different ethnic and cultural backgrounds, such as the Chinese population. A large-scale neuroimaging project in parallel to the HCP but with a focus on the East Asian population would allow assessments on both diversity and reproducibility of brain–behavior association studies across different ethnicities and cultures.

In 2017, we launched the Chinese Human Connectome Project (CHCP), which aims to provide large sets of multimodal neuroimaging, behavioral and genetic data on the Chinese population that are comparable to the data of the HCP. The CHCP protocols were almost identical to those of the HCP, including the procedure for magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scanning, the data acquisition parameters and the task paradigms for functional brain imaging. The CHCP also collected behavioral and genetic data that were compatible with the HCP dataset. The first phase of the CHCP, which was initially supported by the Beijing Municipal Science & Technology Commission, was completed in 2020.

Results

The current release of the CHCP dataset represents 366 healthy adults recruited from colleges and communities in Beijing. All participants were Han Chinese individuals living in an Eastern culture (M/F: 167/199; age: 34.0 ± 18.1 years (mean ± s.d.)). All subjects were right-handed, and none of the subjects reported a history of psychiatric or neurological disorders. All subjects provided written informed consent. The protocol was approved by the Institutional Review Board at Peking University.

MRI scanning and protocol

Consistent with the HCP protocol7,15, we acquired multimodal brain imaging data, including structural MRI, diffusion MRI (dMRI), resting-state functional MRI (rfMRI) and task functional MRI (tfMRI) from each participant by using a 3 T Siemens Prisma MRI scanner (Siemens Healthineers). Seven tasks were used in tfMRI scanning, including motor, gambling, relational, social, emotional, working memory and language processing tasks. The task paradigms were identical to the HCP protocols7,11 but were presented in Chinese. The key scanning parameters for the CHCP study are shown in Table 1. The acquisition parameters of the CHCP and HCP are shown in further detail in Supplementary Tables 1–4.

Quality control (QC) is vital in the MRI scanning of CHCP. Following the QC in the HCP procedure16, a series of steps were implemented in the CHCP scanning, including pre-acquisition scanner monitoring, real-time oversight during acquisition, and the post-acquisition manual and automated image review. The pre-acquisition procedures involved the regular maintenance of the scanner and daily head coil quality assurance to verify that the signal-to-noise ratio of each coil element represented the normal working condition. A real-time monitoring system was used to detect and record head movement during fMRI data acquisition. The post-acquisition manual and automated image review include the visual inspection of all images by a technologist and automated QC measures for high-quality images.

Altogether, the CHCP images were screened for excessive motion and other artifacts, and acquisition and processing errors. For the T1-weighted (T1W) and T2-weighted (T2W) structural images, each image was visually checked on a slice-by-slice basis along each principal axis. Typical data quality issues, including motion artifacts appearing as blurry ripple-like bands across the image, signal loss caused by dental implants and poor head positioning resulting in wraparound artifacts, were visually checked during scanning to ensure image quality. For dMRI scanning, a comprehensive and nonparametric approach for movement and distortion correction was used to extract a rich set of quality control metrics. For both rfMRI and tfMRI scanning, the engagement of participants was monitored in real time, and several strategies were adopted to minimize the impact of head movement. The details can be found in the Methods section.

Comparisons between CHCP and HCP data

The comparisons between CHCP and HCP data were primarily based on two subsets of participants from the CHCP and HCP datasets who were matched with respect to demographic features: age, gender, levels of education and handedness. In addition, only the participants whose brain images were of high quality for MR structural (that is T1W/T2W and dMRI) and functional modality (that is, rfMRI and tfMRI) were included in each subset. Since the HCP dataset contains twin participants (both monozygotic and dizygotic), only one participant from each twin pair was eligible in the subset to avoid the confounding effects of twin similarity in the brain network17. Using these strict criteria, a total of 140 participants from the CHCP (68 females, aged 21–31 years, mean ± s.d.: 24.0 ± 2.3 years) and 140 participants from the HCP (62 females, aged 22–27 years, mean ± s.d.: 24.4 ± 1.7 years) were used for functional comparison in the current analysis, and there were no significant age differences between the two subsets (P > 0.1, two-sided independent-sample t-test). An initial analysis of the structural and functional measures between the CHCP and HCP data showed comparable qualities in the multimodal brain images, including the dMRI (Fig. 1a) and functional MRI images (Fig. 1b). We calculated the surface-based regional homogeneity18 and fractional amplitude of low-frequency fluctuation19 of the rfMRI data by using an integrative pipeline, specifically, DPABIsurf20. The regional homogeneity and fractional amplitude of low-frequency fluctuation maps of the CHCP and HCP data exhibited highly consistent spatial patterns. In the tfMRI data, the intraclass correlation was calculated to quantify the reliability of the task-evoked activations across two sequential scans. Extended Data Fig. 1 shows the reliability of the key contrasts of the seven tasks, and most of the activated brain areas in each task showed a relatively higher intraclass correlation for both the CHCP and HCP.

Structural connectomes reveal differences in CHCP versus HCP

Using a previous atlas as a reference21, we parcellated the CHCP and HCP brain into 210 cortical regions by using dMRI data (Fig. 2a) with an individualized parcellation technique22. The group-level cortical parcellation maps, which were termed the brainnetome atlas, were constructed based on individualized maps from 140 demographically matched participants in each dataset. Several brain areas in these two atlases, including the anterior and posterior portions of the right cingulate cortex, the medial parietal cortex and precuneus, the bilateral temporal pole, the posterior portion of the left superior and inferior temporal cortex (including part of Wernicke’s area), the bilateral central sulcus area, the inferior frontal cortex (part of Broca’s area) and the orbital gyri, showed marked topographic differences (Fig. 2b). Furthermore, the structural connections within and between seven canonical functional networks23 showed significant differences between the CHCP and HCP in the intra-network connections in the dorsal attention, ventral attention, frontoparietal and default mode networks as well as the auditory network, and in inter-network connections including the visual–most other (except for the default mode) networks, somatomotor–default, dorsal attention–auditory, dorsal attention–frontoparietal, ventral attention–frontoparietal and frontoparietal–default networks (P < 0.001, two-sided, false discovery rate (FDR) corrected) (Fig. 2c).

Brain functional network differences in CHCP versus HCP

We parcellated the brain cortex into seven canonical functional networks based on rfMRI data from the participants in the CHCP and HCP (Fig. 3a). Interestingly, compared with the canonical seven-network atlas constructed by Yeo and colleagues23, both the CHCP and HCP atlases that we identified here showed that the limbic network was integrated into the default mode network, while the other networks (visual, somatomotor, ventral attention, dorsal attention and frontoparietal) remained unchanged. Instead, an alternative network along the superior temporal lobe was identified from parcellation estimates (Fig. 3a) and was labeled the auditory network according to the latest population-level 17-network parcellation24. The auditory network was consistently identified in both the CHCP and HCP atlases; this may be due to the high spatiotemporal resolution brain images in the CHCP and HCP datasets allowing the separation of the auditory and somatomotor networks that are spatially close to one another24. Notably, the label of each network should not be regarded as the related network that corresponds to that in the literature, and the functions of the network should not be associated with its assigned name23. An analysis of the surface area of each network for the CHCP and HCP revealed that the seven-network atlas of the CHCP had a significantly greater cortical surface area size ratio (that is, the local surface area compared to the total surface area) in the default mode network (permutation test P value Pperm < 0.05, two-sided, FDR corrected) but a smaller cortical surface area ratio in the auditory network than the HCP atlas (Pperm < 0.001, two-sided, FDR corrected). While the difference in the ventral and dorsal attention networks were marginally significant (ventral attention: Pperm = 0.057 uncorrected, two-sided, Pperm = 0.099, FDR corrected; dorsal attention: Pperm = 0.045 uncorrected, two-sided, Pperm = 0.099, FDR corrected), there were no significant differences found in the frontoparietal, visual and somatomotor networks with respect to the cortical surface area size ratio between these two groups (Fig. 3b). Further analysis of the topographic differences between the CHCP and HCP atlases (Fig. 3c) revealed large topographic differences in the frontoparietal, dorsal attention and default mode networks in both hemispheres but minimal differences in the visual and somatomotor networks, while the 1-Dice coefficient values in all seven networks were significant (Pperm < 0.001, two-sided, FDR corrected). Additionally, the topological differences in the hemispheric asymmetry between these two populations revealed that the dorsal attention and frontoparietal networks exhibited the highest asymmetry, and the visual, auditory, ventral attention and default mode networks showed moderate asymmetry, whereas the somatomotor network exhibited the least asymmetry.

Differences in the brain activation in complex cognition

Figure 4a shows the group-level statistical maps of the key contrasts for each of the seven tasks in the CHCP and HCP data. The z-maps include left hand versus right hand in the motor processing task, mostly reward versus mostly loss in the gambling task, relational versus control in the relational processing task, social versus random motion in the social cognition task, face versus shape in the emotion processing task, 2-back versus 0-back in the working memory task and stories versus mathematics in the language processing task. The results of the two-sample t-test and effect size between the CHCP and HCP at the vertex level are shown in Extended Data Fig. 2. Furthermore, for all seven tasks, we analyzed the differences in the distribution of individuals’ contrast values based on the seven-network functional atlas described above for each group. The differences between the CHCP and HCP were tested by using the nonparametric Kolmogorov–Smirnov (K–S) test25 with a threshold of P < 0.001 FDR corrected (two-sided). Figure 4b shows the distribution difference of brain activation (that is, the contrast value) in the motor and language tasks. Intriguingly, significant differences were observed in most of the seven networks during the language task, whereas there was no significant difference in any of the seven networks (all P > 0.05, two-sided) during the motor task. The distributions of brain activation for the other five tasks (gambling, relational, social, emotion and working memory) are presented in Extended Data Fig. 3. We found similar patterns for the volume-based group-level statistical maps between the CHCP and HCP in all seven tasks (see Extended Data Fig. 4 for details). The analysis of the general effect size of the difference between the CHCP and HCP for each key contrast in tfMRI showed that the language task had the greatest effect size for both the left and right hemispheres while the motor task had the smallest effect size (Fig. 4c).

Discussion

The CHCP provides structural and functional (both resting and task) brain imaging data as well as behavioral and genetic data that were collected from a large cohort of Chinese adults by using a protocol that is comparable to that of the HCP. The CHCP dataset is now released to the public for the first time and will serve as a valuable supplement to the HCP; it will allow examinations of both diversity and reproducibility of the human connectomic characteristics between two populations from drastically distinct ethnic and cultural backgrounds. The CHCP dataset also recorded the subjects’ performance during tasks in a scanner and their scores on a battery of behavioral tests and self-report questionnaires. Combined with the HCP, these data will allow investigations to map macroscopic human brain circuits and their relationships to behavior in two distinct cultural and ethnic populations. In this work, we described the details of the CHCP data and performed multiple analyses to compare the structural and functional connectomics between the CHCP and HCP datasets.

Decades of neuroplasticity research have uncovered that the human brain is drastically shaped by experience2,26. Previous work has revealed culture-related variations in human cognition, emotion, and motivation5. However, it remains unclear which parts of the brain are influenced by cultural experience. Neuroscientists and psychologists have endeavored to distinguish the culture-sensitive from the universal, culture-invariant neurobiological substrates of human cognition4 and to understand how they differentially contribute to cognition and behavior. Nevertheless, previous research on cultural differences in brain characteristics mainly focused on a few brain regions involved in certain mental processes and rarely investigated the structural and functional differences at the whole-brain level due to their small sample size. The CHCP provides a valuable dataset to supplement the HCP, and both datasets consist of large samples of healthy adults parallel in demographic features and multimodal imaging protocols (T1W, T2W, dMRI, rfMRI and tfMRI). The availability of these two large-scale datasets enables researchers to make comparisons of the brain connectomics between people from different ethnic and cultural backgrounds.

In the current analyses, we were able to identify not only distinctions but also overlaps between these two groups, both in structure and in function. In the brainnetome atlas, which was constructed with dMRI, we observed topographic differences along the midline of the cerebral cortex, such as in the orbitofrontal, anterior to middle, and several posterior regions of the cingulate cortex, the precuneus, the medial parietal cortex, and the left superior and middle temporal cortex. We also observed distinctions in the functional connectivity networks between the CHCP and HCP populations, including the surface area of the auditory and default mode network and topography of the dorsal attention, default mode and frontoparietal networks with and without the correction of the size of the cortical network parcellation (Fig. 3 and Extended Data Fig. 5). Interestingly, the primary areas, including the somatomotor and visual networks exhibited relatively high consistency across the CHCP and HCP populations, as evidenced by a similarity in their functional network and intra-network structural connections. These regional differences were consistent with those obtained in a previous study that reported significantly higher interindividual variability in the resting-state functional connectivity in the heteromodal association cortex than in the unimodal cortices27. Such differences across populations could be due to stable factors related to individual features28, such as the acquired development of individual behavior and the influence of macro-environmental factors on brain function, as was observed in the current study.

Our comparison between the CHCP and HCP also revealed notable differences in functional brain activity during complex cognitive processes. Significant separation of the activation distribution was found in the language task (story versus math) between the CHCP and HCP, but a highly transcultural overlap of the activation distributions was found in the motor task. Further analysis of the effect size of tfMRI revealed a consistent tendency in contrasts of interest, that is, the most substantial difference between the CHCP and HCP in task-evoked activity was shown in language processing for both the left and right hemispheres (a Cohen’s d value > 0.5 in most activated brain areas and a global mean absolute value > 0.3 for both hemispheres, see Fig. 4c and Extended Data Fig. 2b), whereas the differences were the lowest in the motor tasks (a global mean absolute value of Cohen’s d value of approximately 0.1). Together with the results of brain structural and functional connectivity in the present work, our current findings show that heterogeneous brain activity remains at a minimal level across different cultural groups for sensory-motor-related brain networks but at a greater level for association-related brain networks (both functional and structural). These results are in accordance with the recent gradient theory of human macroscale cortical organization, which proposes that brain areas at different ends of the sensorimotor-to-transmodal axis (such as the default mode network and frontoparietal network) subserve the hierarchical information processing functions of the human brain in different ways29,30 and suggests that population-level macroscale influences on human cortical functions might follow similar gradients.

Language is closely related to culture and is a highly complex faculty. Even simple language behaviors, such as the comprehension of a single word through reading or listening, engage various mechanisms for visual and auditory information processing and an association of lexical items with semantic representation, and these tasks recruit widely distributed cortical areas31. Mandarin Chinese and English are the most widely spoken languages in the world, but they differ in several characteristics, such as their appearance in writing and the way they sound. Unlike English, which is a nontonal language, Mandarin Chinese uses lexical tones to convey lexical meanings through different pitch patterns. Additionally, the logographic writing system of Mandarin Chinese is distinct from the alphabetic writing system of English. Previous studies have revealed that neural activity in certain brain regions, such as the left middle frontal gyrus, is critical for fluent reading in Chinese speakers but not in English speakers32, and the cortical dynamics among classic language-related brain regions are significantly different between English and Mandarin Chinese native speakers as they process intelligible speech33. Cross-language studies have also found that speakers of different languages show critical variations in the conception of time34 and arithmetic processing35, which suggests that linguistic knowledge exerts a striking influence over scaffolding thoughts in abstract domains.

Notably, our analysis revealed remarkable differences in brain activation during the language task (story versus math) between the CHCP and HCP, and it might reflect the associations with the different neural activities underlying language processing between Chinese and English, although the between-scanner confounding factors need an explicit harmonization. Nevertheless, such a difference could also be due to the difference in mathematical processing between the two groups. In the HCP protocols, the language task was designed to specifically reveal the cortical regions typically associated with various components of language processing7, and mathematic processing such as the arithmetic task was used here as a control condition because it presents verbal and sentence-like stimuli that could be a match to the story stimuli on low-level features (for example, auditory and phonological inputs)36. However, such a contrast between story and math cannot fully rule out the possible difference in nonlinguistic math processing, which is true for both the within-group and between-group analysis. Future research that comprehensively integrates behavioral data with neuroimaging features will be intriguing to further investigate the relations between individuals’ performance and their neural correlates in different cognitive tasks across cultures, especially for language and math processing. By collecting individual-level brain activity data from two populations during language processing as well as during essential cognition, social cognition, memory, emotion, and decision-making processing, the CHCP and HCP datasets create a unique opportunity for scientists and linguists to uncover language-specific associations with cognitive and social processing in the human brain.

Nature and nurture in the human brain

Numerous studies have explored the influences of nature on the human brain; for example, previous research on twins investigated the heritability of brain anatomy and indicated that genetic factors substantially contribute to individual variation in brain morphology37,38, the regional surface area, and the regional thickness39 and that such heritability differs across brain regions40,41,42. The geometry of the human cortical surface is also highly predictive of an individual’s genetic ancestry43. Regarding the genetic basis of human brain networks, converging evidence indicates that anatomical connectivity is under a stronger genetic influence than functional connectivity and suggests heterogeneous levels of genetic control over different brain regions and connections44. In parallel, environmental influences, such as socioeconomic status, have also been found to be associated with the development of certain brain structures and functional networks2. These factors all lead back to the long-standing question of how nature and nurture shape different human brain regions and connections in different ways. While the diversity of genetic factors has been well addressed in the HCP dataset by including twins (both monozygotic and dizygotic) and people of a variety of races (Caucasian, African American, Asian and so on)7, the questions of nature versus nurture with respect to the human brain can be further explored through studies that take into account both genetic (for example, race and twin status) and environmental (for example, culture and socioeconomic status) factors with extended datasets that contain brain, behavioral and genetic data from a different race or cultural environment. Since the genetic–cultural interactions were proposed in the culture-behavior-brain loop of human development45, the genetic difference between East Asian and Caucasian populations is also worth considering in future research as another factor that influences the comparison between the CHCP and HCP. Despite the relatively small sample size for genetic research, genetic data from the CHCP could be further integrated with other neuroimaging genetics cohorts, and contribute to addressing the role of genetics in brain–behavior associations across different populations.

Growing diversity in brain imaging data collections

Recently, several scientific studies have collected large-scale brain imaging datasets from an expanded population by taking into consideration a new set of important factors in understanding the human brain and behavior. For example, studies that include individuals with different age ranges beyond young adults have examined children as part of the Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development study46, and adults aged 36 to 100 years as part of a lifespan HCP project in the United States47, as well as school-age children in China48 and longitudinal brain–mind cohorts such as the Chinese Color Nest Project49, which aimed to address questions on the development, maturation and aging of the human brain50 and their associations with various factors48,51,52. The UK Biobank, a large-scale prospective dataset, contains multimodal images of the brain, heart, body and so on, and a wide range of phenotypic and genotypic features with longitudinal follow-up for health-related outcomes in 40- to 69-year-old British subjects, which provides opportunities for the discovery of potential imaging markers to detect brain disease at an early stage51,53,54. The latest research has combined global cross-dataset large-sample brain images (structural and resting-state functional) from the Chinese Imaging Genetics (CHIMGEN) cohort55 with the European longitudinal IMAGEN cohort56 and observed important influences on the human brain and behavior from macroscopic factors such as geographical location and urbanicity57. Recently, brain-wide association studies have called for larger sizes of cross-sectional samples to capture reproducible brain–behavioral phenotype associations58. Although the current CHCP sample is relatively limited from this perspective, by taking the current CHCP dataset as a commencement, and with the imminent launch of the China Brain Project, which will focus extensive research efforts on the neural mechanisms that underlie cognition and brain disease59, large-scale brain imaging data from the Chinese population60 along with a rich range of measures of biological, mental and lifestyle features will continue to accumulate. In fact, many of the current studies on brain-wide cultural differences, especially for certain cognitive or social processing4,5, were based on only dozens of subjects from each culture due to the lack of large public datasets from alternative culture/ethnic groups rather than Western populations. Together with other datasets (for example, the recent ‘I See Your Brain’ data release60), the CHCP dataset can provide larger samples in an alternative cultural/ethnic group from East Asia, researchers will be able to reveal brain–behavior association findings that are more reproducible and generalizable61, and the CHCP could serve as an important foothold in this effort. Overall, the growing diversity of large-sample brain imaging data will enable a comprehensive understanding of the human brain and mind as well as the diseases that affect them.

Extensive research in cultural psychology has indicated that the difference between Western and East Asian cultures is complex and multilevel. For example, the thinking style is commonly considered holistic in Eastern cultures and analytic in Western cultures62. Another aspect of cultural difference is the concept of individuality and self-in-relation-with-others: whether a person’s self is construed as socially embedded or as an entity with dispositional attributes that are detached from the context63. Cultural studies have confirmed that people from East Asia tend to be collectivistic and interdependent, whereas people in Western culture tend to be individualistic and independent64. It is interesting that divergence in this fundamental concept has a specific consequence on people’s cognition, motivation and emotion4,64. In addition to self-concept, a more comprehensive tight-loose theory has been proposed in the last ten years as a new aspect of culture based on a study across 33 nations65. The researchers illustrated that the differences among cultures, especially between Eastern and Western cultures, could be explained by the tightness (having strong social norms and a low tolerance of deviant behavior) and looseness (having weak social norms and a high tolerance of deviant behavior), and such tightness–looseness is also manifested in and has an impact on our everyday situation and the psychological, distal ecological, historical, social and institutional contexts. Therefore, many aspects of culture may have corresponding impacts on and contributions to the difference between the results of the CHCP and HCP, and future research should take into account multiple levels in the cultural domain and systematically investigate the neural correlations of such differences.

In addition to culture, several other variables should also be considered when comparing the CHCP and HCP datasets. Technically, although the data acquisition and experimental paradigms have been carefully designed to be comparable between the CHCP and HCP protocols, and the brain images were all generated from 3 T Siemens MR scanners, the HCP used a custom-made Skyra 3 T scanner, whereas the CHCP used a Prisma 3 T scanner by following the harmonized acquisition parameters recommended by the HCP (https://www.humanconnectome.org/hcp-protocols-ya-3t-imaging, Appendix I: HCP Sessions Protocols; see also https://www.humanconnectome.org/hcp-protocols-ccf-template). Notably, overall imaging differences may be inevitable between different scanners and sites. Importantly, a further study involving traveling participants will be critical to verify and harmonize the technical difference between the CHCP and HCP scanner/site66,67,68. In addition to the scanner/site differences, the standard normalization space should also be considered when comparing the CHCP and HCP datasets by using certain volumetric and surface brain templates, wherein the between-individual bias caused by registration accuracy cannot be fully excluded. Moreover, despite differences in the cultural environment, participants in the CHCP and HCP could also differ in other macro-environmental factors that can affect the human brain and health, such as urbanization57, vegetation coverage in living areas69, population density70 and educational resources71. It is intriguing for future research to take into account these factors during the comprehensive comparison between the CHCP and HCP by utilizing quantitative measurements of individuals’ environments from public data such as the national survey databases (https://data.stats.gov.cn/english/) and global satellite remote-sense mapping72 as well as from cross-geographic brain image datasets55.

Future plans for the CHCP

Moving forward, the CHCP will focus on the following: (1) initiating a traveler study (within-subject calibration study) by scanning a number of participants using identical settings across different scanning sites/scanner types to provide additional details on scanner/site differences; (2) obtaining cortical maps by using multimodal CHCP neuroimaging data based on advanced methods developed by the HCP, such as multimodal parcellation10, to deepen our understanding of the structural and functional organization of the human brain; (3) performing genomics-related analyses based on blood samples drawn from the CHCP cohort to reveal the genetic underpinnings of brain differences across the CHCP and HCP populations; (4) expanding the sample size of the CHCP dataset by collaborating with other imaging sites in China; (5) acquiring data from participants with a broader age range, as well as 7 T MRI and magnetoencephalography data from both resting-state and task conditions. (6) CHCP endeavors to make the imaging and related nonimaging data available to the scientific community. To meet the standard of transparency and usability set by HCP and other high-impact releases of open datasets, the CHCP database will be continuously updated and maintained. By resolving this discrepancy, the CHCP will fulfill its goal to facilitate global data sharing and contribute to scientific research.

Methods

Experimental procedure

The participants came to the laboratory on two separate days and underwent a total of four MRI scanning sessions (two sessions per day). To be consistent with the HCP procedure and guarantee the data quality for each imaging modality15, the whole sessions included a set of structural scans (13 min in total), two dMRI imaging scans with anterior-to-posterior (AP) and posterior-to-anterior (PA) phase-encoding directions (13 min in total) and two 45-min fMRI scans (each scan included 15 min of rfMRI followed by 30 min of tfMRI and was divided into AP and PA phase-encoding directional scans). Because 30 min of data were required to achieve a stable correlation matrix for brain parcellation73, a total scanning time of 30 min was selected for rfMRI in the CHCP. The code for the seven tfMRI paradigms was obtained from the official HCP website, and the stimuli and instructions for the experiments were translated into Mandarin Chinese. Stimuli were presented by a Windows-based Dell computer using code based on Eprime Toolbox v.2.0.8.90. To ensure compatible content in the story condition for language processing, all of the stories used in this task were translated and back-translated independently by two bilingual speakers until this process converged into a final version, which was then transformed into audio recordings. Each subject was scanned for two sessions (one structural/diffusion session and one fMRI session) each day, and the order of sessions was counterbalanced across subjects. After completing the full sessions of brain scanning on day two, they were led to another room for behavioral testing, which took approximately one hour. The participants who agreed to provide blood samples for DNA extraction and further genetic analysis gave separate written informed consent. They were contacted within one month after they finished the previous procedure and came to the laboratory for blood sample collection. For more details on the experimental procedure, see Supplementary Methods.

Behavioral test protocols

Behavioral tests in the CHCP were generated based on the original HCP behavioral tests11, which were abbreviated to be feasible within one hour of testing but aimed to cover the essential domains of the behavioral assessments in the HCP behavioral tests (including both National Institutes of Health (NIH) Toolbox and non-NIH Toolbox measures) to the greatest extent possible. We made efforts to keep every domain/subdomain in the HCP from both NIH and non-NIH Toolbox measures11 in the CHCP behavioral test. For example, all four domains (cognition, emotion, motor and sensory) of the NIH Toolbox measures were included, while the measures that served similar purposes were left out, such as locomotion (4-m walk test) in the motor domain, which was selected, while endurance (2-min walk test) was removed. Although the behavioral tests in the CHCP left out some test batteries/questionnaires, the contents and items in each remaining questionnaire were intact, so that the behavioral data from each questionnaire were valid and comparable with those of the HCP. Details can be found in Supplementary Tables 5 and 6.

MRI preprocessing

All CHCP and HCP brain images from each MRI modality were preprocessed by using the ‘minimal preprocessing pipelines’74 by using the original codes published on Github (https://github.com/Washington-University/HCPpipelines/releases) to perform the volumetric and cortical surface preprocessing. Next, further modality-specific processing steps were conducted based upon these preprocessed data. A summary table of the details on data processing for the CHCP and HCP is included in the supplementary materials (Supplementary Table 7). For a detailed description of the data processing of each MRI modality, see the following sections.

To summarize, the overall processing for both the CHCP and HCP data consisted of preprocessing (for example, artifact removal, registration, alignment and so on) and additional modality-specific processing (for example, global signal regression, bandpass filtering, smoothing and so on). Data analysis and visualization were performed using customized code in MATLAB or Python and tools from R-studio packages. All preprocessing and modality-specific processing steps for both the CHCP and HCP data were identical. For better support and compatibility with our local computational platform, we adopted an updated version of the HCP pipeline (https://github.com/Washington-University/HCPpipelines/releases/tag/v4.0.0) on the preprocessing of the CHCP data. Together, the overall processing and analyses applied to the CHCP and HCP followed the same steps under the broader HCP pipeline framework.

Structural MRI preprocessing

Since the scanning protocol of the CHCP dataset is almost the same as that of the HCP dataset, the preprocessing of the CHCP dataset employed ‘minimal preprocessing pipelines’ by using the HCP code published on Github (https://github.com/Washington-University/HCPpipelines/releases)74. For the structural MRI data, the preprocessing involved three key pipelines: the first was carried out entirely on the volume data (PreFreeSurfer), the second involved reconstructing the cortical surface (FreeSurfer) and the final converted the proprietary FreeSurfer format to the NIFTI and GIFTI formats (PostFreeSurfer). For more details see Supplementary Methods.

dMRI preprocessing

dMRI is highly sensitive to head motion, which leads to signal dropout, which affects downstream model-based measurements75. To address this problem, Functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging of Brain (FMRIB) Software Library eddy (FSL eddy), a comprehensive and nonparametric approach for movement and distortion correction, was used to extract a rich set of quality control metrics. Subjects were excluded for poor dMRI data if the maximum relative voxel displacement exceeded 1.5 mm or if the average voxel displacement exceeded 2 mm. For the dMRI data, the diffusion images were processed by the HCP diffusion preprocessing pipeline using the FMRIB diffusion toolbox (FSL v.5.0; http://www.fmrib.ox.ac.uk/fsl). For more details on dMRI preprocessing, see Supplementary Methods.

The eigenvector (v1) associated with the largest eigenvalue was used as an indicator of fiber orientation. To generate whole-brain maps of v1, diffusion tensors were fitted in each subject using the b = 1,000 s mm−2 data and then averaged across the CHCP subset and the HCP subsets (140 demographically matched participants in each subset). A color-coded orientation map was created by assigning red, green and blue channels to the x (right–left), y (anterior–posterior) and z (superior–inferior) components of v1 (refs. 76,77), respectively. The eigenvector maps of the CHCP and HCP subsets are shown in Fig. 1a.

Brain parcellation based on dMRI

By leveraging the preprocessed high-quality brain imaging data from the CHCP and HCP, we constructed structural connectivity-based parcellations at the individual level for 140 demographically matched participants from each dataset (CHCP: 62 females, aged 20–31 years, mean ± s.d.: 23.8 ± 2.5 years; HCP: 60 females, aged 22–27 years, mean ± s.d.: 24.1 ± 1.5 years; and there were no significant age differences between the two subsets, P > 0.1) with the technique proposed by Han and colleagues22. With a previous group atlas as a reference21, we parcellated the brain of each individual into 210 cortical regions using dMRI data and performed a volume-to-surface mapping registration to realign the individualized cortical parcellation to the surface template. A group-level vertex-wise probabilistic map was then calculated for each region that represents the overlap of this region across individuals’ parcellations for each dataset. Then, for 3D visualization, the maximum probability maps of the CHCP and HCP brainnetome atlases were constructed by assigning each vertex to the region with maximum probability where it was parcellated into according to the above vertex-wise probabilistic maps21,78.

Estimating the geometric difference across brainnetome atlases

For each cortical region, the similarity in region topography was calculated as the Dice coefficient between the CHCP and HCP brainnetome atlases. Since a larger region tends to represent a higher Dice coefficient, the primary analysis for cross-subset similarity in topography was corrected by regressing out the size of the cortical region using an ordinary least-squares regression (that is, a general linear model, GLM). The cross-cultural similarity in topography was then inverted (by subtraction from one) and thus normalized to obtain the weighted 1-Dice coefficient, which is shown in Fig. 2b. The comparison analysis without correction by the size of cortical network parcellation is also provided in Supplementary Materials (Extended Data Fig. 6).

As the group-level structural connectivity-based parcellation of each dataset is probabilistic, here, an extension of the Dice coefficient (probabilistic Dice coefficient) was used, which is based on the absolute probability similarities between a pair of cortical regions across two atlases.

$${\mathrm{Probabilistic}}\;{\mathrm{Dice}}\;{\mathrm{coefficient}} = \frac{{2 \times \mathop {\sum }\nolimits_k \min (P_{{\mathrm{A}}_k},\;P_{{\mathrm{B}}_k})}}{{\mathop {\sum }\nolimits_i P_{{\mathrm{A}}_i} + \mathop {\sum }\nolimits_j P_{{\mathrm{B}}_j}}}$$

where \(P_{{\mathrm{A}}_i}\) denotes the probability value at vertex i of the cortical region in one parcellation, and \(\mathop {\sum}\nolimits_i {P_{{\mathrm{A}}_i}}\) represents the sum of the probability values in the cortical region. \(P_{{\mathrm{B}}_j}\) denotes the probability value at vertex j of the cortical region in the other parcellation, and \(\mathop {\sum}\nolimits_j {P_{{\mathrm{B}}_j}}\) represents the sum of the probability values in the cortical region. k denotes the vertex in the intersection of the cortical region between the two parcellations, and \({\mathrm{min}}(P_{{\mathrm{A}}_k},\;P_{{\mathrm{B}}_k})\) represents the minimal probability value between \(P_{{\mathrm{A}}_k}\) and \(P_{{\mathrm{B}}_k}\). Then, \(\mathop {\sum}\nolimits_k {{{{\mathrm{min}}}}} (P_{{\mathrm{A}}_k},P_{{\mathrm{B}}_k})\) indicates the sum of the minimal probability values across vertices in the intersection of the cortical region between the two parcellations.

The results indicate that the general patterns of difference between the CHCP and HCP parcellations based on the structural connectivity were largely consistent for those with and without parcel size correction. In principle, the cortical regions with a relatively larger parcel size tend to show larger overlapped areas across groups (greater Dice coefficient, smaller 1-Dice coefficient) in general. Here, the results without parcel size correction in larger brain regions (such as the parcel located at the precentral gyrus, middle/posterior cingulate cortex) might have overstated the topographic similarity (Dice coefficient) and underestimated the topographic difference (1-Dice coefficient) compared to those corrected with parcel size, thus showed reduced difference compared to results with parcel size correction.

To validate the reliability of the constructed brainnetome atlases based on 140 participants from the CHCP and 140 participants from the HCP data, further validation tests were conducted by using two extended subsets from the CHCP and HCP datasets. Similar inclusion criteria were applied, except that the participants only needed to have brain images with eligible quality in the tested MRI modality rather than in all MRI modalities. A total of 220 participants from the CHCP and 220 participants from the HCP (age range: 18–37 years, right-handed, including all 140 CHCP and 140 HCP participants from the formal atlas construction) were included and split into half-half: 110 ‘parcellation’ and 110 ‘validation’ for CHCP, and 110 ‘parcellation’ and 110 ‘validation’ for the HCP. Each pair of ‘parcellation’ and ‘validation’ subsets were matched for their age and sex. Then, the group-level brainnetome atlases were constructed separately for the ‘parcellation’ and ‘validation’ subsets within each dataset. A comparison of each pair of brainnetome atlases showed that 89.1% and 88.9% of the vertices were assigned to the same brain regions across the ‘parcellation’ and ‘validation’ samples for the CHCP and HCP datasets, respectively (Extended Data Fig. 7). This validated that a sample of 110 participants is sufficient to create a reliably reproducible brainnetome atlas in our current study.

Analysis of the structural connections of seven functional networks

Moreover, to characterize the group difference in the structural connections within and between seven canonical functional networks (see later sections for more details) for the CHCP and HCP data, we conducted a further probabilistic tractography analysis based on preprocessed dMRI data. The seed and target regions for calculating structural connectivity strength were defined by using the Schaefer 400 atlas, which parcellated the cortex into 400 areas but still preserved the network structure79. Specifically, each brain region from the Schaefer 400 atlas was remapped and assigned to one of seven canonical networks in which this network shared the maximum overlap with this brain region. The reassignment procedure was carried out separately for the CHCP and HCP seven-network atlases. Then, the assigned Schaefer 400 atlas was coregistered to each subject’s native diffusion space to define seed and target regions for further probabilistic tractography analysis and for the construction of structural connections. All seed and target cortical regions were constrained by the gray-white boundary and cortical gray matter ribbon, and the structural connectivity strength from the seed to each of the other 399 target regions was calculated by using probabilistic tractography. At each seed voxel, 1,000 samples were initiated, and the other tracking parameters were set as the default (2,000 steps maximum, a step-length of 0.5 mm and a curvature threshold of 0.02). To enhance the biological plausibility of the probabilistic tractography, the streamlines were terminated when they entered superficial gray matter, and discarded when they traversed cerebrospinal fluid. Overall, this fiber tracking procedure allowed us to construct a weighted 400 × 400 connectivity matrix for each subject based on the Schaefer 400 atlas. Next, we used a consistency-based thresholding method to mitigate the influence of false positives in the structural connectomes80. Then, each subject’s structural connectivity matrix was set as the thresholded at the 75th percentile. Finally, these structural connectivity matrices were used to investigate the inter- and intra-network structural connectivity differences between the CHCP and HCP, and the FDR correction was performed across the total number of inter- and intra-network modules with a corrected P < 0.001 (Fig. 2c).

fMRI data preprocessing

Standardized quality control was applied to the fMRI data. Subjects with large head motion were excluded (their maximum relative root mean square framewise displacement exceeded 3 mm or mean relative root mean square framewise displacement exceeded 0.2 mm during the rfMRI and tfMRI scans). The preprocessing of fMRI data (both rfMRI and tfMRI) involves two key pipelines; the first is performed on volume-based data (fMRIVolume). After the volume-based analysis, the fMRI volume data were mapped to cortical surfaces and subcortical gray matter and stored in Connectivity Informatics Technology Initiative (CIFTI) format (fMRISurface)7. See Supplementary Methods for details of the fMRIVolume and fMRISurface preprocesses.

Seven-network atlas construction

We used the cluster algorithm method to construct a population-level seven-network atlas based on the rfMRI data23. Specifically, we first defined the connectivity profile of each vertex in fs_LR_32k meshes as its functional connectivity with 1,483 regions of interest (ROI). The 1,483 ROI vertices were uniformly sampled in fs_LR_32k with fs_LR_900 mesh. For each subject, we computed the Pearson’s correlation between the spatial location (fs_LR_32k) and the 1,483 ROI vertices. Therefore, 59,412 cortical vertices were characterized as a sequence of correlation coefficients with the 1,483 ROI, and we further binarized the 59,412 × 1,483 matrix of correlations by retaining the top 10% of correlation coefficients. Finally, the subject correlation matrix was averaged across the demographically matched CHCP subset and HCP subset separately (140 subjects in each).

For both the CHCP and HCP group average correlation matrices, a mixture model was applied to estimate the cortical networks with similar connectivity profiles23,81. The clustering algorithm used in this study modeled the data with a von Mises-Fisher distribution and was operated by randomly assigning 59,412 vertices to different groups and iteratively reassigning the groups of vertices to maximize the agreement of the connectivity profiles among vertices of the same group (see Yeo et al.23 for details of the clustering process).

With the established CHCP and HCP seven-network atlases, we further explored the differences in each cortical network surface area size ratio, which was calculated as the size of the network divided by the total cortical surface area of the brain. Specifically, the surface area was calculated using the function wb_command -cifti-all-labels-to-rois and wb_command -cifti-weighted-stats -sum in Workbench software (https://www.humanconnectome.org/software/workbench-command). Then, a statistical analysis was performed based on a permutation test. Subjects in the combined CHCP and HCP subsets were shuffled and divided equally into two permuted groups, which made each group contain subjects from both the CHCP and HCP. Next, a pair of group-level brain atlases were constructed by reperforming the same clustering algorithm with the two permuted groups. The above process was repeated 1,000 times to establish a null distribution of the surface area size ratio difference, which was used to determine the significance of the actual surface area size ratio difference between the CHCP and HCP subsets. Furthermore, we investigated the topographic differences between the CHCP and HCP atlases based on the 1-Dice coefficient82. Specifically, the topographic differences between the CHCP and HCP brain atlases were represented by the 1-Dice coefficient with each network, and the 1-Dice coefficient was corrected by regressing out the size of the cortical network parcellation (Fig. 3c). The results without correction by the size of cortical network parcellation are also provided in the Supplementary Materials (Extended Data Fig. 5). Finally, the statistical analysis was performed using a permutation test with a similar procedure as that performed for the surface area size ratio.

In the functional connectivity parcellations, the results without parcel size correction showed that the topographic difference in the auditory network and the dorsal attention networks were exaggerated. These two networks were with greater 1-Dice coefficients compared to those with parcel size correction, whereas the topographic differences in the frontoparietal and default mode networks without parcel size correction were underestimated, with smaller 1-Dice coefficients relative to those with parcel correction. Notably, the surface area size ratio (seen in Fig. 3b) was quite small in the auditory and dorsal attention networks and was most significantly different between the CHCP and HCP in these two networks in opposite directions (auditory: CHCP < HCP, Pperm < 0.001; dorsal attention: CHCP > HCP, Pperm < 0.05; both FDR corrected). It is possible that topographic difference without taking into account each network’s parcel size results in greater variability in the auditory and dorsal attention network, while for the frontoparietal and default mode networks that have a relatively larger size (surface area), the topographic difference might be diminished due to their large parcel area if not corrected. Interestingly, parcel size correction seems to have less influence on the visual and somatomotor networks, which suggests that the parcel areas of these two networks were less variable between the CHCP and HCP groups than the other networks, which is also consistent with our findings in the current study that the cultural/ethnic difference between the CHCP and HCP groups were probably followed a sensorimotor-to-transmodal axis similar to that proposed by the functional gradient theory of human cortical organization29,30.

To validate the reliability of the seven-network group atlases based on the CHCP and HCP rfMRI data, subsets of 110 ‘parcellation’ and 110 ‘validation’ for the CHCP, and 110 ‘parcellation’ and 110 ‘validation’ for the HCP were established according to identical procedures described in the previous section for the dMRI brainnetome atlas. Then, the seven-network atlases were reconstructed separately for the ‘parcellation’ and ‘validation’ subsets within each dataset. A comparison of each pair of seven-network atlases showed that 95.2% and 95.0% of the vertices were assigned to the same networks across the ‘parcellation’ and ‘validation’ samples for the CHCP and HCP datasets, respectively (Extended Data Fig. 8). This validates that a sample of 110 participants is sufficient to create a reliably reproducible seven-network atlas in our current study.

tfMRI analysis

The participants’ response accuracy and reaction time were recorded in the task fMRI. The mean response accuracy across all tasks (except for the motor and gambling tasks, since the participants were not required to press response keys in the motor task and the win-loss ratio was manipulated in the gambling task by the experimental design) was 86.86% in the tfMRI of the CHCP subset (Extended Data Fig. 9). The high accuracy validates the engagement of the CHCP participants during the tasks.

The volume-based and surface-based tfMRI data outputs of the fMRIVolume and fMRISurface pipelines were used for further GLM analysis. For both the volume-based and surface-based analysis streams, the numbers of predictors were as follows: (1) Four predictors were included in the GLM for the working memory task (that is, 2-back face, 0-back face, 2-back place, and 0-back place). (2) Five predictors were included in the motor task (right hand, left hand, right foot, left foot and tongue). (3) Two predictors were included in the language processing task (story and math). (4) Two predictors were included in the social cognition task (social and random motion). (5) Two predictors were included in the relational processing task (relational processing and a control matching condition). (6) Two predictors were included in the emotion processing task (emotional faces and a control shape condition). (7) Two predictors were included in the gambling processing task (reward-heavy and loss-heavy blocks).

The subject-level and group-level analyses used fMRI Expert Analysis Tool (FEAT) to estimate the average effects across runs for each subject, and FMRIB’s local analysis of mixed-effects tools were then employed to estimate the average effects of interest for each group. FMRIB’s local analysis of mixed effects treated the subjects as random effects in the mixed-effects analyses. The volume-based group-level analyses were performed using voxelwise comparisons in Montreal Neurological Institute (MNI) space. The grayordinate-based analyses were conducted identically to the volume-based analyses except that cross-subject statistical comparisons were performed in standard grayordinate space. As in the individual analysis, CIFTI data were converted to the NIFTI-1 format to analyze the left and right brain hemispheres separately. The final NIFTI-1 matrices were converted to the GIFTI format for visualization using the Connectome Workbench platform. For grayordinate-based analyses, the group maps are shown with a minimum threshold of z = ±5.01 (P < 0.05 voxel-level, Bonferroni corrected). Furthermore, to quantify the effect size of the population difference in these tfMRI results, we calculated the voxelwise Cohen’s d value83 of all seven tfMRI contrasts between the CHCP and HCP subsets, as shown in Extended Data Fig. 2b. The mean absolute values of the effect size were also calculated in the left and right hemispheres for the target tfMRI contrast across seven tasks (see Barch et al. 11 for details of the GLM analysis).

Extended data

Extended Data Fig. 1 Reliability in tfMRI activation maps measured by ICC for both the CHCP and HCP data.

The image-wise intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) values in seven tasks were calculated across the two different phase-encoding runs for both the CHCP (left column) and HCP (right column) data. Together with the tfMRI activation maps shown in Fig. 4a, these results suggested that the brain areas which were significantly activated in tfMRI tend to show higher ICC values than brain areas which were not activated.

Extended Data Fig. 2 Maps of two-sample t-test and effect size on tfMRI activation for CHCP vs. HCP.

Sample sizes were 140 in both the CHCP and HCP groups. a, Group-level difference of the CHCP vs. HCP in the brain activations for the seven tasks. The white contours represent the area with a threshold (z ≤ −5.13 and z ≥ + 5.13, P < 0.05 vertex-level, two-sided, Bonferroni corrected) across all 91,282 grayordinates. b, The between-group effect size of the CHCP vs. HCP in the seven tasks. The effect size was measured by the index of Cohen’s d, and the white contours represent the areas with Cohen’s d values larger than 0.5 or smaller than −0.5.

Extended Data Fig. 3 Distribution of the activation intensity in tasks from the CHCP and HCP data.

Sample sizes were 140 in both the CHCP and HCP groups. a, Gambling task. No significant differences were found in distributions between the CHCP and HCP datasets with a threshold of two-sided P < 0.001 FDR corrected. b, Relational task. No significant differences were found. c, Social task. The difference in the distributions of the ventral attention network was marginally significant between groups (P = 0.003, two-sided, FDR corrected). d, Emotion task. The distributions of the visual and dorsal attention networks were significantly different between the CHCP and HCP (P < 0.001, two-sided, FDR corrected). e, Working memory task. The visual, dorsal attention, frontoparietal and somatomotor networks were significantly different between groups (P < 0.001, two-sided, FDR corrected). The results for the motor and language tasks are shown in Fig. 4b. The density refers to the probability of the subject number under a certain contrast value, and all of the statistics shown in this figure were based on the K-S test with FDR correction.

Extended Data Fig. 4 Volume-based activation maps in seven tasks from the CHCP and HCP data.

For volume-based group-level analyses, to estimate the average effects across runs for each subject, FEAT was first used, and then, FMRIB’s local analysis of mixed effects (FLAME) tools were employed to estimate the average effects of interest for each group. The group maps are displayed with a threshold of z =± 5 (P < 10−5 voxel-level, two-sided, uncorrected). The left and right columns show the results of the activation maps for the seven tasks from the CHCP and HCP datasets, respectively.

Extended Data Fig. 5 Topographic difference on the resting-state 7-network atlases between CHCP and HCP groups.

Sample sizes were 140 in both the CHCP and HCP groups. The topographic difference on 7-network atlases across CHCP and HCP were shown with parcel size correction (left) and without parcel size correction (right) in the left and right hemispheres (two-sided Pperm < 0.001 for each value of 1-Dice coefficient, permutation test between CHCP and HCP, FDR corrected).

Extended Data Fig. 6 Topographic difference on the dMRI brainnetome atlas across the CHCP and HCP data.

Sample sizes were 140 in both the CHCP and HCP groups. The distribution of variability (1-Dice coefficient) across brain regions between the CHCP and HCP brainnetome atlases were shown with parcel size correction (left) and without parcel size correction (right).

Extended Data Fig. 7 Test for reliability of the brainnetome atlas for the CHCP and HCP data.

The brainnetome atlases are highly consistent across the “Parcellation” sample (n = 110) and “Validation” sample (n = 110) for both groups. A total of 89.1% and 88.9% of the vertices were assigned to the same regions across the “Parcellation” and “Replication” brainnetome atlases for the CHCP and HCP subsets, respectively.

Extended Data Fig. 8 Test for reliability of the 7-network atlas for the CHCP and HCP data.

The 7-network estimates are highly consistent across the “Parcellation” sample (n = 110) and “Validation” sample (n = 110) for both groups. A total of 95.2% and 95.0% of the vertices were assigned to the same networks across the “Parcellation” and “Replication” 7-network atlases for the CHCP and HCP subsets, respectively.

Extended Data Fig. 9 Distributions of the accuracy measures from behavioral data during the task fMRI for the CHCP and HCP subsets.

The distributions of accuracy are shown in histograms. The mean response accuracy values were calculated for each subset (relational: CHCP: 77.63%, HCP: 77.49%; social: CHCP: 80.09%, HCP: 90.49%; emotion: CHCP: 95.82%, HCP: 97.52%; working memory: CHCP: 90.47%, HCP: 88.25%; language: CHCP: 90.31%, HCP: 89.04%).
